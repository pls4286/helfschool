▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — 75% stat · salt sources
Salt is something most of us barely think about. It arrives in our food invisibly — in bread, cereals, soups, sauces, ready meals — long before we ever reach for the shaker.
— pause —
The relationship between salt and blood pressure is one of the most studied, most replicated, and most clinically important findings in nutritional science. The evidence is not disputed. Eating less salt lowers blood pressure. Lower blood pressure means lower risk of heart attack, stroke, and premature death.
— pause —
Here's the most important and least understood fact: 75% of the salt we eat is already in our food. Before we cook. Before we add anything. The shaker accounts for only about 25%. The biggest gains come not from putting the shaker away — but from choosing less processed food more often.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Blood pressure stat card
Most people in the UK consume around 8–9 grams of salt per day — well above the NICE and NHS recommended maximum of 6 grams. That's roughly one teaspoon — including all the salt already in food, not just what's added at the table.
— pause —
When there is too much sodium in the blood, the body retains water to dilute it — increasing the volume of blood in circulation, and with it, the pressure on artery walls. That's the mechanism. It's direct, well understood, and consistent across populations.
— pause —
The DASH-Sodium trial found that reducing sodium intake produced reductions in systolic blood pressure of 8–14 mmHg in people with hypertension. That's comparable to the effect of a single antihypertensive medication. From diet alone.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — Label reading · potassium foods
In UK practice, when elevated blood pressure is identified, salt reduction is first-line lifestyle advice — recommended by NICE guideline NG136 for everyone with hypertension, alongside medication where indicated.
— pause —
On food labels — high salt is defined as more than 1.5g per 100g. Some labels show sodium rather than salt: multiply the sodium figure by 2.5 to get the salt equivalent.
— pause —
And potassium is the other side of the equation. Foods rich in potassium — bananas, sweet potatoes, spinach, beans, dairy — directly counteract sodium's effect on blood pressure. Increasing potassium is as important as reducing sodium.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–7 — Research evidence
The INTERSALT study — comparing sodium intake and blood pressure across 52 populations worldwide — found that populations with the lowest sodium intakes had significantly lower blood pressure, and far less of the age-related rise in blood pressure that most people in the West experience as normal.
— pause —
The Cochrane review confirmed that modest, sustained salt reduction over four or more weeks produces consistent, significant blood pressure reductions in both people with hypertension and those with normal blood pressure. The lower the intake achieved, the lower the blood pressure.
— pause —
On potassium — a WHO-commissioned BMJ meta-analysis found higher potassium intake was associated with a 24% lower risk of stroke. No adverse effect on blood lipids or kidney function.
— pause —
The UK salt reduction programme between 2003 and 2011 reduced average intake by around 15%. During that period, stroke mortality fell by 42% and ischaemic heart disease mortality by 40%. Population-level change is possible — and the evidence is consistent with it making a real difference.
