Segment 1 — What is it?
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 2: 75% stat card · salt sources donut chart

"Salt is something most of us barely think about. It arrives in our food invisibly — in bread, breakfast cereals, soups, sauces, ready meals — long before we ever reach for the shaker."

"The relationship between salt and blood pressure is one of the most studied, most replicated, and most clinically important findings in nutritional science. The evidence is not disputed. Eating less salt lowers blood pressure. Lower blood pressure means lower risk of heart attack, stroke, and premature death." PAUSE

"Here's the most important and least understood fact about salt: 75% of the salt we eat is already in our food. Before we cook. Before we add anything. The shaker on the table accounts for only about 25%. The biggest gains come not from putting the shaker away — but from choosing less processed food more often."

Segment 2 — Why does it matter?
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 3: Blood pressure stat card · 8–14 mmHg reduction · DASH-Sodium

"Most people in the UK consume around 8–9 grams of salt per day — well above the NICE and NHS recommended maximum of 6 grams. That's roughly one teaspoon — including all the salt already present in food, not just what's added at the table."

"When there is too much sodium in the blood, the body retains water to dilute it — increasing the volume of blood in circulation, and with it, the pressure on artery walls. That's the mechanism. It's direct, it's well understood, and it's consistent across populations." PAUSE

"The DASH-Sodium trial found that reducing sodium intake produced reductions in systolic blood pressure of 8–14 mmHg in people with hypertension. That's comparable to the effect of a single antihypertensive medication. From diet alone."

Segment 3 — What your doctor will do
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 4: Practical tips cards · label reading guide · potassium foods

"When a GP sees raised blood pressure, salt reduction is first-line lifestyle advice — recommended by NICE guideline NG136 for everyone with hypertension, alongside medication where needed."

"Practical advice: reduce processed and packaged food — the main source of hidden salt. Check labels. High salt means more than 1.5g per 100g — choose less often. Use herbs, spices, lemon, and pepper as flavour alternatives." PAUSE

"And potassium is the other side of the equation. Foods rich in potassium — bananas, sweet potatoes, spinach, beans, dairy — counteract sodium's effect on blood pressure. Increasing potassium is as important as reducing sodium."

Segment 4 — What the research shows
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 5: Research cards · INTERSALT · DASH-Sodium · Cochrane · UK programme · Potassium BMJ

"The INTERSALT study — comparing sodium intake and blood pressure across 52 populations worldwide — found that populations with the lowest sodium intakes had significantly lower blood pressure. And crucially — far less of the age-related rise in blood pressure that we've come to treat as normal."

"The Cochrane systematic review by He, Li, and MacGregor confirmed that modest, sustained salt reduction over four or more weeks produces consistent, significant blood pressure reductions in both hypertensive and normotensive individuals. The lower the salt intake achieved, the lower the blood pressure — within the range of 3 to 12 grams per day." PAUSE

"And on potassium — a WHO-commissioned BMJ meta-analysis found higher potassium intake was associated with a 24% lower risk of stroke. No adverse effect on blood lipids or kidney function. The evidence is clear."

"The UK salt reduction programme between 2003 and 2011 reduced average intake by 15%. During that period, stroke mortality fell by 42% and ischaemic heart disease mortality by 40%. Population-level change is possible — and it works." PAUSE
