Six popular supplements · what trials show — 1 of 2
🌿
Plant sterols & stanols
2 g/day reduces LDL ~7–12% in meta-analysis. No trial has shown this translates into fewer cardiovascular events.
−7–12%
🐟
Fish oil · omega-3
Lowers triglycerides — not LDL. UK Biobank 2024: regular use linked to ~13% higher atrial fibrillation (AF) risk without prior CVD.
+AF 13%
🍚
Red yeast rice
Contains monacolin K — chemically identical to a prescription statin drug. Same risks, no dose control, no monitoring. EFSA 2025: no safe dose.
⚠️ Caution

Research · supplements vs statins head-to-head
Key Finding
−37.9%
LDL reduction
with a low-dose statin (5mg) at 28 days — vs placebo
SPORT — Cleveland Clinic
Laffin et al · JACC · 2023 · n=190
A low-dose statin (5mg) delivered a 37.9% LDL reduction in 28 days. Six popular supplements showed no significant LDL drop vs placebo. Garlic slightly increased LDL.
Key Finding
0%
meaningful LDL reduction
from any of 6 popular supplements vs placebo — SPORT trial
SPORT — supplement arm
Laffin et al · JACC · 2023
Head-to-head comparison is unusually clear — no supplement separated meaningfully from placebo on LDL. This includes plant sterols and red yeast rice at trial doses.
Research · plant sterols & fish oil
Key Finding
−7–12%
LDL reduction
from plant sterols at 2g/day — 124 randomised trials
Plant sterols meta-analysis
Ras et al · Br J Nutr · 2014 · 124 RCTs
Plant sterols at 2g/day produce a consistent 7–12% LDL reduction — a real effect. But no outcome trial has shown this translates into fewer heart attacks or strokes.
Key Finding
+13%
higher AF risk
with regular fish oil in people without prior CVD · n=415,000
Fish oil & atrial fibrillation
Chen et al · BMJ Medicine · 2024 · n=415,000
Regular fish oil users without prior CV disease had ~13% higher AF risk and ~5% higher stroke risk. Possible modest benefit in those with existing CV disease.
Research · red yeast rice — the hidden statin
Key Finding
No safe dose
of monacolin K as food supplement
EFSA concluded January 2025 — reactions at doses as low as 3mg/day
EFSA — red yeast rice safety opinion
European Food Safety Authority · January 2025
EFSA (2025) concluded there is no safe dose of monacolin K as a food supplement. Serious reactions — including muscle damage — reported at doses as low as 3mg/day.
Key Finding
Regulated
dose, interactions & monitoring
prescription statins carry safeguards red yeast rice does not
Why prescription matters
NICE NG238 · MHRA oversight
Prescription statins carry standardised dosing, interaction checks, and monitoring pathways. Red yeast rice delivers the same active compound without any of these regulatory safeguards.
Why statins work where most supplements don't — 1 of 2
🧪
Statins block the key enzyme
HMG-CoA reductase inhibition is the rate-limiting step in cholesterol synthesis — direct, potent, reproducible LDL reduction.
→ ~−40% LDL
🌿
Plant sterols block absorption
Compete with cholesterol in the gut — modest effect, ceiling at ~7–12% LDL drop, dependent on dietary cholesterol intake.
→ −7–12%
🐟
Fish oil lowers triglycerides
Reduces hepatic triglyceride output — different target from LDL. No consistent LDL effect; possible atrial fibrillation risk without prior CVD.
→ Trigs only
Why statins work where most supplements don't — 2 of 2
🍚
Red yeast rice IS a statin
Monacolin K is chemically identical to a prescription statin drug — same mechanism, same risks, but unregulated, variable dose, no monitoring.
→ Unregulated
🎯
Outcome trials exist for statins
170,000+ people across 26 trials: fewer heart attacks and strokes per 1 mmol/L LDL drop. Supplements lack this outcome evidence.
→ −22% events
📏
Numbers vs outcomes
Lowering a number is a surrogate. Preventing a heart attack is the actual goal. Only statins currently have both pieces of evidence.
→ Both
