Six popular supplements · what trials show
🌿
Plant sterols & stanols
2 g/day reduces LDL ~7–12% in meta-analysis. No trial has shown this translates into fewer cardiovascular events.
−7–12%

Fish oil · omega-3
Lowers triglycerides dose-dependently — not LDL. UK Biobank 2024: regular use linked to ~13% higher AF risk without prior CVD.

Monacolin K is chemically identical to lovastatin (Mevacor) — same risks, no dose control, no monitoring. EFSA 2025: no safe dose.

Research · supplements vs statins head-to-head
−37.9%
LDL · rosuvastatin 5 mg · 28 days
SPORT — Cleveland Clinic
Laffin et al · JACC · 2023 · n=190
Randomised, placebo-controlled head-to-head trial. Low-dose rosuvastatin (Crestor) delivered a substantial LDL reduction. Six popular supplements showed no significant LDL reduction vs placebo. Garlic actually increased LDL slightly.
0%
LDL reduction · 6 supplements · vs placebo
SPORT — supplement arm
Laffin et al · JACC · 2023
The evidence from controlled head-to-head comparison is unusually clean — and unusually unfavourable to the supplement aisle. This includes plant sterols, which produced a small non-significant shift, and red yeast rice, which also failed to separate from placebo at this dose.
Research · plant sterols & fish oil
−7–12%
LDL · plant sterols 2 g/day · 124 trials
Plant sterols meta-analysis
Ras et al · Br J Nutr · 2014 · 124 RCTs
Plant sterols at 2 g/day produce a consistent 7–12% LDL reduction — a real effect on the number. No outcome trial has yet shown this translates into fewer heart attacks or strokes. The number changes; the proven clinical benefit has not yet been demonstrated.
+13%
AF risk · regular fish oil · no prior CVD
Fish oil & atrial fibrillation
Chen et al · BMJ Medicine · 2024 · n=415,000
Regular fish oil users without prior CV disease had ~13% higher AF risk and ~5% higher stroke risk. Possible modest benefit in those with existing CV disease. The "safe default supplement" framing does not hold up in the largest observational datasets.
Research · red yeast rice — the hidden statin
No safe dose
monacolin K · EFSA · January 2025
EFSA — red yeast rice safety opinion
European Food Safety Authority · January 2025
EFSA concluded there is no safe dose of monacolin K in food supplements. Monacolin K is chemically identical to lovastatin (Mevacor). Serious adverse reactions — including rhabdomyolysis — have been reported at doses as low as 3mg/day. EU-wide restriction in progress.
Regulated
prescription statins · dose & monitoring
Why prescription matters
NICE NG238 · MHRA oversight
Prescription statins carry standardised dose, checked interactions, baseline and follow-up blood tests, and clear clinical pathways if symptoms develop. Red yeast rice delivers the same active compound without any of these safeguards.
Why statins work where most supplements don't
🧪
Statins block the key enzyme
HMG-CoA reductase inhibition is the rate-limiting step in cholesterol synthesis — direct, potent, reproducible LDL reduction.
→ ~−40% LDL
🌿
Plant sterols block absorption
Compete with cholesterol in the gut — modest effect, ceilings at ~7–12% LDL drop, dependent on dietary cholesterol.
→ −7–12%
🐟
Fish oil lowers triglycerides
Reduces hepatic triglyceride output — different target from LDL. No consistent LDL effect; possible AF risk.
→ Trigs only
🍚
Red yeast rice IS a statin
Monacolin K = lovastatin. Same mechanism, same risks — but unregulated, variable dose, no monitoring.
→ Unregulated
🎯
Outcome trials exist for statins
170,000+ people across 26 trials: fewer heart attacks and strokes per 1 mmol/L LDL drop. Supplements lack this outcome evidence.
→ −22% events
📏
Numbers vs outcomes
Lowering a number is a surrogate. Preventing a heart attack is the actual goal. Only statins currently have both pieces of evidence.
→ Both
