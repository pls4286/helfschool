Six popular supplements · what trials show
🌿
Plant sterols & stanols
2 g/day reduces LDL ~7–12% in meta-analysis. No randomised trial has yet shown this translates into fewer cardiovascular events.
−7–12%
🐟
Fish oil · omega-3
Lowers triglycerides dose-dependently but not LDL. UK Biobank 2024: regular use linked to ~13% higher AF risk in those without CVD.
+AF 13%
🍚
Red yeast rice
Monacolin K is chemically identical to lovastatin (a statin) — same risks, no dose control, no monitoring. EFSA 2025: no safe dose.
⚠️ Caution
🧄
Garlic extract
No consistent LDL benefit in trials. In SPORT, garlic slightly raised LDL vs placebo. No cardiovascular outcome data.
No effect
🍂
Cinnamon
No significant LDL effect in randomised trials. Popular for "heart health" claims without underlying cholesterol data.
No effect
🌻
Turmeric / curcumin
No significant LDL effect in RCTs. Claims often rest on anti-inflammatory pathway marketing, not cholesterol outcomes.
No effect

Research · supplements vs statins head-to-head
−37.9%
LDL · rosuvastatin 5 mg · 28 days
SPORT — Cleveland Clinic
Laffin et al · JACC · 2023 · n=190
Randomised, placebo-controlled, head-to-head trial. Low-dose rosuvastatin delivered a substantial LDL reduction. Six popular supplements — fish oil, cinnamon, garlic, turmeric, plant sterols, red yeast rice — showed no significant LDL reduction vs placebo.
0%
LDL reduction · 6 supplements · vs placebo
SPORT — supplement arm
Laffin et al · JACC · 2023
Garlic actually increased LDL slightly vs placebo. Plant sterols produced a small non-significant shift. The evidence from controlled head-to-head comparison is unusually clean — and unusually unfavourable to the supplement aisle.
Research · plant sterols & fish oil
−7–12%
LDL · plant sterols 2 g/day · 124 trials
Plant sterols meta-analysis
Ras et al · Br J Nutr · 2014 · 124 RCTs
Plant sterols at 2 g/day produce a consistent 7–12% LDL reduction — a real effect on the number. No outcome trial has yet shown this translates into fewer heart attacks or strokes.
+13%
AF risk · regular fish oil · no prior CVD
Fish oil & atrial fibrillation
Chen et al · BMJ Medicine · 2024 · UK Biobank n=415,000
Regular fish oil users without prior CV disease had ~13% higher AF risk and ~5% higher stroke risk. Possible modest benefit in those with existing CV disease. The "default healthy supplement" framing does not hold up in the largest datasets.
Research · red yeast rice — the hidden statin
No safe dose
monacolin K · EFSA · January 2025
EFSA — red yeast rice safety opinion
European Food Safety Authority · 2025
EFSA concluded there is no safe dose of monacolin K in food supplements — monacolin K is chemically identical to lovastatin. An EU-wide restriction is in progress. Same side-effect profile as prescription statins; no dose control or monitoring.
Regulated
prescription statins · dose & monitoring
Why prescription matters
NICE NG238 · MHRA oversight
Prescription statins carry standardised dose, checked interactions, baseline and follow-up bloods, and clear routes if symptoms appear. Muscle symptoms on a statin are clinically recognised as time-sensitive — 111 or urgent GP exists for this.
Why statins work where most supplements don't
🧪
Statins block the key enzyme
HMG-CoA reductase inhibition is the rate-limiting step in cholesterol synthesis — direct, potent, reproducible LDL reduction.
→ ~−40% LDL
🌿
Plant sterols block absorption
Compete with cholesterol in the gut — modest effect, ceilings out at ~7–12% LDL drop, dependent on dietary cholesterol.
→ −7–12%
🐟
Fish oil lowers triglycerides
Reduces hepatic triglyceride output — different target from LDL. Modest effect on triglycerides, no consistent LDL effect.
→ Trigs only
🍚
Red yeast rice IS a statin
Monacolin K = lovastatin. Works the same way — but unregulated, variable dose, no monitoring. Same risks, less safety net.
→ Statin unregulated
🎯
Outcome trials exist for statins
170,000+ people across 26 trials: fewer heart attacks and strokes per 1 mmol/L LDL drop. Supplements lack this outcome evidence.
→ −22% events
📏
Numbers vs outcomes
Lowering a number is a surrogate. Preventing a heart attack is the actual goal. Only statins currently have both pieces of the evidence.
→ Both
