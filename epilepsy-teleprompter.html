Segment 2 — What is epilepsy? (~45 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 2–3: Seizure types

Epilepsy is a neurological condition — a condition of the brain and nervous system — defined by a tendency to have recurrent, unprovoked seizures. A single seizure doesn't make a diagnosis. It's the pattern of recurrence, without a clear provoking cause, that defines it.

Seizures happen when there's a sudden burst of disordered electrical activity in the brain. And how a seizure looks depends entirely on where in the brain it starts.

Focal seizures begin in one specific area. Depending on which part of the brain is involved, a person might experience rhythmic jerking of one limb, a strong sense of déjà vu, a rising sensation in the stomach, or altered awareness. Focal seizures can stay localised — or they can spread and become generalised.

Generalised seizures involve both sides of the brain from the outset. The most well-known type is the tonic-clonic seizure — where the body stiffens, then goes into rhythmic jerking. But there are other types: absence seizures, which are brief blank staring episodes most common in children; myoclonic jerks, which are sudden muscle jerks often happening on waking; and atonic seizures, where the person suddenly drops due to complete loss of muscle tone — these carry a real risk of head injury.

And one important point — many people with epilepsy experience an aura before certain seizures. That's a warning symptom, which is itself a type of focal seizure. Auras are usually consistent for the same person, and give a brief window before the larger seizure begins.

Segment 3 — Assessment and diagnosis (~40 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 4–5: Assessment and diagnosis

How is epilepsy diagnosed? The single most important tool is the clinical history — a detailed account of what happened, ideally including from someone who witnessed it. NICE guidance is clear: anyone with a suspected first seizure should be referred promptly to a neurologist or epilepsy specialist. Diagnosis should not be made in primary care.

An EEG — which records the brain's electrical activity through sensors on the scalp — is a key investigation. But it can be normal between seizures even in confirmed epilepsy, so a normal result doesn't rule it out.

MRI brain imaging is recommended for most people newly diagnosed with epilepsy, to look for structural causes and help guide treatment.

And here's something that might surprise you — a UK specialist clinic study found that more than one in four patients referred with a diagnosis of epilepsy didn't actually have it. The most common errors were incomplete history-taking and misinterpretation of the EEG. The most common alternative diagnosis is NEAD — episodes that resemble seizures but are not caused by abnormal electrical activity in the brain. Getting the diagnosis right matters enormously — because the treatment choices that follow depend on it entirely.

Segment 4 — Medications (~40 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 6: Medications

The mainstay of epilepsy management is anti-seizure medication — or ASM. For focal epilepsy, two anti-seizure medication classes are typically first-line. For generalised epilepsy the choices are broader, though one historically very effective class is now significantly restricted due to pregnancy safety concerns.

I want to be clear about one particular anti-seizure medication class and pregnancy, because this is genuinely important. This class is teratogenic — capable of causing serious harm to a developing foetus, including structural birth defects and neurodevelopmental problems. The MHRA — the medicines regulator — operates a specific Pregnancy Prevention Programme restricting its use. This class must not be used in girls and women of childbearing potential unless there is no effective alternative and all the programme requirements are met.

This is not a theoretical risk. It's among the most important prescribing safety issues in UK neurology, and it's a conversation that needs to happen with a specialist.

Segment 5 — Drug-resistant epilepsy (~45 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 7–8: Drug-resistant epilepsy

For most people, medication controls seizures well. But more than 30% of people with epilepsy develop what's called drug-resistant epilepsy — where seizures continue despite adequate trials of two or more medications. And the research tells us something important: once two drugs have failed to control seizures — not because of side effects, but because they simply don't work — the probability of any further medication achieving seizure freedom falls to only about 11%. That is why drug resistance is taken seriously, and why surgery, nerve stimulation and dietary therapy exist as genuine alternatives rather than last resorts.

For people with drug-resistant epilepsy, there are other options. Epilepsy surgery — removing or disconnecting the brain tissue responsible for generating seizures — offers the best chance of long-term seizure freedom where a structural cause can be identified. Vagus nerve stimulation uses an implanted device to reduce seizure frequency. And ketogenic diet therapy — a medically supervised very high-fat, very low-carbohydrate diet — has evidence particularly in children with certain epilepsy syndromes. Newer approaches, including devices that detect seizure onset and deliver a targeted electrical response, are available at specialist centres.

None of these are simple interventions, and they all require specialist assessment. But they represent real options for people who haven't responded to medication.

And one more thing I want to mention — SUDEP, Sudden Unexpected Death in Epilepsy. The overall annual risk is approximately one in a thousand. But that risk is substantially higher in people with frequent uncontrolled tonic-clonic seizures, and substantially lower in those with good seizure control. NICE guidance now requires that all people with epilepsy are told about SUDEP — because understanding it supports the steps that reduce it, and the most powerful of those steps is achieving good seizure control.

Segment 6 — Research and close (~50 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 9: Research — treatment outcomes

The research picture on treatment outcomes is clear and important. A 30-year cohort study of nearly 1,800 patients found that around 50% became seizure-free on their first anti-seizure medication. A further 12% responded to the second, and around 4% to the third. Overall, nearly 64% achieved seizure freedom. But the message is stark — the first drug is by far the most likely to work. After two drug failures, the probability of the next one succeeding is only around 11%.

That's why getting the diagnosis right — and choosing the right first medication — is so clinically important.

▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 10: Research — misdiagnosis and SUDEP

On misdiagnosis — the Smith and Chadwick study from the Walton Centre found a rate of 26.1% among patients referred with epilepsy to a specialist clinic. That's a striking figure, and it confirms why specialist review is so important before committing someone to long-term anti-seizure medication.

And on SUDEP — the Thurman systematic review established an overall annual incidence of approximately 1.16 per 1,000 patients. The risk is not evenly distributed — it is substantially higher in those with frequent uncontrolled tonic-clonic seizures, and substantially lower in those who achieve good seizure control. Knowledge of SUDEP, and the steps that reduce it, is now a standard part of epilepsy care under NICE guidance.
