How Doctors Estimate Heart Attack Risk

Understanding QRISK3, cardiovascular risk assessment, and what your score really means

  • What is it?
  • Key Terms
  • Why does it matter?
  • What your doctor might do
  • What the research shows
1

What is it?

When your GP talks about your risk of a heart attack or stroke, they're not guessing. They are using a structured, evidence-based approach that combines several pieces of information about you — your age, blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking status, and other factors — to calculate a personalised estimate of your risk over the next ten years.

This is called cardiovascular risk assessment, and it is one of the most important tools in preventive medicine. Rather than waiting for something to go wrong, it allows your doctor to identify people who would benefit most from lifestyle changes or medication — before a heart attack or stroke happens.

In the UK, the main tool used for this is called QRISK3. It gives a result expressed as a percentage — for example, a score of 12% means you have roughly a 12 in 100 chance of having a heart attack or stroke in the next ten years. This isn't a certainty — it's a probability, based on how people with a similar profile to yours have fared in large population studies.

The score is used as a starting point for a conversation between you and your doctor, not as a verdict. Many factors are taken into account, and the decision about what to do next is always made together.

Key Terms

Cardiovascular disease (CVD)
An umbrella term for conditions affecting the heart and blood vessels, including heart attack, stroke, heart failure, and coronary heart disease. The leading cause of premature death in the UK.
Heart attack (myocardial infarction)
What happens when the blood supply to part of the heart muscle is suddenly blocked, usually by a blood clot forming on top of a fatty plaque inside a coronary artery. Without blood flow, heart muscle begins to die.
Stroke
What happens when blood supply to part of the brain is cut off — either by a clot blocking an artery, or by a blood vessel bursting. Like a heart attack, but affecting the brain.
QRISK3
The main cardiovascular risk calculator used in UK primary care. It combines over 20 pieces of information about a patient to estimate their 10-year risk of a heart attack or stroke, expressed as a percentage.
ECG (electrocardiogram)
A simple, painless test that records the electrical activity of the heart using stickers placed on the skin. It can show irregular rhythms, signs of previous heart damage, and other abnormalities.
Modifiable risk factor
A risk factor that can be changed or treated — such as smoking, high blood pressure, or high cholesterol — as opposed to fixed factors like age or family history that cannot be altered.
10-year cardiovascular risk
The estimated probability of having a heart attack or stroke within the next ten years, expressed as a percentage. A score of 10% means roughly 10 in every 100 people with that risk profile would be expected to have an event within a decade.

Why does it matter?

Most heart attacks and strokes don't happen out of nowhere. They are the end result of years of gradual, often silent damage to blood vessels — damage that can be identified and slowed down long before any symptoms appear.

A landmark international study — the INTERHEART study — involving over 30,000 people across 52 countries found that just nine modifiable risk factors account for more than 90% of the risk of a first heart attack in both men and women, across all ages, ethnic groups, and regions of the world.

Identifying risk early gives you and your doctor time to act — through lifestyle changes, medication, or both — before damage becomes irreversible.

Cardiovascular disease remains the leading cause of premature death in the UK. Early risk assessment is one of the most cost-effective things the NHS does.

What your doctor might do

The NHS Health Check

In England, everyone aged 40 to 74 is invited for a free NHS Health Check every five years. This is specifically designed to assess cardiovascular risk. It includes measurements of blood pressure, cholesterol, weight, and blood sugar, and uses this information to calculate your QRISK3 score.

If you are under 40 but have known risk factors — such as a strong family history of heart disease, high blood pressure, or diabetes — your GP may assess your risk earlier.

What goes into the calculation

QRISK3 takes into account a wide range of factors, some of which you may not expect:

Factor typeExamples
Fixed (cannot be changed)Age, sex, ethnicity, family history of heart disease before 60, postcode
Modifiable (can be improved)Smoking, blood pressure, cholesterol, body weight, diabetes, physical activity
Medical conditionsAtrial fibrillation (an irregular heart rhythm that significantly increases stroke risk), chronic kidney disease, rheumatoid arthritis, severe mental illness
MedicationsSteroids, antipsychotics

What the score means

QRISK3 scoreWhat it means
Below 10%Lower risk — lifestyle advice usually recommended
10% or aboveHigher risk — statin therapy should be offered alongside lifestyle advice
20% or aboveHigh risk — treatment is strongly recommended
For people aged 85 and over, NICE guidance recognises that age alone places them at increased risk, regardless of their calculated score.

Looking further ahead — lifetime risk

For younger people whose 10-year score may appear low but who have significant risk factors, doctors can also use a lifetime risk tool — QRISK3-lifetime — which gives a broader picture of long-term risk. This is particularly useful for motivating lifestyle changes in people who might otherwise feel reassured by a low 10-year score.

Beyond the calculator — how doctors refine risk

A risk calculator is a starting point, not the whole picture. Once a QRISK3 score has been calculated, your doctor may want to refine their assessment further — particularly if your score sits close to a treatment threshold, or if there are features of your history that suggest your risk may be higher than the number suggests.

Clinical judgement

Some important risk factors don't feed neatly into a calculator. An experienced GP will take these into account alongside the score:

Family history

A close relative who had a heart attack or stroke before the age of 60 is a significant warning sign, even if the calculator has partially captured it.

Obesity and body shape

Abdominal obesity — carrying excess weight around the middle — is independently associated with cardiovascular risk beyond what BMI alone captures.

Sedentary lifestyle

Prolonged physical inactivity is a strong independent risk factor, and one that is often underweighted in formal calculators.

Ethnicity

People of South Asian background have a significantly higher cardiovascular risk than the calculator may fully reflect, and doctors are advised to apply clinical judgement in this group.

Coronary artery calcium (CAC) scoring

For people whose calculated risk is borderline, a coronary artery calcium scan can be a valuable additional tool. This is a type of CT scan that detects calcium deposits in the walls of the coronary arteries (the blood vessels that supply the heart muscle itself with oxygen and nutrients). Calcium deposits are a direct marker of atherosclerosis (the gradual build-up of fatty deposits inside artery walls, causing them to narrow and harden over time).

A high calcium score in someone with a borderline QRISK3 result can tip the balance towards treatment. A score of zero is reassuring and may support a decision to hold off on medication. CAC scoring is not yet universally available on the NHS but is used selectively.

ECG (electrocardiogram)

If you have symptoms — such as chest pain, breathlessness on exertion, or palpitations — your GP may arrange an ECG. This can show signs of previous undetected heart damage, irregular heart rhythms such as atrial fibrillation, or other abnormalities that would significantly change your risk picture and clinical management.

Putting it all together

The best cardiovascular risk assessment combines the calculator score with the full clinical picture — your history, your family, your lifestyle, and your symptoms.

The score informs the conversation. The doctor and patient together make the decision.

What the research shows

The QRISK3 calculator — how it was built

QRISK3 was developed and validated in a prospective cohort study published in the BMJ (2017), using data from over 7 million people registered in UK general practices. It has been shown to predict cardiovascular risk more accurately than its predecessors, particularly in groups previously underrepresented — including those with mental illness, inflammatory conditions, and those taking medications that affect cardiovascular risk.

BMJ 2017 — Hippisley-Cox et al.

Current NICE guidance

NICE guideline NG238 (updated December 2023) recommends using QRISK3 to estimate 10-year cardiovascular risk in all adults aged 25 to 84 without established cardiovascular disease. Those with a score of 10% or more should be offered statin therapy alongside lifestyle advice. NICE also recommends that statin treatment should not be automatically ruled out for those below this threshold, particularly where lifetime risk is elevated or patient preference favours it.

NICE Guideline NG238

Five risk factors — half the global burden

A study published in the New England Journal of Medicine (2022), drawing on data from over 2 million people across 133 cohorts in 39 countries, identified five key modifiable risk factors that together account for approximately half of all cardiovascular disease worldwide — and showed that eliminating them at age 50 could add more than a decade of cardiovascular disease-free life.

A note on the limits of risk calculators. Risk calculators estimate probability at a population level and cannot predict what will happen to any individual with certainty. QRISK3 may underestimate risk in certain groups — including people with HIV, those already on blood pressure or cholesterol medication, or those with autoimmune conditions. Your doctor's clinical judgement, your own preferences, and the full picture of your health all play a part in deciding what to do next.
