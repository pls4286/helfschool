Alcohol and Health

What alcohol actually does to the body, what the modern evidence shows about risk, and how UK guidelines have changed — in plain English.

📖 10 min read 👨‍⚕️ Dr Paul · Retired NHS GP 📅 April 2026 🔬 Evidence-based
Health education — not medical advice. This article is for general information. Anything personally relevant is a conversation for you to have with your GP or healthcare professional.
Section 1

What alcohol is — and what it does in the body

Alcohol, in the drinking sense, is ethanol — a small, water-soluble molecule. Because it is so small, the body cannot keep it out: within minutes of a drink reaching the stomach, ethanol passes into the bloodstream and circulates to every organ, including the brain, the liver, the heart, the gut lining and the breast tissue. There is no part of the body that alcohol does not reach.

The liver handles most of the clearance. An enzyme called alcohol dehydrogenase converts ethanol into acetaldehyde (a highly reactive, DNA-damaging compound), which is then converted by a second enzyme into harmless acetate, and finally into carbon dioxide and water. Acetaldehyde is the key to understanding why alcohol causes cancer — it is classified by the World Health Organization's International Agency for Research on Cancer as a Group 1 human carcinogen (a substance with sufficient evidence to cause cancer in humans), alongside tobacco smoke and asbestos.

In the UK, alcohol is measured in units. One unit is defined as 10 millilitres — or 8 grams — of pure ethanol. The number of units in a drink depends on both its volume and its strength (ABV, alcohol by volume). A quick guide:

Quick formula: volume (ml) × ABV (%) ÷ 1000 = units. A 175ml medium glass of 13% wine is 2.3 units.

The UK Chief Medical Officers' guidelines, updated in January 2016, describe a weekly framework rather than a daily one — because most people in the UK do not drink every day. The headline number is 14 units a week, for both men and women, as the level below which health risks are kept to a low level. If someone does drink 14 units, the guidance describes spreading them across three or more days rather than in one or two heavy sessions, with several drink-free days in the week.

Three risk tiers are commonly used in UK clinical practice (NICE CG115) to describe weekly intake:

Low risk
Up to 14 units / week

Both sexes. Spread over 3+ days, with several drink-free days. Risk of harm is kept to a low level — not zero, but comparable to the risk accepted in everyday activities such as driving.

Increasing risk
15 – 50 units / week

Women 15–35, men 15–50. Regular drinking in this band steadily raises the risk of cancer, high blood pressure, liver disease, mental health problems, and injury.

Higher risk
Above 35 (W) / 50 (M)

Women above 35 units/week, men above 50. Drinkers in this group are likely to already be experiencing health harm, even if it is not yet visible. Many will meet criteria for alcohol dependence.

Two further points matter. First, pregnancy: the CMOs describe no safe level of alcohol in pregnancy — the safest approach is not to drink at all. Second, single-occasion (binge) drinking: even if weekly intake is within guidelines, drinking a week's worth in one evening substantially raises short-term risks of injury, accidents, alcohol poisoning, and arrhythmia (an abnormal heart rhythm).

📖 Key Terms
Acetaldehyde
A highly reactive compound produced when the liver breaks down alcohol. It damages DNA and is the main reason alcohol causes cancer.
ABV (alcohol by volume)
The percentage of a drink that is pure alcohol. A 12% ABV wine is 12% alcohol by volume.
AUDIT-C
A three-question screening tool (frequency, typical amount, binge episodes) used in UK primary care to identify drinking at hazardous or harmful levels.
Binge drinking
Drinking enough on one occasion to reach intoxication — in UK guidelines, more than 6 units for women or 8 units for men in one session.
Carcinogen (Group 1)
A substance with sufficient evidence of causing cancer in humans, according to the International Agency for Research on Cancer. Alcohol was classified Group 1 in 1988.
Cirrhosis
Advanced, permanent scarring of the liver. The final stage of alcohol-related liver disease — not reversible, but further damage can be halted by stopping drinking.
Dependence
A cluster of features — strong urges to drink, loss of control, withdrawal symptoms, tolerance, drinking taking priority over other activities — that indicate the body and mind have become reliant on alcohol.
Hazardous drinking
A pattern of drinking that is likely to cause harm in the future, even if it has not caused harm yet. Typically corresponds to regular drinking above 14 units/week.
Harmful drinking
Drinking that is already causing clear harm — physical, mental, or social. Often described as "high-risk" drinking in NICE guidance.
Mendelian randomisation
A research method that uses genetic differences between people — which affect how much alcohol they drink — to test whether alcohol causes disease rather than merely being associated with it.
PAR (population-attributable risk)
The share of cases of a disease in a population that is linked to a particular cause — e.g. the proportion of breast cancers linked to alcohol.
Steatosis (fatty liver)
The first stage of alcohol-related liver disease, in which fat builds up inside liver cells. Common in regular heavy drinkers, and fully reversible if drinking stops.
TMREL
Theoretical Minimum Risk Exposure Level — the level of a behaviour at which overall health risk is lowest. For alcohol in adults under 40, population studies now place this close to zero.
UK unit
10ml, or 8g, of pure ethanol. A standard UK measure used on drink labels and in health guidance — not the same as a "standard drink" in other countries.
Section 2

Why does alcohol matter for health?

For most of the last half-century, public understanding of alcohol has been shaped by a now-weakened idea: that a little is good for you. More recent, larger, and better-designed studies have steadily eroded that picture. In 2023, the Office for National Statistics recorded 10,473 alcohol-specific deaths in the UK10 — the highest number since records began, and 38% higher than in 2019.10 About three-quarters of those deaths were from alcohol-related liver disease.10 None of these figures include the much larger total of alcohol-related deaths — cancers, heart disease, strokes, and injuries where alcohol was one contributing factor.

Alcohol affects many body systems simultaneously. The five headline reasons it matters:

  • 1
    It causes at least seven cancers
    Alcohol is an established cause of cancers of the mouth, throat (pharynx and larynx), oesophagus (food pipe), liver, bowel (colorectum), and female breast.8 In the UK, alcohol is responsible for around 3.3% of all new cancer cases each year — approximately 11,900 cases — including roughly 1 in 10 cases of female breast cancer (about 4,400 women per year).11 For context, tobacco accounts for around 15% of UK cancer cases and overweight/obesity for 6% — alcohol is a major contributor, but not the largest.11
  • 2
    It raises cardiovascular risk across the board
    Alcohol raises blood pressure, triggers atrial fibrillation (an abnormal heart rhythm — increasingly recognised even at modest intake), and increases risk of haemorrhagic stroke (bleeding into the brain) and heart failure.4 Genetic studies now show the rise in risk is monotonic — meaning risk goes up with every unit, not down at low doses.4
  • 3
    It damages the liver in predictable stages
    Alcohol-related liver disease moves through three recognised stages — fatty liver (steatosis), inflammation (alcoholic hepatitis), and scarring (cirrhosis).12 Fatty liver develops in almost all heavy drinkers and is reversible. Around 20–30% of those who keep drinking heavily progress to alcoholic hepatitis. Roughly 10% (up to 1 in 5) develop cirrhosis, which is permanent.12
  • 4
    It shrinks the brain — at lower levels than many realise
    Imaging studies of tens of thousands of UK adults show smaller grey matter and reduced white-matter integrity — the brain's wiring — detectable even at 1–2 units a day.6 Heavy, chronic drinking causes cognitive decline and increases dementia risk;7 it is also one of the Lancet Commission's 14 modifiable risk factors for dementia.13
  • 5
    It worsens mental health and sleep
    Alcohol is often used to manage anxiety, low mood, or poor sleep — yet it consistently worsens all three.9 It fragments sleep architecture (reduces deep and REM sleep), flattens mood in the days after drinking, reduces the effectiveness of antidepressant medication, and is strongly linked to suicide risk at higher intakes.9
Section 3

What your doctor might do

In UK practice, GPs approach alcohol through screening, conversation, investigation, and — where relevant — a structured treatment pathway set out in NICE guideline CG115. None of it assumes that someone must stop drinking completely; the aim is an informed conversation about risk and about change.

📋
Ask about units, not just "do you drink?"
The UK primary-care standard is the AUDIT-C — three questions about frequency, typical amount in a session, and how often the person drinks 6+ (women) or 8+ (men) units on a single occasion. A score of 5 or more triggers a fuller conversation, often using the full 10-question AUDIT to distinguish hazardous from dependent drinking.
🩸
Check blood tests where there is concern
Common tests include liver function tests (LFTs, which include ALT, AST and ALP — enzymes released when liver cells are stressed), GGT (gamma-glutamyl transferase — often raised in regular heavy drinking), and a full blood count looking at the MCV (mean corpuscular volume — red cell size, often enlarged in chronic heavy drinkers). Normal bloods do not rule out harm, but abnormal ones prompt further investigation.
🔬
Investigate the liver where indicated
Where there is a concern about alcohol-related liver disease, UK practice is to use non-invasive tools — particularly FibroScan (transient elastography — an ultrasound-based scan that measures liver stiffness, a marker of scarring). Ultrasound can identify fatty liver; more advanced scarring may require referral to a hepatologist (liver specialist).
💬
Offer a brief intervention for hazardous drinking
For most people drinking above 14 units/week but not dependent, the evidence-based NHS response is a short structured conversation — sometimes called a "brief intervention" — that sets out personal risk, explores motivation for change, and agrees specific, practical goals. Cochrane reviews suggest this alone moves a meaningful proportion of people back toward low-risk drinking.
⚕️
Structured treatment for dependence
Where dependence is identified, NICE describes a stepped-care pathway — psychological therapy (CBT, behavioural couples therapy, motivational interviewing), medication (acamprosate or naltrexone to reduce craving after detox; disulfiram where supervised), and, for more severe cases, specialist assessment for assisted withdrawal. Abrupt withdrawal in heavy drinkers can be dangerous — clinically recognised as time-sensitive — so medically supervised detox with thiamine (vitamin B1) and benzodiazepine cover is standard.
🤝
Community support
Alongside NHS treatment, UK GPs typically signpost to community services — local alcohol services (commissioned by the Integrated Care Boards — NHS bodies that plan and fund health services for a region), Alcoholics Anonymous, SMART Recovery, Alcohol Change UK, and online self-help tools. Long-term evidence is strongest where medical treatment and peer support are combined.

None of this is a single-visit process. In UK practice, the typical pattern is a conversation, blood tests, a follow-up review of those results, and an agreed plan — whether that plan is "cut down over three months", "I want to stop and need help with withdrawal", or "I want to think about it". For anything that feels personally relevant, this is a conversation for you to have with your GP or healthcare professional.

Section 4

What the research shows

The evidence on alcohol has shifted substantially in the last decade. The older story — moderate drinking may be good for the heart — came from observational studies where "non-drinkers" often included people who had stopped drinking because of existing illness (the "sick quitter" problem). Newer studies using genetic methods, and meta-analyses that exclude this bias, tell a different story. Six key studies:

Global Burden of Disease 2016
Griswold et al · The Lancet · 2018
Global Analysis
2.8M
alcohol-attributable deaths globally, 2016

This analysis pulled together 694 data sources and 592 studies from 195 countries. It found alcohol was the seventh-leading risk factor for death and disability worldwide, accounting for 2.2% of female and 6.8% of male deaths.

The headline conclusion: the level of alcohol consumption that minimises overall health risk — across all causes combined — is zero. Any cardiovascular benefit at low doses is outweighed by increased cancer, injury, and infection risk.

Systematic analysis — the most comprehensive global estimate of alcohol's health burden published to date.
Global Burden of Disease 2020 — age-specific
Bryazka et al · The Lancet · 2022
Global Analysis
1.34B
people consumed harmful amounts of alcohol in 2020

The follow-up analysis refined the picture by age. For people aged 15–39, the theoretical minimum risk exposure level (TMREL — the level at which overall risk is lowest) was close to zero standard drinks per day: no health benefit, only risks (injury in particular).

For people aged 40 and over, the curve was J-shaped in some regions — a small amount of alcohol may offer a modest net benefit for cardiovascular disease, stroke or diabetes — but this varies by individual risk profile and region, and does not apply at higher intakes.

Key take-home: the "safe amount" depends on age. For younger adults, the modern evidence says abstinence is best.
UK Biobank — Mendelian randomisation
Biddinger et al · JAMA Network Open · 2022
Genetic Analysis
1.3×
rise in hypertension risk per standard-deviation increase in alcohol intake

This analysis of 371,463 UK Biobank participants used genetic variants that influence alcohol intake as a natural experiment — meaning the findings reflect causation, not just correlation. Each standard-deviation rise in alcohol intake was associated with 1.3× higher risk of high blood pressure and 1.4× higher risk of coronary artery disease.

Crucially, the relationship was non-linear and monotonic — light drinking carried modest increases in risk, while heavier drinking produced exponentially larger rises. There was no point on the curve at which alcohol lowered cardiovascular risk.

Result: the old "J-curve" in observational data disappears once genetic methods correct for confounding.
All-cause mortality meta-analysis
Zhao et al · JAMA Network Open · 2023
Meta-analysis
25g
daily intake threshold where mortality risk rises — ~3 UK units for women, ~5–6 units for men

This systematic review pooled 107 prospective cohort studies. Once studies were corrected for the "sick-quitter" problem (former heavy drinkers being classed as non-drinkers) and for reverse causation, low-to-moderate alcohol intake was no longer associated with reduced mortality risk.

Increased risk of death became evident at 25g/day (~3 UK units) for women and 45g/day (~5–6 UK units) for men.

This meta-analysis is one of the main reasons public-health guidance has moved away from "a glass a day is good for you".
UK Biobank brain imaging
Daviet et al · Nature Communications · 2022
Neuroimaging
~2 yrs
brain-ageing effect of moving from 1 → 2 units/day at age 50

Brain MRI scans of 36,678 middle-aged and older adults in the UK Biobank found negative associations between alcohol intake and both grey and white matter volumes — the bulk of the brain's tissue and its connecting wiring.

The effect was not confined to heavy drinking: reductions in brain volume were detectable from 1–2 units a day. Larger intakes produced progressively larger effects on brain structure.

One of the largest brain-imaging studies of alcohol — clarifies that "moderate" drinking is not neurologically neutral.
Whitehall II — 23-year dementia follow-up
Sabia et al · BMJ · 2018
Cohort Study
+17%
dementia risk per 7-unit increase above 14 units/week

This UK civil-service cohort followed 9,087 adults for 23 years. Drinkers exceeding the 14-unit weekly guideline showed a stepwise increase in dementia risk — HR (hazard ratio — each 7-unit increase above guideline was associated with 17% higher risk; HR 1.17).

Alcohol was also confirmed as a modifiable risk factor in the Lancet Commission's 2024 update, which identified 14 modifiable contributors to dementia, including excessive alcohol use.

Consistent with the brain-imaging findings — chronic high intake has measurable long-term cognitive consequences.

How alcohol actually causes harm

🧬
DNA damage via acetaldehyde
When the liver breaks down alcohol, it first produces acetaldehyde — a reactive compound that binds to DNA, causes mutations, and interferes with DNA repair. This is the principal mechanism by which alcohol causes cancer.
🔥
Oxidative stress and inflammation
Alcohol metabolism generates reactive oxygen species — unstable molecules that damage cell membranes and proteins. Chronic drinking drives systemic inflammation, which contributes to cancer, heart disease, and liver injury.
💧
Hormonal disruption
Alcohol raises blood oestrogen levels and lowers sex hormone-binding globulin — one of the main reasons it increases female breast cancer risk. It also impairs testosterone regulation in men.
🫀
Direct cardiac effects
Alcohol irritates the electrical conduction system of the heart, triggering atrial fibrillation; it raises blood pressure acutely and chronically; and at high chronic intakes it weakens the heart muscle itself (alcoholic cardiomyopathy).
🍽️
Gut barrier disruption
Alcohol damages the gut lining, increasing intestinal permeability ("leaky gut") and allowing bacterial products to cross into the bloodstream. This drives liver inflammation and systemic immune activation.
🧠
Neurotoxicity
Alcohol is directly toxic to brain cells at high doses, and impairs thiamine (vitamin B1) absorption — the cause of Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome (a severe, sometimes irreversible memory disorder in chronic heavy drinkers).

Putting it all together

Alcohol has been part of human culture for thousands of years, and for many people it is woven into celebration, social life, and daily unwinding. The research reviewed here is not an argument against any of that — it is an argument for clarity about what is actually being traded. Alcohol is a carcinogen, a cardiovascular stressor, a liver toxin and a brain toxin. The earlier picture of "moderate drinking is good for you" has largely unravelled under the weight of genetic studies and better-designed meta-analyses.

The modern evidence describes a straightforward dose-response: less is better, and drink-free days matter. For people under 40, population-level analyses now place the lowest-risk intake close to zero. For older adults, a small amount may be net-neutral or modestly beneficial in some contexts — but only against a background of otherwise good cardiovascular health, and only at intakes well below the 14-unit guideline.

UK Chief Medical Officers' guidance — no more than 14 units a week, spread over three or more days, with drink-free days — is a practical framework, not a target. Many of the benefits of cutting down appear quickly: blood pressure falls within weeks, sleep quality improves within days, and the liver's fatty changes reverse within months of reducing intake substantially.

None of this is about judgement. For anyone wondering how their own drinking maps onto these risks — or considering change — this is a conversation for you to have with your GP or healthcare professional. Stopping completely carries the lowest overall health risk. For anyone who chooses to keep drinking, the evidence is just as clear: any reduction produces measurable benefit — you don't need to get it perfect to see it.
