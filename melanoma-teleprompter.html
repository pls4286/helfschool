▶ Slide 2 — Myths · ADVANCE NOW — then read
Two things I want to address straight away. First — a tan is not a sign of health. It's the skin's visible response to DNA damage from UV radiation. And second — melanoma doesn't only affect people with fair skin. It occurs across all skin tones, including a subtype that develops on the palms, soles of the feet, and under the nails — which can be particularly hard to spot in people with darker skin.
▶ Slides 3–4 — What is melanoma? · ADVANCE NOW — then read
Melanoma starts in the pigment cells of the skin. What makes it dangerous is its ability to spread — to the lymph nodes and to distant organs — if it's not caught early. So knowing what to look for matters. The ABCDE rule: Asymmetry — one half doesn't match the other. Border — irregular or ragged edges. Colour — multiple shades within the same mole. Diameter — wider than 6mm, roughly the size of a pencil eraser. And Evolution — any change over time. Also worth knowing: about half of all melanomas carry a specific gene mutation — a BRAF mutation — which now shapes treatment decisions from the moment of diagnosis.
▶ Slide 5 — Why it matters · ADVANCE NOW — then read
Around 18,300 people are diagnosed with melanoma in the UK each year. And here's the number that I think matters most: when melanoma is caught at stage one — still confined to the skin — around 100% of people survive five years or more. That falls to around 85% at stage two, and around 75% at stage three. Stage at diagnosis is the single most powerful factor in outcome. And 86% of cases are linked to UV radiation — making this one of the most preventable cancers we know of.
▶ Slides 6–7 — What your doctor might do · ADVANCE NOW — then read
If a lesion looks concerning, the first step is usually a clinical assessment — increasingly with a dermatoscope, a handheld magnifying device that reveals structure below the skin surface. If there's doubt, the lesion is removed and examined — an excision biopsy. For higher-risk disease, gene testing for the BRAF mutation now happens at diagnosis, not just when the disease progresses. Treatment for early-stage melanoma is primarily surgery. For more advanced disease, two approaches have transformed outcomes: immunotherapy — which helps the body's immune system attack the cancer — and gene-targeted treatment, for the half of patients whose tumour carries a BRAF mutation.
▶ Slide 8 — Research 1 of 2 · ADVANCE NOW — then read
The clinical trial evidence here is remarkable. The KEYNOTE-006 trial compared a checkpoint inhibitor immunotherapy against an older immune treatment in advanced melanoma. Immunotherapy more than doubled median survival — from around 16 months to nearly 33. And the COMBI trials showed that gene-targeted combination treatment produced 5-year survival in 34% of patients with BRAF-mutant metastatic melanoma. A decade ago, almost none of those patients would have survived five years.
▶ Slide 9 — Research 2 of 2 · ADVANCE NOW — then read
The CheckMate 067 trial took this further — combining two immunotherapy agents that block different immune-suppression pathways simultaneously. At five years, 52% of patients on combination immunotherapy were alive, compared with 26% on the older treatment alone. These numbers represent one of the most profound shifts in cancer survival we have seen in any tumour type in modern oncology.
