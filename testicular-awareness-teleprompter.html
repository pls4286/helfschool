Segment 2 · What is awareness · What does normal look like

So what is testicular awareness? At its core, it's simply getting familiar with what's normal for your testicles — their usual size, shape, weight, and feel. That familiarity is what makes a change noticeable.

What does normal look and feel like? Each testicle should feel smooth and firm — no hard lumps on the surface. One being slightly larger than the other, or hanging a little lower, is entirely normal. That's just anatomy.

Something men often misidentify the first time they examine themselves is the epididymis. That's the soft, slightly raised ridge that runs along the back of each testicle — it carries sperm, it's supposed to be there, and it can feel a bit like a ridge or bump. Getting familiar with how it normally feels means you won't mistake it for something new.

Segment 3 · Changes worth having assessed

There are six changes that NHS guidance recognises as worth having assessed. First — a painless lump or swelling on a testicle, even something as small as a pea. I want to specifically flag the word painless here — testicular cancer is often painless. That absence of pain is clinically significant. Don't wait for it to hurt.

Second, one testicle getting progressively larger. Third, a new heavy or dragging sensation in the scrotum. Fourth, an ache or dull pain in the testicle, scrotum, or groin. Fifth, a hard or changed area in the epididymis — that soft tube at the back. And sixth — anything that simply feels different from what you know is your normal.

Most scrotal lumps are not cancer — they have benign causes, like cysts or inflammation. But all of them are worth having checked, rather than waiting to see what happens.

Segment 4 · How to check

NHS guidance recommends checking around once a month. The best time is during or after a warm bath or shower, when the scrotum is relaxed. Hold the scrotum in the palm of one hand, then gently roll each testicle between your thumb and fingers, feeling the surface carefully. Then feel behind each one for the epididymis, noting whether it feels as usual. Repeat on the other side.

The whole thing takes a few minutes. Regular checking builds the baseline that makes changes stand out — and that's exactly what awareness means in practice.

Segment 5 · Research

The research picture for testicular cancer is one of medicine's genuine success stories. Among men aged 15 to 44 at diagnosis, 97.6% survive for 10 years or more. Across all ages, 10-year survival stands at 96.5%. Mortality has fallen by more than four-fifths since the early 1970s.

Those figures reflect decades of treatment advances. But survival is always best when diagnosis is earliest — and awareness is what makes that possible for most men.
