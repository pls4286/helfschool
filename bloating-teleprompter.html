▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — 64% stat card
Bloating is one of the most common digestive symptoms there is. A 2025 UK survey found that nearly two thirds of UK adults had experienced it in the past month — including people with no diagnosed gut condition. It was the digestive symptom that caused the most anxiety.
— pause —
Most people don't mention it to their GP. They assume it's normal, or something they ate. And yet in most cases there's a reason for it — and once the reason is identified, it's manageable. Bloating deserves to be taken seriously.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — How bloating happens
Bloating isn't one thing — there are several different mechanisms, and which applies determines the approach.
— pause —
The most common: certain carbohydrates — known as FODMAPs — aren't fully absorbed in the small intestine. They pass into the colon, where bacteria ferment them rapidly and produce gas. The gut distends. You feel bloated. But gas production is only part of the story. Some people produce normal amounts of gas but their gut nerves are oversensitive — they feel what most people wouldn't notice. This is called visceral hypersensitivity, and it's central to IBS.
— pause —
There's also impaired gas transit — constipation means gas builds rather than passing. And SIBO — small intestinal bacterial overgrowth — where bacteria ferment food in the small intestine much earlier than they should. Bloating within minutes of eating is a hallmark.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4–5 — Causes & FODMAP table
FODMAPs — fermentable oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides and polyols — are short-chain carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed and rapidly fermented in the gut. The key ones: onion and garlic are among the highest FODMAP foods. Wheat — bread, pasta, cereals. Dairy, specifically the lactose. Apples, pears, mangoes. Beans and lentils.
— pause —
The low-FODMAP alternatives are specific, not restrictive. Rice instead of wheat. Lactose-free milk. Firm banana instead of apple. Hard cheese is fine. Strawberries are fine. Carrots are fine. One important point: the low-FODMAP diet is a diagnostic tool, not a permanent diet. In UK practice, foods are restricted for 2 to 6 weeks, then reintroduced one by one to identify specific triggers. Most people can eat the majority of foods again once the triggers are known.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 6 — Stepped approach
Most bloating doesn't need extensive investigation — the history is usually informative. But where there's clinical reason, targeted tests are used. Blood tests check for coeliac disease, anaemia, and thyroid problems. A hydrogen breath test diagnoses lactose intolerance, fructose malabsorption, and SIBO. For women with new, persistent bloating, a pelvic ultrasound is part of the clinical pathway — persistent unexplained bloating in women is clinically recognised as time-sensitive.
— pause —
Dietary approaches — particularly a dietitian-led low-FODMAP trial — are first line. Addressing constipation matters significantly. For medication: antispasmodics, low-dose amitriptyline for gut-brain modulation, rifaximin where SIBO is confirmed. Gut-directed hypnotherapy and CBT have good evidence, particularly where anxiety is involved. One critical point: coeliac testing requires gluten in the diet. Starting gluten-free before testing gives a false-negative result — and the diagnosis can be missed entirely.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 7–8 — Research evidence cards
The evidence is clear on the dietary approach. The landmark FODMAP trial — Halmos and colleagues, Monash University, 2014 — was a randomised crossover study. IBS patients on a low-FODMAP diet had significantly lower symptom scores, and specifically reduced bloating, pain, and wind.
— pause —
A 2022 Gut network meta-analysis pooled 13 randomised trials and found the low-FODMAP diet ranked first for global symptoms, abdominal pain, and specifically bloating and distension — across all dietary interventions studied. And a 2023 Rome Foundation global study — 51,000 people across 26 countries — found nearly 18% experience bloating at least weekly. Women are twice as likely as men. The evidence consistently shows bloating is common, underscreened, and responsive to targeted treatment.
