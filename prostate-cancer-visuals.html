Prostate
Cancer
What medicine knows — and what the evidence shows about treatment, screening, and survival.
Dr Paul · Retired NHS GP · helf.school
The scale of prostate cancer in the UK
~57,900
new cases per year
The most common cancer in UK males — 28% of all new male cancer cases. Around 160 diagnoses every day.
Cancer Research UK · 2019/2021–2022
78.9%
survive 10 or more years
Around 8 in 10 men diagnosed in the UK now survive 10 years or more — up from fewer than 1 in 4 in the early 1970s.
Cancer Research UK · 2018
⚠️
Around 12,200 deaths per year — 2nd most common cause of cancer death in UK males. Lifetime risk: 1 in 6 UK males diagnosed.
What is prostate cancer?
🫀
The prostate gland
Walnut-sized gland sitting below the bladder in men — surrounds the urethra and produces fluid that forms part of semen.
Anatomy
🔬
Cancer development
Cells divide and grow uncontrolled. Almost all are adenocarcinomas — gland-cell cancers. Most grow slowly; some are more aggressive.
>99%
📊
ISUP grade groups — how aggressive?
Cancer cells graded using Gleason score → translated into ISUP groups 1–5. Group 1 = least aggressive. Group 5 = most aggressive.
1 → 5
📍
Localised disease
Cancer confined within the prostate gland. The most treatable stage — radical prostatectomy or radiotherapy can be curative.
Stage 1–2
🗺️
Locally advanced
Cancer has grown beyond the prostate capsule — the outer covering — into surrounding tissue, but has not spread to distant sites.
Stage 3
🌐
Metastatic
Spread to bone, lymph nodes, or other distant sites. Manageable — not curable. Hormone therapy and chemotherapy extend survival.
Stage 4
Risk factors and the PSA test
⏳
Age
The most significant risk factor. Risk rises sharply after 50. Most diagnoses are in men aged 70–74. Incidence is highest in men aged 75–79.
After 50
🧬
Ethnicity — Black men at higher risk
Clinically recognised higher incidence rates in Black men compared with White men — established in UK clinical guidelines (NICE NG131).
↑ Risk
👨‍👧‍👦
Family history
First-degree relative (father/brother) with prostate cancer increases risk. BRCA gene mutations — associated with breast cancer — also raise risk.
↑ Risk
🚫
No preventable causes
Prostate cancer is NOT clearly linked to any modifiable lifestyle risk factor — no diet, exercise, or alcohol pattern has been conclusively established.
Cannot prevent
🩸
The PSA test — a marker, not a diagnosis
PSA (prostate-specific antigen) blood test measures a protein from the prostate. Elevated level prompts investigation — can also be raised by BPH, prostatitis, infection.
Marker only
⚠️
~3 in 4 with raised PSA will not have cancer
Around 75% of men with elevated PSA do not have prostate cancer. PSA is an imperfect marker — it prompts investigation, not a definitive diagnosis.
75% benign
How prostate cancer is diagnosed — NICE NG131
💊
Symptoms — often none
Urinary difficulty, blood in urine or semen, or bone pain may occur. Many men have no symptoms at all — PSA testing or incidental finding.
Often silent
🩸
PSA test and urgent referral
PSA above age-specific threshold → urgent 2-week wait referral to urology. Around 3 in 4 elevated PSA results will not be cancer.
2WW referral
🔭
mpMRI — first-line investigation
Multiparametric MRI recommended by NICE NG131 before biopsy. Scores 1–5 on Likert scale. Identifies suspicious areas — reduces unnecessary biopsies.
NICE NG131
🔬
Prostate biopsy
Tissue samples confirm diagnosis. Transrectal and transperineal approaches used in UK practice. NICE NG131 notes a shift towards transperineal — may reduce sepsis risk.
Confirms
📊
Grading + staging scans
ISUP grade group determined from biopsy. CT and/or bone scan used to assess spread for higher-risk disease. PSMA-PET increasingly used in specialist centres.
ISUP 1–5
🏥
MDT review — risk stratification
All prostate cancer cases discussed at multidisciplinary team meeting. Grade, stage, and PSA combined to risk-stratify — low, intermediate, or high — and agree treatment.
MDT
Treatment approaches in UK practice — NICE NG131
👁️
Active surveillance
NICE-recommended for low-risk localised disease. Regular PSA tests, MRI, and repeat biopsy — treatment deferred unless progression detected.
NICE NG131
🔪
Radical prostatectomy — surgery
Removal of the entire prostate — robotic-assisted in most UK centres. Side effects include urinary leakage and erectile dysfunction (variable severity).
Curative
☢️
Radiotherapy
External beam (EBRT) or internal seeds (brachytherapy). Often combined with hormone therapy for higher-risk disease. Bowel and urinary side effects.
Curative
💉
Hormone therapy (ADT)
Suppresses testosterone — injections (goserelin/Zoladex, leuprorelin/Prostap) or daily oral tablet (relugolix/Orgovyx). Long-term side effects include bone density loss and fatigue.
ADT
💊
Docetaxel (Taxotere) — chemotherapy
Added to ADT for fit men with newly diagnosed metastatic disease. STAMPEDE trial showed extended median survival vs hormone therapy alone.
+10 months
💊
Abiraterone (Zytiga) + prednisolone
Further suppresses testosterone production. NICE-approved option for metastatic hormone-sensitive prostate cancer — given with prednisolone (a corticosteroid).
NICE TA493
Research · treatment and screening
Key Finding
2.7%
prostate cancer-specific mortality at 15 years
similar across monitoring (3.1%), surgery (2.2%), and radiotherapy (2.9%) — P=0.53 · 1,643 men
ProtecT — localised prostate cancer
Hamdy FC et al · N Engl J Med · 2023 · PMID 36912538
1,643 UK men with PSA-detected localised prostate cancer followed for a median 15 years. Prostate cancer-specific mortality was low and statistically similar across all three treatment groups. The key differences lie in side effects, not survival — the choice between monitoring, surgery, and radiotherapy is a trade-off of effects, not mortality risk.
Key Finding
0.69%
prostate cancer mortality — PSA screening group
vs 0.78% without screening — 415,357 men, UK primary care, 15-year follow-up
CAP trial — PSA screening
Martin RM et al · JAMA · 2024 · PMID 38581198
The largest PSA screening trial ever conducted — 415,357 UK men aged 50–69 randomised to an invitation for a single PSA test or standard care. 15-year follow-up showed significantly lower prostate cancer mortality in the screened group. First UK trial evidence that a single PSA test reduces prostate cancer deaths — though the absolute difference is modest.
Research · advanced disease treatment
Key Finding
81 months
median overall survival — docetaxel + hormone therapy
vs 71 months — hormone therapy alone · metastatic hormone-naïve prostate cancer · HR 0.78
STAMPEDE — docetaxel + ADT
James ND et al · The Lancet · 2016 · PMID 26719232
Large UK platform trial comparing standard hormone therapy (ADT) alone against ADT plus docetaxel (Taxotere — chemotherapy that interferes with cancer cell division) in men with high-risk or metastatic prostate cancer. Adding docetaxel upfront improved overall survival in the metastatic group — median 81 vs 71 months (HR 0.78; 95% CI 0.66–0.93; P=0.006). This established early docetaxel alongside ADT as standard care for fit men with newly diagnosed metastatic prostate cancer.
What the evidence shows
The most common.
The most treatable.
Prostate cancer is common — and where the evidence is clear, the choices matter.
🔬
ProtecT trial — localised disease
At 15 years, prostate cancer-specific mortality was similar across monitoring, surgery, and radiotherapy — 2.7% overall. The choice is about side effects, not survival.
2.7%
🩸
CAP trial — PSA screening
A single PSA test reduced 15-year prostate cancer mortality — 0.69% vs 0.78% in the unscreened group. First UK trial evidence for PSA screening benefit.
0.69%
💊
STAMPEDE — metastatic disease
Adding docetaxel chemotherapy to hormone therapy extended median overall survival by 10 months — 81 vs 71 months — in metastatic prostate cancer.
+10 months
👁️
Active surveillance — not always treatment
NICE NG131 recommends active surveillance for low-risk localised prostate cancer — close monitoring defers treatment until the cancer shows signs of progression.
NICE
