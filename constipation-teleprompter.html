▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — Big picture
Constipation is extremely common. Around 1 in 7 adults in the UK is affected at any given time — and it affects women twice as often as men. It accounts for around 83,000 hospital admissions a year in England, alongside 18.6 million laxative prescriptions annually.
— pause —
The important point is that most cases are functional — meaning no structural cause is found. Lifestyle, medications, and toileting habits account for the majority. And that means most cases respond well to straightforward, evidence-based measures.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 3–4 — What drives constipation
The most common drivers are low dietary fibre, insufficient fluid intake, and physical inactivity. Certain medications are also important — opioids, iron supplements, some antidepressants, and calcium-based antacids are all well-established causes. And repeatedly ignoring the urge to open the bowels weakens the reflex over time.
— pause —
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–6 — What the evidence supports
On the positive side, the evidence points clearly to a set of practical steps. Fibre is foundational — 25 to 30 grams daily, from vegetables, fruit, wholegrains, and legumes, increased gradually to avoid bloating. Fluids alongside: 1.5 to 2 litres daily. Regular movement helps — colonic motility responds directly to physical activity.
— pause —
And one step that's frequently overlooked: a footstool when using the toilet, raising the feet 20 to 30 centimetres. This mimics the squatting position, relaxes the puborectalis muscle, and makes passage easier with less straining. Simple, free, and evidence-aligned.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 7 — Research cards
On laxatives — the key finding is that macrogol — sold as Movicol or Laxido — is the evidence-based first choice. A Cochrane systematic review found macrogol superior to lactulose across all four primary outcomes: stool frequency, stool consistency, straining, and need for rescue laxative use. This is what underpins NICE guidance for adults.
— pause —
On fibre supplements — a 2016 meta-analysis in Alimentary Pharmacology and Therapeutics found a 77% response rate with fibre supplements — psyllium and ispaghula husk, sold as Fybogel — compared to 44% on placebo. That's a relative risk of 1.71.
— pause —
One important distinction: this evidence is for fibre supplements specifically — psyllium and ispaghula — not for increasing whole-food dietary fibre, for which the randomised trial evidence is more limited.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 8–9 — Mechanisms
The mechanisms are straightforward. Fibre adds bulk and retains water in the stool. Fluid keeps stool hydrated and soft. Movement stimulates peristalsis through the enteric nervous system. The footstool relaxes the anorectal angle. Macrogol draws water into the colon by osmosis — without being absorbed systemically.
— pause —
And if constipation is persistent or doesn't respond to these measures, the clinically important question is whether there's an underlying cause — hypothyroidism, diabetes, a pelvic floor problem, or a medication effect. Identifying and treating the cause is more effective than treating the symptom indefinitely.
