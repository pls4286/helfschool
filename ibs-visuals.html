Irritable Bowel Syndrome —
real symptoms, real disorder
What IBS actually is, why it gets undertreated, and what the evidence says works.
Dr Paul · Retired NHS GP · helf.school
IBS — scale and subtypes
1 in 10
UK adults have IBS — one of the most common conditions in primary care worldwide
Oka et al. · Lancet Gastroenterol Hepatol 2020 · systematic review · Sperber et al. · Gastroenterology 2021 · 73,076 adults · 33 countries
IBS-D
Diarrhoea-predominant
Loose stools, urgency, frequent bowel movements. Often triggered by eating. Most common subtype overall.
IBS-C
Constipation-predominant
Hard stools, straining, feeling of incomplete emptying. Bloating prominent. More common in women.
IBS-M
Mixed
Both loose stools and constipation — often alternating. Most complex to manage. Significant bloating.
IBS-U
Unclassified
Meets IBS criteria but doesn't fit neatly into D, C, or M. Stool pattern varies or is inconsistent.
IBS is now formally classified as a disorder of gut-brain interaction (DGBI) — driven by abnormal gut-brain communication, not structural damage or inflammation. Normal scans and tests do not mean nothing is wrong.
Why IBS matters — what it does to daily life
😟
Pain & unpredictability
Cramping abdominal pain, urgency, unpredictable bowel changes — dictating where people go, what they eat, when they travel.
Chronic
💼
Work & social life
Missed work, social avoidance, cancelled plans. Quality of life scores in IBS are consistently lower than in many other chronic conditions.
↓ QoL
😔
Mental health
Anxiety and depression are far more prevalent in IBS — not as causes, but as two-way gut-brain interactions. Each makes the other worse.
↔ Two-way
🍽️
Diet & nutrition anxiety
Many people restrict foods excessively — sometimes to the point of nutritional risk. Unguided elimination diets without dietitian support can cause harm.
↑ Risk
🏥
Misdiagnosis & undertreated years
Up to 1 in 4 people later diagnosed with IBS were first told they had something else — many without a management plan or follow-up.
Delayed
What your doctor might do — stepped approach
🧪
Rule out first — bloods & calprotectin
FBC + CRP, coeliac serology (common IBS-D mimic), faecal calprotectin (distinguishes IBS from IBD). Normal results support the diagnosis.
1st step
🍽️
Dietitian-led low-FODMAP diet
4–6 week restriction then systematic reintroduction. Effective in ~50–70% of people — requires dietitian guidance for safe implementation.
Dietitian
💊
Symptom-specific medications
Antispasmodics (mebeverine/Colofac) for cramps · loperamide for IBS-D · laxatives for IBS-C · peppermint oil capsules for bloating.
Targeted
💊
Low-dose amitriptyline — ATLANTIS 2023
463 patients, 55 GP practices — amitriptyline significantly improved IBS vs placebo. NNT = 6. No specialist referral needed.
NNT = 6
🎯
Gut-directed hypnotherapy & CBT
Gut-directed hypnotherapy has trial evidence comparable to low-FODMAP — with added mental health benefit. CBT also effective. NHS referral pathways exist.
→ Refer
Research · scale & treatment
Key Finding
1 in 10
UK adults — underdiagnosed globally
Sperber et al. · 73,076 adults · 33 countries · 2021
Sperber et al. — global epidemiology of IBS
Gastroenterology · 2021 · 73,076 adults · 33 countries
IBS is significantly underdiagnosed and undertreated globally. Many patients receive "everything looks normal" without explanation, management plan, or referral pathway ⚑.
Key Finding
NNT = 6
low-dose amitriptyline · IBS
463 patients · 55 GP practices · 6 months
ATLANTIS trial — amitriptyline for IBS
Ford et al. · The Lancet · 2023 · 463 patients · 55 GP practices
Amitriptyline significantly improved IBS Symptom Severity Scores vs placebo over 6 months. NNT = 6 — no specialist referral required. A clear signal for primary care to offer this when first-line fails ⚑.
Research · dietary & psychological treatments
Key Finding
50–70%
symptom improvement
low-FODMAP diet · IBS · Monash University
Halmos et al. — low-FODMAP crossover RCT
Gastroenterology · 2014 · Monash University · 30 IBS patients
Low-FODMAP significantly reduced IBS symptoms compared with normal diet. The evidence-based first-line dietary approach for IBS — but requires dietitian guidance for safe implementation ⚑.
Key Finding
≈ Equal
hypnotherapy vs low-FODMAP
at 6 months — plus added mental health benefit
Peters et al. — gut-directed hypnotherapy vs low-FODMAP
American Journal of Gastroenterology · 2016 · 74 patients · Monash University
Hypnotherapy and low-FODMAP produced comparable, durable improvement at 6 months. Hypnotherapy additionally improved anxiety and quality of life — an important added benefit ⚑.
Research · diagnosis & subtypes
Key Finding
Rome IV
current diagnostic standard
recurrent pain ≥1 day/week for 3 months + stool changes
Mearin et al. — Rome IV criteria for IBS
Gastroenterology · 2016 · 150(6):1393–1407
Rome IV requires recurrent abdominal pain ≥1 day/week for 3 months, plus ≥2 of: change in stool frequency, stool form, or defecation-related pain. The international standard underpinning all modern IBS diagnosis ⚑.
Key Finding
~10%
IBS follows a gut infection
post-infectious IBS — a distinct recognised subtype
Post-infectious IBS — systematic review
Rodríguez et al. · Neurogastroenterology & Motility · 2020
Approximately 10% of IBS cases follow an acute gut infection — the infection resolves but the gut does not return to normal. Post-infectious IBS (PI-IBS) has altered gut microbiome and permeability ⚑.
Research · misdiagnosis & gut-brain axis
Key Finding
1 in 4
IBS patients previously misdiagnosed
calprotectin reliably distinguishes IBS from IBD
NICE CG61 · IBS diagnosis evidence
NICE Clinical Guideline CG61 · reviewed 2023
Up to 1 in 4 people later diagnosed with IBS were previously labelled with another condition. Faecal calprotectin reliably distinguishes IBS from IBD (inflammatory bowel disease) in primary care ⚑.
Key Finding
↔ Two-way
gut-brain axis · IBS & mental health
vagus nerve, microbiome, enteric nervous system
Mayer et al. — the gut-brain axis in IBS
Nature Reviews Gastroenterology & Hepatology · 2023
IBS is driven by bidirectional gut-brain communication — anxiety worsens gut symptoms; gut symptoms worsen anxiety. Explains why psychological therapies produce physical gut improvement ⚑.
How IBS actually works — the gut-brain axis
⚡
Visceral hypersensitivity
Gut nerves are oversensitive — normal contractions that others don't notice cause real pain. Consistently found in IBS. Not imagined.
→ Pain
🔄
Altered gut motility
The gut moves too fast (loose stools, urgency in IBS-D) or too slowly (constipation, bloating in IBS-C). Disrupted muscle coordination.
→ D or C
🦠
Altered gut microbiome
Gut bacteria composition differs in many people with IBS — particularly after PI-IBS. Drives fermentation patterns and symptom severity.
→ Fermentation
🧠
Gut-brain communication loop
Brain amplifies gut signals via the HPA stress axis and vagus nerve. Cortisol increases gut permeability and worsens IBS — a self-reinforcing loop.
→ Amplified
🫧
FODMAP fermentation
FODMAPs are poorly absorbed, pull water into the gut, and ferment rapidly — producing gas, distension and urgency.
→ Bloating
