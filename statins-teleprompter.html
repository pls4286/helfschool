Segment 1 — What are statins?

"Statins are a class of medication that lower LDL cholesterol — the so-called bad cholesterol — by reducing the amount the liver produces. They work by blocking an enzyme called HMG-CoA reductase — the key step in the body's cholesterol-making process."

"The most commonly prescribed statin in the UK is atorvastatin — usually 20mg for primary prevention, 80mg for secondary prevention. They're taken once daily, long-term. And they work best alongside lifestyle changes — not instead of them." PAUSE

"Statins are one of the most thoroughly studied drugs in the history of medicine. Over 170,000 participants across 26 major trials. The evidence base is formidable."

Segment 2 — Why does it matter?

"Statins are one of the most important tools we have for preventing heart attacks and strokes. But here's the problem — they're significantly underused. As of 2025, only around 57% of patients at higher cardiovascular risk had a current prescription for lipid-lowering therapy."

"Why? Largely because of concerns about side effects — many of which are not supported by the best available evidence. PAUSE The nocebo effect — where the expectation of harm leads to real, experienced symptoms — plays a huge role here. We'll get to that in the research section."

"The numbers matter. For every 10,000 people with existing cardiovascular disease taking a statin for five years — approximately 1,000 avoid a major cardiovascular event. For primary prevention — higher risk, no previous event — that figure is 500. These are large, meaningful numbers." PAUSE

Segment 3 — What your doctor will do

"Under current NICE guidance, statins should be offered to anyone with established cardiovascular disease regardless of cholesterol level, and anyone with a 10-year QRISK3 score of 10% or above. For those below 10%, statins can now be considered — particularly if lifestyle changes haven't been enough." PAUSE

"The recommended first-choice statin in the UK is atorvastatin. Primary prevention: 20mg daily. Secondary prevention — existing cardiovascular disease: 80mg daily. The treatment target is a reduction in non-HDL cholesterol of at least 40%."

"On side effects — particularly muscle symptoms — the clinical picture is worth knowing. In UK practice, switching to a different statin or adjusting the dose often resolves the problem. The research shows the outcome medicine is most concerned about is stopping unilaterally — which leaves the cardiovascular risk unaddressed while the person was being protected." PAUSE

Segment 4 — What the research shows

"The Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration — the world's largest pooled analysis of statin data — has consistently found that each 1 mmol/L reduction in LDL reduces the risk of major cardiovascular events by approximately one fifth. This holds across men and women, all ages studied, regardless of starting cholesterol level."

"On side effects — the 2022 CTT meta-analysis, drawing on individual data from over 150,000 people across 23 trials, found that statins caused only a small excess of mostly mild muscle pain. More than 90% of all muscle symptom reports in people taking statins were not caused by the statin itself." PAUSE

"The ASCOT-LLA trial demonstrated this beautifully. When patients knew they were taking a statin, muscle symptoms were 41% more likely than in those who didn't know. When the same patients were blinded — unaware of what they were taking — muscle symptoms were virtually identical to placebo. That's the nocebo effect in action."

"And a 2026 Lancet analysis of statin product labels concluded that most of the side effects listed on packaging — including cognitive impairment, depression, sleep disturbance — are not supported by causal evidence from randomised trials." PAUSE

Segment 5 — Closing

"So — statins are among the most effective and best-evidenced medicines in cardiovascular prevention. For people at significant risk, the benefits substantially outweigh the genuine risks."

"The decision to take a statin should always be made in partnership with your doctor — based on your individual risk profile, your preferences, and your circumstances. If you've been offered one and you're hesitant — take what you've heard today to that conversation. That's exactly what it's for." PAUSE
