▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — How statins work
Statins are a class of medication that lower LDL cholesterol — the so-called bad cholesterol — by reducing the amount the liver produces. They work by blocking an enzyme called HMG-CoA reductase — the key step in the body's cholesterol-making process.
— pause —
The most commonly prescribed statin in the UK is atorvastatin — usually 20mg for primary prevention, 80mg for secondary prevention. They're taken once daily, long-term, and in clinical practice they're initiated alongside lifestyle measures — the two are complementary.
— pause —
Statins are one of the most thoroughly studied drugs in the history of medicine. Over 170,000 participants across 26 major trials. The evidence base is formidable.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 3–4 — Side effects and benefits
Statins are one of the most important tools available for preventing heart attacks and strokes. But they're significantly underused. As of 2025, only around 57% of patients at higher cardiovascular risk had a current prescription for lipid-lowering therapy.
— pause —
Why? Largely because of concerns about side effects — many of which are not supported by the best available evidence. The nocebo effect — where the expectation of harm leads to real, experienced symptoms — plays a significant role here. We'll get to that in the evidence section.
— pause —
The numbers matter. For every 10,000 people with existing cardiovascular disease taking a statin for five years — approximately 1,000 avoid a major cardiovascular event. For primary prevention — higher risk, no previous event — that figure is 500. These are large, meaningful numbers.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — Dosing table
Under current NICE guidance — NG238 — statin therapy is clinically indicated for anyone with established cardiovascular disease regardless of cholesterol level, and for anyone with a 10-year QRISK3 score of 10% or above. For those below 10%, statins can now be considered — particularly where lifestyle measures alone have not been sufficient.
— pause —
The recommended first-choice statin in the UK is atorvastatin. Primary prevention: 20mg daily. Secondary prevention — existing cardiovascular disease: 80mg daily. The treatment target for primary prevention is a reduction in non-HDL cholesterol of at least 40%.
— pause —
On muscle symptoms — in UK clinical practice, switching to a different statin or adjusting the dose is the recognised approach when symptoms arise. The outcome that cardiovascular medicine is most concerned about is stopping without review — which leaves risk unaddressed while the individual had been receiving protection.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–7 — Research evidence
The Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration — the world's largest pooled analysis of statin data — has consistently found that each 1 mmol/L reduction in LDL reduces the risk of major cardiovascular events by approximately one fifth. This holds across men and women, all ages studied, regardless of starting cholesterol level.
— pause —
On side effects — the 2022 CTT meta-analysis, drawing on individual data from over 150,000 people across 23 trials, found that statins caused only a small excess of mostly mild muscle pain. More than 90% of all muscle symptom reports in people taking statins were not caused by the statin itself.
— pause —
The ASCOT-LLA trial demonstrated the nocebo effect clearly. When patients knew they were taking a statin, muscle symptoms were 41% more likely than in those who didn't know. When the same patients were blinded — unaware of what they were taking — muscle symptoms were virtually identical to placebo. The expectation of harm produced real symptoms.
— pause —
And a 2026 Lancet analysis of statin product labels concluded that most side effects listed on packaging — including cognitive impairment, depression, and sleep disturbance — are not supported by causal evidence from randomised trials.

So — what are the key points?
— pause —
Statins are among the most effective and best-evidenced medicines in cardiovascular prevention. For people at significant risk, the benefits substantially outweigh the genuine risks. Concern about side effects is understandable, but the blinded trial evidence is clear — most reported muscle symptoms are not caused by the drug.
— pause —
Around 4 in 10 people at higher cardiovascular risk remain untreated. Closing that treatment gap could prevent tens of thousands of heart attacks and strokes per year in the UK. The evidence is strong. The noise is stronger.
