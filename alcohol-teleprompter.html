▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — UK units grid
First — the UK unit. One unit is 10 millilitres of pure alcohol.
— pause —
A small glass of wine — 1.5 units. A large glass — 3. A bottle of wine — 9. A pint of lager — about 2.3. A single spirit — 1 unit.
— pause —
The UK Chief Medical Officers' guideline, updated in 2016 — no more than 14 units a week, men and women. Spread over three or more days, with drink-free days in between. 14 units is about six pints of lager, or a bottle and a half of wine — for the whole week.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Five systems
Alcohol reaches every organ. Five systems matter most.
— pause —
Cancer. Alcohol is an established cause of seven cancers — mouth, throat, oesophagus, liver, bowel, and female breast. A Group 1 carcinogen — same category as tobacco. Around 11,900 UK cancer cases a year are attributable to alcohol.
— pause —
Heart and circulation. Raises blood pressure. Triggers atrial fibrillation. Genetic studies show risk rises with every unit — no protective dose. Liver. Three stages — fatty liver, inflammation, cirrhosis. Three-quarters of UK alcohol-specific deaths are from liver disease.
— pause —
Brain. MRI studies show shrinkage detectable from one to two units a day. Long-term heavy drinking raises dementia risk. And mood and sleep. It fragments sleep, flattens mood, weakens antidepressants. It feels like it helps. The data says otherwise.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — What your doctor might do
A GP's first move is the AUDIT-C — three quick questions that identify drinking already in the risk zone.
— pause —
Blood tests — liver function, GGT, red-cell size — flag chronic heavy use. If the liver looks stressed, a FibroScan measures scarring without a biopsy. For drinking above 14 units but not dependent, the NHS response is a brief intervention — a structured conversation about risk, motivation, and goals. Cochrane-backed.
— pause —
Where there's dependence, NICE CG115 describes stepped care — CBT, acamprosate and naltrexone, and supervised withdrawal. Abrupt withdrawal in heavy drinkers is clinically recognised as requiring medical supervision — that's why it's managed carefully, with thiamine cover.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 5 — Global Burden of Disease
Now the research. This is where the story has changed.
— pause —
The Global Burden of Disease study, in The Lancet 2018, pooled data from 195 countries. 2.8 million alcohol-attributable deaths globally. Seventh-leading risk factor worldwide. Headline finding: the level that minimises overall health risk is zero.
— pause —
The 2022 follow-up refined it by age. For adults under 40, the lowest-risk intake is near zero. For over-40s, a small amount may be net-neutral — but only at intakes well below 14 units a week.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 6 — J-curve debunked
The idea that a glass a day is good for you has largely unravelled.
— pause —
Biddinger, JAMA Network Open 2022 — UK Biobank data, 371,000 people, using genetic markers as a natural experiment. Each standard-deviation rise in alcohol gave 1.3 times the risk of high blood pressure, 1.4 times coronary artery disease. Risk rises monotonically. No protective dose.
— pause —
Zhao, JAMA Network Open 2023 — pooled 107 studies, 4.8 million people. Corrected for the old bias of classing former heavy drinkers as non-drinkers. Low-to-moderate drinking showed no mortality benefit. The J-curve was an artefact.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 7 — Brain and dementia
Two more — the brain, and dementia.
— pause —
Daviet, Nature Communications 2022 — MRI scans of 36,000 UK Biobank adults. Grey and white matter loss detectable from one to two units a day. At age 50, going from one to two units a day equated to brain changes equivalent to ageing by about two years.
— pause —
Sabia, BMJ 2018 — 9,000 UK civil servants, followed for 23 years. Drinkers above 14 units showed a stepwise dementia rise — about 17% higher risk per 7 units above guideline. The Lancet Commission 2024 confirms alcohol as one of 14 modifiable dementia risk factors.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 9 — CTA
So what does the evidence point toward?
— pause —
Stopping completely carries the lowest overall health risk — that option is always there. For those who continue drinking, the evidence is equally clear: any reduction produces measurable benefit. Blood pressure falls within weeks. Sleep improves within days. Fatty liver reverses within months.
— pause —
Four patterns that the evidence links to lower harm: including three or more drink-free days each week; choosing smaller measures and lower-ABV options; and understanding actual weekly unit intake — most people underestimate by half.
— pause —
As always — anything personally relevant here is a conversation for you to have with your GP or healthcare professional.
