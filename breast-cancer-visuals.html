Not one disease — three distinct types
Receptor status determines treatment · Source: Cancer Research UK
🟢
ER-positive (oestrogen receptor-positive)
The most common type — cancer cells use oestrogen to grow. Responds well to endocrine therapies including tamoxifen and aromatase inhibitors (letrozole, anastrozole, exemestane). The subject of the strongest evidence base in breast cancer treatment.
~80%
🟡
HER2-positive
Overproduces the HER2 protein (human epidermal growth factor receptor 2), driving faster growth. Responds to targeted therapies including trastuzumab (Herceptin — a monoclonal antibody). Given alongside chemotherapy for one year.
~15–20%
🔴
Triple-negative
Lacks oestrogen receptors, progesterone receptors, and HER2 — so cannot be treated with hormone-based or HER2-targeted therapies. Systemic chemotherapy (drug treatment working throughout the body) is the main systemic treatment option.
~15%

Ten-year survival stands at more than 3 in 4 women — 76.6% predicted for those diagnosed in 2018. Early detection is the single most powerful determinant of outcome.
How breast cancer is diagnosed
The triple assessment — standard UK diagnostic pathway · NICE NG101
1
Clinical examination
Assessment of the breast and lymph nodes (small glands under the arm) by a trained clinician. Covers shape, skin changes, nipple changes, and any lumps or thickening felt on examination.
Clinical
2
Imaging
Mammography (a low-dose X-ray of the breast) for women aged 40 and over. Ultrasound (a scan using sound waves to image internal structures) for younger women where denser breast tissue is harder to read on X-ray.
Imaging
3
Biopsy
A tissue sample taken from the area of concern — usually by needle — examined under a microscope. The only way to confirm cancer and determine receptor status (ER-positive, HER2-positive, or triple-negative).
Pathology
🎀
NHS Breast Screening Programme — women aged 50 to 70 invited for mammography every 3 years. Aims to detect cancers before symptoms appear, when treatment is most effective.
Treatment is personalised to the cancer's biology
Combined approach — determined by type, stage, and receptor status · NICE NG101
🔪
Surgery
Breast-conserving surgery (lumpectomy — removal of the tumour and a margin of surrounding tissue) or mastectomy (removal of the whole breast). Sentinel lymph node biopsy stages the axilla (armpit region) without full node clearance in most cases.
⚡
Radiotherapy
High-energy radiation directed at the breast or chest wall after breast-conserving surgery — reduces the risk of local recurrence (the cancer returning in the same area).
💊
Endocrine (hormone-blocking) therapy
Tamoxifen for premenopausal women with ER-positive disease. Aromatase inhibitors (letrozole, anastrozole, exemestane) for postmenopausal women. Typically 5–10 years of treatment.
🧪
Chemotherapy & targeted therapy
Chemotherapy used before surgery (neoadjuvant) or after (adjuvant). Trastuzumab (Herceptin) added for HER2-positive disease — given alongside chemotherapy for one year.
What the research shows
Sources: Marmot et al, Lancet 2012 · EBCTCG, Lancet 2011
NHS Breast Screening
20%
reduction in breast cancer mortality
women invited to screening · meta-analysis of 11 RCTs · Marmot 2012

The Independent UK Panel (Marmot 2012) pooled 11 randomised controlled trials, finding a relative risk of 0.80 (95% CI 0.73–0.89) — a 20% reduction in breast cancer mortality. The panel estimated around 1,300 deaths prevented per year. The same review identified a significant harm: for every 10,000 women invited to screening for 20 years, 129 cases represent overdiagnosis — cancers that would not have caused problems during the woman's lifetime but are treated nonetheless.

Independent UK Panel on Breast Cancer Screening. Lancet 2012;380(9855):1778–86. PMID 23117178.
5 years of tamoxifen — ER-positive
~⅓
reduction in breast cancer mortality
throughout first 15 years · EBCTCG · 20 trials · 21,457 women

The EBCTCG patient-level meta-analysis of 20 trials found that in women with ER-positive disease, five years of adjuvant tamoxifen reduced the breast cancer mortality rate by about a third throughout the first 15 years — irrespective of age, nodal status, or whether chemotherapy was also given. This established tamoxifen as a cornerstone of treatment for premenopausal ER-positive breast cancer.

EBCTCG. Lancet 2011;378(9793):771–84. PMID 21802721.
What the research shows
Source: EBCTCG, Lancet 2015 · 31,920 postmenopausal women with ER-positive early breast cancer
Aromatase inhibitors — postmenopausal ER-positive
~40%
reduction in 10-year breast cancer mortality
vs no endocrine treatment · EBCTCG 31,920 women

A further EBCTCG meta-analysis of 31,920 postmenopausal women with ER-positive early breast cancer found that five years of an aromatase inhibitor — letrozole, anastrozole, or exemestane — reduces 10-year breast cancer mortality by about 15% compared with tamoxifen, and by about 40% compared with no endocrine treatment. The proportional benefit was consistent regardless of age, tumour grade, nodal status, or HER2 status, and was present both during and after treatment ended.

These findings underpin NICE NG101 guidance recommending aromatase inhibitors as the standard first-choice endocrine therapy for postmenopausal women with ER-positive early breast cancer at medium or high risk of recurrence.

EBCTCG. Lancet 2015;386(10001):1341–52. PMID 26211827.
