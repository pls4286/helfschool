Not one disease — three distinct types
Receptor status determines treatment · Source: Cancer Research UK
🟢
Most common · ~80%
Hormone-sensitive (ER-positive)
Cancer cells use oestrogen to grow. Responds well to hormone-blocking treatment — the subject of the strongest evidence base in breast cancer. Around 80% of breast cancers fall into this category.
🟡
HER2-positive · ~15–20%
HER2-positive
Overproduces the HER2 protein, driving faster growth. Responds well to targeted antibody treatment given alongside chemotherapy. The development of this treatment transformed outcomes for HER2-positive disease.
🔴
Hardest to treat · ~15%
Triple-negative
Lacks oestrogen, progesterone, and HER2 receptors — so cannot be treated with hormone-blocking or HER2-targeted therapies. Chemotherapy (drug treatment working throughout the body) is the main systemic treatment option.

Ten-year survival stands at 76.6% — more than 3 in 4 women. Early detection is the single most powerful determinant of outcome.
How breast cancer is diagnosed
The triple assessment — standard UK diagnostic pathway · NICE NG101
👩‍⚕️
Step 1 — Clinical
Clinical examination
Assessment of the breast and lymph nodes (small glands under the arm) by a trained clinician. Covers shape, skin changes, nipple changes, and any lumps or thickening found on examination.
🔬
Step 2 — Imaging
Mammography or ultrasound
Mammography (low-dose X-ray) for women aged 40 and over. Ultrasound (a scan using sound waves) for younger women, where denser breast tissue is harder to read on X-ray. Both can identify areas needing further investigation.
🧫
Step 3 — Pathology
Biopsy — the only way to confirm
A tissue sample taken by needle, examined under a microscope. The only way to confirm cancer and determine receptor status — ER-positive, HER2-positive, or triple-negative — which determines the treatment pathway.
🎀
NHS Breast Screening Programme — women aged 50 to 70 invited for mammography every 3 years. Aims to detect cancers before symptoms appear, when treatment is most effective.
Treatment — personalised to the cancer's biology
Combined approach determined by type, stage, and receptor status · NICE NG101
🏥
Primary treatment
Surgery — the first step for most
Breast-conserving surgery (removing the tumour and a margin of surrounding tissue) or mastectomy (removal of the whole breast). Sentinel lymph node biopsy assesses whether cancer has reached the armpit glands without requiring full clearance in most cases.
💊
For hormone-sensitive disease
Hormone-blocking treatment
For ER-positive breast cancer — the most common type — hormone-blocking treatment reduces the oestrogen that cancer cells use to grow. Typically given for 5 to 10 years. This treatment has one of the strongest evidence bases in oncology.
⚡
Additional treatments
Radiotherapy, chemotherapy, targeted treatment
Radiotherapy after breast-conserving surgery reduces local recurrence. Chemotherapy before or after surgery for higher-risk disease. Targeted antibody treatment added for HER2-positive disease, given alongside chemotherapy for one year.

Marmot 2012 — The Independent Breast Screening Review

The Independent UK Panel pooled 11 randomised controlled trials of breast screening and found a 20% reduction in breast cancer mortality in women invited to screening. The review also identified a significant harm: for every 10,000 women screened over 20 years, around 129 cases represent overdiagnosis — cancers detected and treated that would not have caused problems in the woman's lifetime.

Independent UK Panel on Breast Cancer Screening. Lancet 2012;380(9855):1778–86. PMID 23117178.

EBCTCG 2011 — Hormone-blocking treatment evidence

A patient-level meta-analysis of 20 trials found that five years of hormone-blocking treatment reduced breast cancer mortality by about a third throughout the first 15 years in women with ER-positive disease — irrespective of age, nodal status, or whether chemotherapy was also given. This established hormone-blocking treatment as a cornerstone of care.

EBCTCG. Lancet 2011;378(9793):771–84. PMID 21802721.

EBCTCG 2015 — Aromatase inhibitor class evidence

A further meta-analysis of 31,920 postmenopausal women found that five years of a hormone-blocking treatment from the aromatase inhibitor class reduced 10-year breast cancer mortality by about 40% compared with no hormone-blocking treatment, and by about 15% compared with the older hormone-blocking treatment. The benefit was consistent regardless of age, tumour grade, nodal status, or HER2 status.

These findings underpin NICE NG101 guidance recommending this class as the standard first-choice hormone-blocking treatment for postmenopausal women with ER-positive early breast cancer at medium or high risk of recurrence.

EBCTCG. Lancet 2015;386(10001):1341–52. PMID 26211827.
The evidence is clear on one thing
Breast cancer caught early has around 100% five-year survival
🎀
The NHS Breast Screening Programme is the evidence-based route to early detection
Women aged 50 to 70 are invited for mammography every 3 years. The evidence shows a 20% reduction in breast cancer mortality in women invited to screening.
🔍
Breast awareness — knowing what's normal — is clinically recognised as important
Changes in shape, skin, nipple, or any new lump or thickening are recognised as changes worth discussing with a GP or healthcare professional.
📈
Mortality has fallen 46% since the early 1970s — treatment works
Screening, hormone-blocking treatment, targeted treatment, and improved surgery have together produced one of the most significant cancer survival improvements of the modern era.
