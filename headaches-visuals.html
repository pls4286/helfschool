Headaches —
Types & Red Flags
How the three main types differ, why accurate identification matters, and which patterns need prompt assessment.

⚠️
The three primary types require fundamentally different treatments — accurate type identification directly determines whether treatment works.
What distinguishes the three types · 1 of 2
📍
Location
TTH: bilateral pressing band. Migraine: typically one-sided. Cluster: strictly around one eye or temple — always the same side.
Location
💢
Severity
TTH: mild to moderate pressing pain. Migraine: moderate to severe pulsating. Cluster: severe to very severe — rated among the most painful conditions known.
Severity
⏱️
Duration
TTH: 30 minutes to 7 days. Migraine: 4 to 72 hours. Cluster: 15 minutes to 3 hours — brief duration rules out oral painkillers.
Duration
What distinguishes the three types · 2 of 2
🤢
Associated features
TTH: none typically. Migraine: nausea, light sensitivity, sound sensitivity. Cluster: same-side tearing, nasal congestion, and drooping eyelid during attacks.
Features
🏃
Behaviour during attack
TTH: unaffected by routine activity. Migraine: worsened by activity — patient prefers to lie still. Cluster: agitated, restless, unable to keep still.
Behaviour
⚠️
Medication overuse headache
Daily headache caused by overusing acute treatments — more than 10 days per month for triptans or opioids, or 15 for painkillers.
>10 days/mo
Treatment per headache type · 1 of 2
💊
TTH — simple analgesia
Simple over-the-counter analgesics — effective and appropriate for tension-type headache. Responds well to standard pain relief taken at onset of the headache.
Analgesia
🚫
TTH — not triptans
Triptans are migraine-specific — not effective for tension-type headache. Using them for TTH also risks developing medication overuse headache.
No triptans
🎯
Migraine — triptan + painkiller, early
NICE first-line triptan combined with an anti-inflammatory painkiller — treat at attack onset. Combination is clinically superior to either treatment alone.
Early + combo
Treatment per headache type · 2 of 2
💨
Cluster — high-flow oxygen
High-flow oxygen via mask for 15–20 minutes. Level A evidence, no systemic effects — the only primary headache type treated with oxygen.
O₂ first
💉
Cluster — subcutaneous injection
Subcutaneous triptan injection as first-line alongside oxygen — oral treatments are too slow for attacks lasting as little as 15 minutes.
SC injection
🔄
MOH — withdraw the drug
Stopping the overused medication is the recognised treatment. Headache initially worsens before improving — around half achieve meaningful improvement within two months.
Withdraw
Research · cluster treatment & TTH global burden
Key Finding
74%
headache response within 15 minutes / subcutaneous injection vs 26% placebo · cluster headache
49 patients · randomised crossover · NEJM 1991
Subcutaneous injection for cluster headache
Sumatriptan Cluster Headache Study Group · N Engl J Med · 1991;325:322–326
74% of cluster attacks responded within 15 minutes vs 26% on placebo. 46% were completely pain-free. Established subcutaneous injection as first-line acute treatment for cluster headache.

Global burden — headache disorders
GBD 2016 Headache Collaborators · Lancet Neurol · 2018;17:954–976
1.89 billion people affected — the most prevalent neurological condition worldwide. Despite high prevalence, individual TTH attacks are less disabling than migraine, but cumulative burden on healthcare is substantial.
Patterns clinically recognised as requiring prompt assessment · 1 of 2
⚡
Thunderclap headache
Reaches maximum intensity within seconds — clinically recognised as time-sensitive to exclude bleeding around the brain. 111 and urgent GP services exist for this.
Urgent
👴
New headache over age 50
Particularly if progressive, or with scalp tenderness or jaw pain on chewing — warrants assessment to exclude giant cell arteritis, a vasculitic condition.
Assess
🌡️
Fever, neck stiffness ± rash
A pattern clinically recognised as time-sensitive — 111 and urgent GP services exist for this presentation. May indicate meningitis or other serious infection.
Time-sensitive
Patterns requiring prompt assessment · 2 of 2
🧠
New focal neurological deficit
New weakness, speech difficulty, or visual loss not consistent with migraine aura — clinically recognised as requiring urgent assessment.
Urgent
💪
Worsened by exertion or straining
Headache worsened by coughing, straining, or bearing down may indicate raised pressure inside the skull — warrants investigation to exclude structural cause.
Investigate
🔬
Known cancer or immunosuppression
New or changing headache in someone with known cancer, HIV, or who is immunosuppressed warrants a lower threshold for investigation and assessment.
Lower threshold
