▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — Three stages
So what is diverticular disease? It starts with diverticula — small pouches that bulge outward through weak spots in the wall of the large bowel. They're very common, especially in older adults.
— pause —
There are three stages to understand. First — diverticulosis. That's when you have diverticula but no symptoms at all. About 75% of people with diverticula are in this category. They're often found incidentally on a scan done for something else, and the answer is: do nothing. No treatment, no diet changes, nothing to worry about.
— pause —
Second is diverticular disease — when the pouches start causing symptoms. Lower left abdominal pain, bloating, changes in bowel habit. That's managed with lifestyle changes. And third is diverticulitis — when the pouches become infected or inflamed. That's the more serious end of the spectrum.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Risk factors
Why does it develop? It's strongly linked to a low-fibre Western diet. Diverticular disease is rare in rural sub-Saharan Africa and parts of Asia where fibre intake is high — and it was virtually unknown before the 20th century. The average UK adult eats about 18 grams of fibre per day. The recommended level is 30 grams.
— pause —
Other risk factors include age — it becomes much more common after 50 — obesity, low physical activity, and smoking. And there's something important to know about medications: regular use of ibuprofen and similar anti-inflammatory drugs — what we call NSAIDs — is clinically recognised as increasing the risk of bowel perforation in people with diverticular disease. That's worth knowing, because these are very commonly taken drugs.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — Seeds and nuts myth
Now I want to address something that many people with diverticular disease have been told — and it turns out to be wrong.
— pause —
For decades, the standard advice was to avoid seeds, nuts, popcorn, and fruit skins. The theory was that these small particles might get trapped inside diverticula and cause infection. It sounds plausible — but there was never any evidence behind it.
— pause —
In 2019, NICE — the body that sets clinical guidelines in England — explicitly reversed this advice. There is no need to avoid seeds, nuts, or fruit skins. Not only is there no evidence they cause diverticulitis — avoiding them may actually reduce fibre intake, which is counterproductive. These foods are a useful source of the very thing that helps protect the bowel.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 5 — Doctor's toolkit
So what happens in practice? For asymptomatic diverticulosis — reassurance and no treatment. For symptomatic diverticular disease, the focus is on lifestyle: gradually increasing dietary fibre to 30 grams per day, adequate fluids, regular exercise, stopping smoking.
— pause —
For pain, paracetamol and antispasmodics are the recommended options. NSAIDs and opioid painkillers are clinically recognised as increasing the risk of bowel perforation — which is why NICE identifies them as drugs to use with caution in this condition. And here's another significant change from previous practice: antibiotics are no longer routinely recommended for uncomplicated diverticulitis. Multiple randomised trials showed they don't reduce complications or speed recovery in uncomplicated cases. That's a meaningful shift.
— pause —
For more serious presentations — suspected abscess, or signs of peritonitis — a CT scan is the investigation of choice, and hospital assessment is the recognised clinical pathway.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 6 — The evidence
What does the evidence actually show? There are two important studies.
— pause —
First, a 2020 meta-analysis pooling five prospective cohort studies involving 865,000 participants found a clear dose-response relationship between dietary fibre and diverticular disease risk. At 30 grams per day — the UK recommended intake — risk was 41% lower compared to very low intake. At 40 grams, it was 58% lower. The relationship was consistent and there was no evidence of bias.
— pause —
Second, the EPIC-Oxford cohort — a large UK study of 47,000 people in England and Scotland — found that vegetarians had a 31% lower risk of diverticular disease compared to meat eaters. After accounting for other factors, the main driver was fibre intake. Those with the highest fibre consumption had 41% lower risk than those with the lowest.
