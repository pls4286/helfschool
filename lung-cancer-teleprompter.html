Slide 2 — Myth vs Evidence

Before we get into the science, I want to tackle two myths I see regularly on social media.

First: that lung cancer only happens to smokers. It doesn't. Around fifteen per cent of UK lung cancer cases occur in people who have never smoked. Radon gas, asbestos, air pollution, and genetics all play a role.

Second: that stage four lung cancer is untreatable — that there's nothing medicine can offer. The evidence says otherwise, and we'll come to that in the research section.

Slides 3–4 — What is lung cancer?

Lung cancer starts in the cells lining the airways or lung tissue. There are two main types. Non-small cell lung cancer — NSCLC — accounts for around eighty to eighty-five per cent of cases. Small cell lung cancer accounts for the rest and tends to spread faster.

On risk: seventy-two per cent of UK cases are linked to smoking — the single biggest factor by some distance. But as I said, fifteen per cent occur in people who've never smoked.

Something important has changed in recent years. Every NSCLC tumour is now tested for specific molecular markers — EGFR mutations, PD-L1 levels, and others. Those results determine which treatment has the best chance of working — and that has changed the outlook considerably for some patients.

Slide 5 — Why does it matter?

The scale is stark. Around fifty thousand new cases a year. Ninety deaths every day.

But here is the number I want you to hold onto. Stage one five-year survival is almost sixty-five per cent. The overall ten-year figure is eleven per cent. That gap exists almost entirely because of when the cancer is found.

This is not a disease where the statistics are fixed. They are heavily dependent on how early it is caught.

Slides 6–7 — What your doctor might discuss

The recognised red flag symptoms: a persistent cough that has been there for three weeks or more — new or changing in character. Coughing up blood. New breathlessness. Chest or shoulder pain. Unexplained weight loss. Hoarseness.

Investigation starts with a chest X-ray, then CT scanning, then a tissue biopsy to confirm the diagnosis and carry out molecular testing.

Treatment has changed substantially. For early-stage disease, surgery is often first choice. For tumours with high PD-L1 expression — around a quarter of advanced NSCLC cases — immunotherapy with pembrolizumab, known as Keytruda, is now a standard first-line option. And for EGFR-mutated tumours — ten to fifteen per cent of cases — targeted drugs like osimertinib, known as Tagrisso, have meaningfully extended survival.

Slide 8 — What the research shows (1 of 2)

The NELSON trial randomised nearly sixteen thousand long-term heavy smokers to CT screening or no screening. After ten years, the screened group had twenty-four per cent lower lung cancer mortality. That trial is the evidence behind the NHS targeted lung cancer screening programme now being rolled out in England.

The KEYNOTE-024 trial compared pembrolizumab — Keytruda — with standard chemotherapy in advanced NSCLC with high PD-L1 expression. Progression-free survival: ten point three months versus six months. Overall survival was also significantly better.

Slide 9 — What the research shows (2 of 2)

The FLAURA trial looked at EGFR-mutated advanced lung cancer. Osimertinib — Tagrisso — delivered a median overall survival of thirty-eight point six months, versus thirty-one point eight months on standard EGFR drugs.

In a disease where the prognosis was historically poor, those are meaningful gains — and for the patients who qualify, they matter enormously.

Slide 10 — The one thing

So what is the one thing the evidence is clear about? Earlier detection changes outcomes dramatically.

The recognised red flag symptoms to be aware of: a persistent cough, blood when coughing, unexplained breathlessness or chest pain. The NHS Targeted Lung Health Check is available in many areas for long-term smokers aged forty and over. And the evidence consistently shows that stopping smoking reduces lung cancer risk — at any age.
