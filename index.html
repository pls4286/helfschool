In over twenty years as an NHS GP, I learned that the most important thing a doctor can do is truly listen — and then try to help.

My name is Dr Paul. I have been happily married for 38 years and am the proud father of four now-adult children. For over two decades I served patients across the NHS — conducting thousands of consultations and caring deeply for the people who trusted me with their health.

Throughout my career, I believed that good medicine begins with listening — really listening — to everything a patient has to say, before reaching for a diagnosis or a prescription. A patient who feels heard is a patient who can be helped. That philosophy shaped every consultation I ever had.

But by 2018, the system I worked within was making that increasingly difficult. Appointments grew shorter, pressures greater, and I found myself unable to give patients the time and care they deserved. I felt like a square peg in a round hole — and eventually, reluctantly, the stress took its toll. At the end of 2019 I was Tier One ill health retired by the Chief Medical Officer for the NHS.

helf.school is my way of continuing to listen — and to help. Because an informed patient is an empowered one. When you understand your condition, the evidence behind your treatment, and the reasoning your doctor is using, you can participate more fully in your own care. You can ask better questions. You can make the most of the time you have together. That's what this is all about.

Condition Explainer
Atrial Fibrillation (AF): What is it and what does it mean for you?
🩺 Written by Dr [Name], Retired GP 📅 Last reviewed: March 2025 ⏱ 8 min read
What is it, in plain terms?

Think of your heart as a pump with an electrical system that tells it when to beat. In atrial fibrillation, that electrical system sends chaotic signals — instead of a steady, rhythmic beat, the upper chambers of your heart quiver irregularly. Most people describe it as a fluttering or "flip-flopping" sensation in their chest.

Why does it matter?
AF itself is rarely immediately dangerous, but it increases stroke risk — because irregular pumping can allow blood to pool and clot
It affects around 1 in 40 adults in the UK — you are far from alone
Many people live full, active lives with AF, particularly with good management
Some people have no symptoms at all and discover it incidentally
What your doctor might do

Diagnosis is usually confirmed with an ECG (a simple, painless heart tracing). Management depends on your individual situation — this might include rate or rhythm control medications, blood thinners to reduce stroke risk, or in some cases a procedure called cardioversion or ablation. Your doctor will weigh these options with you.

📚 Sources: NICE Guidelines NG196 · ESC 2020 AF Guidelines · BMJ 2021

Research Summary
Does daily aspirin really prevent heart attacks? What the evidence actually shows
🔬 Research Analysis 📅 Updated: January 2025 ⏱ 6 min read
The old thinking

For decades, low-dose aspirin was widely recommended for primary prevention of heart attacks — meaning for people who hadn't yet had a heart attack, as a preventative measure. Millions of people were advised to take it daily.

What the research now shows
The ASPREE trial (2018, 19,000 participants) found aspirin did NOT significantly reduce heart events in healthy older adults — but did increase bleeding risk
Guidelines have since changed: aspirin is no longer routinely recommended for primary prevention in most people
It remains beneficial for people who have already had a heart attack or stroke (secondary prevention)
Strength of evidence
Evidence quality:
Strong

Multiple large randomised controlled trials with consistent findings. Guidelines from major bodies align. This is well-established science.

📚 ASPREE Trial (NEJM 2018) · ARRIVE Trial · ACC/AHA 2022

Our Approach

How we select our research

The articles on helf.school are informed by published medical research, but we don't claim to cover every study ever written on a topic. Medical literature is vast, and for many conditions there are hundreds — sometimes thousands — of published papers.

What we focus on instead are the trials and reviews that matter most clinically — large, well-designed studies published in recognised medical journals, major clinical guidelines from bodies such as NICE and the WHO, and systematic reviews that draw together the best available evidence.

There will always be smaller or more specialist studies we haven't referenced. That's not an oversight — it's a deliberate choice to keep the information clear, trustworthy, and readable. If you want to explore the full evidence base for any condition, the sources we cite are a good starting point, and databases such as PubMed give access to the wider literature.

Our approved sources include:
NICE Guidelines BMJ The Lancet NEJM JAMA Cochrane Database WHO NHS / NHS Digital ONS CDC PubMed BMJ Best Practice
