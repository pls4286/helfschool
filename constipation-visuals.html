Constipation
What the evidence shows about causes, fibre, laxatives, and getting things moving.

Most cases are functional — no clear structural cause found. Lifestyle, medications, and toileting habits are the most common drivers.
What drives constipation — main causes
1 of 2
🌾
Low dietary fibre
Under 25–30g/day reduces stool bulk and slows colonic transit. Most UK adults consume well below the recommended intake — average estimated at around 18g/day.
<20g/day
💧
Insufficient fluid intake
Dehydration concentrates stool and slows transit. Fluid is essential for fibre to function — without adequate hydration, even a high-fibre diet may not improve bowel frequency.
↑ Hardness
🛋️
Physical inactivity
Sedentary behaviour is independently associated with slower colonic transit. Gut motility responds directly to movement — exercise is one of the few lifestyle measures with consistent evidence for improvement.
↓ Motility
What drives constipation — medications & habits
2 of 2
💊
Constipating medications
Opioids, iron supplements, tricyclic antidepressants, and calcium-based antacids are among the most common pharmaceutical causes. Reviewing current medications is a clinically important early step.
60+ drugs
🚽
Ignoring the urge
Repeatedly suppressing the defecation reflex raises the threshold over time — the urge becomes progressively weaker and less reliable. Acting promptly preserves the reflex.
↑ Threshold
🩺
Underlying conditions
Hypothyroidism, diabetes, IBS-C, pelvic floor dysfunction, and neurological conditions present with constipation as a key feature. Identifying the underlying cause is more effective than treating the symptom indefinitely.
Check cause
What the evidence supports
1 of 2 — diet, fluids, movement
🌾
Increase dietary fibre
25–30g daily is the evidence-based target — vegetables, fruit, wholegrains, and legumes. Increase gradually over 2–3 weeks to allow gut bacteria to adapt and minimise bloating.
25–30g
💧
Adequate fluid intake
1.5–2 litres daily maintains stool hydration and is essential for fibre supplementation to be effective. Dehydration causes the colon to absorb more water from stool, progressively hardening it.
1.5–2L
🚶
Regular movement
Moderate daily activity stimulates colonic motility through the enteric nervous system — transit time responds directly to movement. Even 20–30 minutes of walking regularly can improve bowel habit.
↑ Transit
What the evidence supports
2 of 2 — position, laxative, habit
🪑
Footstool toileting position
Raising feet 20–30 cm mimics natural squatting — relaxes the puborectalis muscle and straightens the anorectal angle. Eases passage and reduces the need for straining.
Footstool
💊
First-line laxative — macrogol
Macrogol (sold as Movicol or Laxido) is the evidence-based first-line laxative for adults in UK clinical practice — preferred over lactulose, which is less effective and causes more bloating.
Macrogol
📅
Respond to the urge
Acting promptly when the urge arrives maintains the defecation reflex — repeated delay weakens the signal over time. The urge typically lasts only a short window before it subsides.
Act on urge
What the evidence shows — laxatives & fibre supplements
Key Finding
4/4
outcomes macrogol superior / vs lactulose on stool frequency, consistency, straining, rescue use
Cochrane review · 10 RCTs · underpins NICE CKS first-line guidance
Macrogol vs lactulose — Cochrane systematic review
Lee-Robichaud et al · Cochrane Database Syst Rev · 2010 · CD007570 ✅
Macrogol superior to lactulose across all four primary outcomes — stool frequency, consistency, straining, and rescue laxative use. Evidence underpins NICE CKS first-line guidance for adult constipation.
Key Finding
77%
response rate with fibre supplements / vs 44% on placebo
RR 1.71 (CI 1.20–2.42) · psyllium & ispaghula husk · ⚑ verify PMID
Fibre supplements in functional constipation — meta-analysis
Christodoulides et al · Aliment Pharmacol Ther · 2016 ⚑ verify PMID before upload
Fibre supplements — psyllium and ispaghula husk (Fybogel) — produced a 77% response rate versus 44% on placebo. Evidence applies to supplements specifically, not to increasing whole-food dietary fibre.
How these changes work in the colon
1 of 2
🌾
Dietary fibre
Adds bulk to stool and retains water in the large intestine — reducing transit time and softening passage. Both insoluble fibre (bulk) and soluble fibre (water retention) contribute.
→ ↑ Bulk, ↓ Transit
💧
Fluid intake
Adequate hydration maintains stool water content — the colon reabsorbs water when intake is low, hardening stool progressively. Sufficient fluid intake prevents this concentration effect.
→ Stool softness
🚶
Physical activity
Movement stimulates the enteric nervous system — peristaltic activity in the colon is directly sensitive to physical activity levels. The mechanism is well established even if optimal dose remains under study.
→ ↑ Peristalsis
How these changes work in the colon
2 of 2
🪑
Footstool position
Raising feet to 20–30 cm creates an anorectal angle of approximately 35°, relaxing the puborectalis muscle — enabling more complete rectal emptying with significantly less straining effort.
→ ↓ Straining
💊
Macrogol (osmotic laxative)
Draws water into the colon by osmosis — softening stool and increasing luminal volume, stimulating the propulsive reflex. Not absorbed systemically; safe for regular use in most adults.
→ Osmotic action
🩺
Treating the underlying cause
Correcting hypothyroidism, reviewing constipating medications, or addressing pelvic floor dysfunction removes the driver — not just the symptom. This is more effective than treating the symptom indefinitely.
→ Root cause
