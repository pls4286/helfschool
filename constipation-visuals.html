Most cases are functional — no clear structural cause is found. Lifestyle, medications, and toileting habits are the most common drivers.
What drives constipation
🌾
Low dietary fibre
Under 25–30g/day reduces stool bulk and slows colonic transit — most UK adults consume well below the recommended amount.
<20g/day
💧
Insufficient fluid intake
Dehydration concentrates stool and slows transit — fluid is essential for fibre to function and maintain stool consistency.
↑ Hardness
🛋️
Physical inactivity
Sedentary behaviour is independently associated with slower colonic transit — gut motility responds directly to movement.
↓ Motility
💊
Constipating medications
Opioids, iron supplements, tricyclic antidepressants, and calcium-based antacids are among the most common pharmaceutical causes.
60+ drugs
🚽
Ignoring the urge
Repeatedly suppressing the defecation reflex raises the threshold over time — the urge becomes weaker and less reliable.
↑ Threshold
🩺
Underlying conditions
Hypothyroidism, diabetes, IBS-C, pelvic floor dysfunction, and several neurological conditions present with constipation as a key feature.
Check cause
What the evidence supports
🌾
Increase dietary fibre
25–30g daily is the evidence-based target — vegetables, fruit, wholegrains, legumes; increased gradually to avoid bloating.
25–30g
💧
Adequate fluid intake
1.5–2 litres daily maintains stool hydration and is essential for fibre supplementation to be effective.
1.5–2L
🚶
Regular movement
Moderate daily activity stimulates colonic motility through the enteric nervous system — transit time responds to movement.
↑ Transit
🪑
Footstool toileting position
Raising feet 20–30 cm mimics natural squatting — relaxes the puborectalis muscle and eases passage with less straining.
Footstool
💊
First-line laxative: macrogol
Macrogol (Movicol, Laxido) is the evidence-based first-line laxative for adults in UK clinical practice — preferred over lactulose.
Macrogol
📅
Respond to the urge
Acting promptly when the urge arrives maintains the defecation reflex — repeated delay weakens the signal over time.
Act on urge

Macrogol vs lactulose — Cochrane review
Lee-Robichaud et al · Cochrane · 2010 · CD007570
Systematic review finding macrogol superior to lactulose across all four primary outcomes — stool frequency per week, stool consistency, straining, and need for additional laxative use. This evidence underpins NICE CKS first-line guidance for adult constipation.
Key Finding
77%
response rate · fibre supplement
vs 44% placebo · RR 1.71 (CI 1.20–2.42) · psyllium / ispaghula husk
Fibre supplementation in functional constipation
Christodoulides et al · Aliment Pharmacol Ther · 2016
Meta-analysis demonstrating that fibre supplements — primarily psyllium and ispaghula husk (Fybogel) — produced a 77% response rate versus 44% on placebo. This evidence applies specifically to fibre supplements, not to increasing whole-food dietary fibre, for which RCT evidence is more limited.
How these changes work in the colon
🌾
Dietary fibre
Adds bulk and retains water in the stool — reduces transit time and softens passage through the large intestine.
→ ↑ Bulk, ↓ Transit
💧
Fluid intake
Adequate hydration maintains stool water content — the colon reabsorbs water when intake is low, hardening stool progressively.
→ Stool softness
🚶
Physical activity
Movement stimulates the enteric nervous system — peristaltic activity in the colon is directly sensitive to physical activity levels.
→ ↑ Peristalsis
🪑
Footstool position
Raises the anorectal angle to approximately 35°, relaxing the puborectalis muscle — enables more complete emptying with less straining.
→ ↓ Straining
💊
Macrogol (osmotic laxative)
Draws water into the colon by osmosis — softens stool and increases luminal volume, stimulating the propulsive reflex without systemic absorption.
→ Osmotic action
🩺
Treating the underlying cause
Correcting hypothyroidism, reviewing constipating medications, or addressing pelvic floor dysfunction removes the driver — not just the symptom.
→ Root cause
