Knowing what is normal for your own body — and noticing when something changes.

What is it?
Breast awareness — the clinical approach
Knowing your own normal — not following a prescribed routine
🔍
Knowing your own normal
Breast awareness describes familiarity with how your own body looks and feels — so that anything new or different becomes noticeable against that personal baseline.
📅
No prescribed schedule or technique
Unlike formal monthly self-examination, breast awareness is not tied to a specific date, time, or technique. Clinical guidance emphasises familiarity, not calendar adherence.
🌿
Relevant for everyone with breast tissue
Breast cancer can develop in anyone with breast tissue — women and men alike. Around 420 men are diagnosed in the UK each year. The principles apply to all.
What does normal feel like?
Normal varies — and changes over time
There is no single standard — what matters is knowing your own
🔄
Cyclical changes are normal
Breast tissue commonly becomes more tender or lumpier before a period — a recognised normal hormonal response.
⚖️
Asymmetry and nodularity are common
It is clinically normal for one breast to differ slightly from the other. Breast tissue can feel dense or nodular — this reflects normal composition, not a problem.
🕐
Breasts change with age and menopause
After the menopause — when oestrogen production falls — breast tissue often feels softer and less dense. This is part of normal ageing. Your normal is the benchmark.
Changes worth assessment · 1 of 2
Changes that are new and persistent
Most have a benign explanation — assessment establishes what they are
❗
A new lump or area of thickening
In the breast, upper chest, or armpit — particularly one that feels harder or different from the surrounding tissue, or that does not change with the menstrual cycle.
📐
A change in size, shape, or outline
Including any new difference between the two sides that was not there before — such as one breast appearing higher, lower, or a changed visible silhouette or contour.
👁️
A change in skin texture
Dimpling, puckering, a rash on the breast or nipple area, or skin developing an orange-peel surface — called peau d'orange — are clinically recognised as worth assessment.
Changes worth assessment · 2 of 2
Further changes to be aware of
New, persistent changes different from your established normal
🔴
Changes to a nipple
New inversion (a nipple turning inward that previously pointed outward), discharge not related to breastfeeding — especially if bloodstained — or a change in nipple position or appearance.
📍
Persistent localised pain
Cyclical breast pain linked to periods is common and not associated with cancer. Persistent, non-cyclical pain in one specific area that does not resolve is the type worth assessment.
🌡️
Redness, warmth, or unexplained swelling
Particularly without a known infection or injury. Some breast cancers present with these signs rather than a detectable lump — making full awareness of the range important.
The NHS Breast Screening Programme
Screening and awareness — complementary, not alternatives
They detect different things at different points in time
🏥
Ages 50 to 70 — every 3 years
The NHS routinely invites eligible people aged 50 to 70 for mammography every 3 years. Invitations are sent automatically. Those over 70 can request continued screening.
🔬
What mammography detects
Mammography — an X-ray of breast tissue — identifies changes too small to feel. Its clinical value is finding early-stage changes before they cause noticeable symptoms.
📆
Awareness between appointments
Routine screening takes place every 3 years. Breast awareness means changes developing between appointments — lumps, skin changes, nipple changes — are more likely to be noticed.

NICE NG101 — why awareness replaced scheduled self-examination

Large randomised trials found no reduction in breast cancer mortality from formal monthly self-examination. NICE NG101 does not recommend formal BSE. The clinically described approach is breast awareness — knowing what is individually normal and noticing change — without a prescribed schedule or technique.

NICE. Early and locally advanced breast cancer: diagnosis and management. NG101. Updated April 2025.
