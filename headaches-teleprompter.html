▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — Big picture
Headache is one of the most common reasons people consult their GP. The vast majority are primary headaches — the headache is the condition itself, with no underlying structural cause.
— pause —
Tension-type headache affects around 26% of the global adult population — making it the most prevalent neurological condition worldwide. Cluster headache affects just 0.1% — but it's one of the most severe pain conditions known. And medication overuse headache affects 1 to 2% of the population — often unrecognised, caused by the very treatments people are using to manage their headaches.
— pause —
Here's the clinically important point: these three types require fundamentally different treatments. Getting the type wrong means the treatment won't work. That's what this article is about.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — What distinguishes the types
So — the three types. Tension-type headache is the most common. The pain is bilateral — a tight band or pressing feeling around the head — mild to moderate, and not worsened by routine activity. It responds to simple analgesia: aspirin, ibuprofen, paracetamol.
— pause —
Migraine is typically unilateral, pulsating, moderate to severe, worsened by activity, and accompanied by nausea or sensitivity to light and sound. Triptans — sumatriptan, sold as Imigran — combined with an NSAID is the NICE first-line approach. The key point: treat early in the attack. Efficacy drops if treatment is delayed.
— pause —
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — Treatment per type
Cluster headache — rare but severe. Attacks are strictly unilateral, centred around one eye, lasting 15 minutes to 3 hours, and occurring up to 8 times a day. The patient is agitated, pacing — which distinguishes it from migraine. Treatment is 100% oxygen at high flow rate, or subcutaneous sumatriptan 6mg by injection. Oral analgesics are too slow for attacks this brief.
— pause —
And medication overuse headache — daily or near-daily headache caused by taking acute treatments too frequently. More than 10 days a month for triptans or opioids, more than 15 days for simple analgesics. The recognised treatment is withdrawal of the overused drug — headache worsens initially before improving.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 5 — Research cards
On the evidence. The sumatriptan cluster headache trial — published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 1991 — tested subcutaneous sumatriptan in 49 patients with cluster headache. Sumatriptan produced a 74% headache response within 15 minutes, compared to 26% on placebo. That's the bedrock trial establishing subcutaneous sumatriptan as first-line acute treatment for cluster headache.
— pause —
On the global burden of tension-type headache — the GBD 2016 headache analysis, published in Lancet Neurology, estimated approximately 1.89 billion people worldwide had tension-type headache. The most prevalent neurological condition on the planet. Despite this, it generates proportionally less disability than migraine, because individual attacks are less severe. But the overall burden on healthcare and quality of life is substantial.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 6 — Red flags
Most headaches are primary and benign. Fewer than 1 in 1,000 people under 50 presenting with an isolated headache will have a brain tumour. Red flags are what appropriately drive investigation — not frequency or severity alone.
— pause —
The key patterns clinically recognised as requiring prompt assessment: thunderclap headache — reaching maximum intensity within seconds — is time-sensitive to exclude bleeding around the brain. New headache in someone over 50, particularly with scalp tenderness. Fever, neck stiffness, and rash — 111 and urgent GP services exist for this pattern. New focal neurological signs. Headache worsened by coughing or straining. And new headache in someone with known cancer or immunosuppression.
