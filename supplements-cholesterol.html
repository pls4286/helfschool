Do Supplements Lower Cholesterol?

What the evidence actually shows about fish oil, plant sterols, red yeast rice, and other popular supplements

🕐 9 min read 📚 Evidence-based ✍️ helf.school
1

What is it?

Walk into any pharmacy or health food shop in the UK and you'll find an entire aisle of supplements promising to support cholesterol, protect your heart, or promote healthy lipid levels. Fish oil capsules, plant sterol drinks, red yeast rice tablets, garlic extract, niacin — the options can feel overwhelming. And they're not cheap.

Millions of people take these products every year, often hoping to avoid or reduce prescription medication, or simply to do something extra for their heart health. The UK supplement market is worth billions of pounds annually. But the critical question is: what does the scientific evidence actually say?

The honest answer is nuanced. Some supplements have genuinely meaningful evidence behind them. Others have been repeatedly shown to perform no better than a placebo in rigorous trials. And a small number carry real safety concerns that are widely underappreciated.

This article works through the main cholesterol-related supplements one by one — looking at what they are, what effect they actually have on cholesterol numbers, and what we know (and don't know) about whether that translates into reduced heart disease risk.

📖 Key Terms

Dietary Supplement

A product taken by mouth that contains a "dietary ingredient" such as a vitamin, mineral, herb, or plant extract. Unlike medicines, supplements are not required to prove they work before being sold.

HDL Cholesterol

Often called "good" cholesterol. HDL helps carry cholesterol away from the arteries and back to the liver. Higher levels are generally associated with lower cardiovascular risk.

LDL Cholesterol

Often called "bad" cholesterol. High LDL levels are a major risk factor for atherosclerosis (artery narrowing) and heart disease. Most cholesterol-lowering treatments target LDL reduction.

Monacolin K

The active compound in red yeast rice. It is chemically identical to lovastatin, a prescription statin drug — meaning it works the same way and carries the same potential side effects.

Phytosterols (Plant Sterols & Stanols)

Naturally occurring plant compounds with a similar structure to cholesterol. They compete with cholesterol for absorption in the gut, reducing how much cholesterol enters the bloodstream.

Placebo

An inactive dummy treatment used in clinical trials. A supplement only "works" in a meaningful sense if it outperforms placebo — many do not in well-controlled studies.

Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT)

The gold standard of clinical evidence. Participants are randomly assigned to receive either a treatment or a placebo, allowing researchers to accurately measure the treatment's true effect.

Triglycerides

A type of fat found in the blood. High triglycerides often accompany high cholesterol and can independently raise cardiovascular risk. Some supplements affect triglycerides without affecting LDL.

2

Why does it matter?

  • Millions of people take cholesterol supplements every year, often spending significant money on products with limited or no proven benefit for reducing heart attack or stroke risk.
  • A landmark 2022 study published in the Journal of the American College of Cardiology found that six of the most popular "heart health" supplements — including fish oil, garlic, cinnamon, and turmeric — performed no better than a placebo at lowering LDL cholesterol over 28 days.
  • Lowering cholesterol numbers on a blood test is not the same as reducing heart attack or stroke risk. Even if a supplement lowers LDL, it may not translate into the clinical benefits that well-studied medicines like statins have demonstrated.
  • Some supplements — particularly red yeast rice — carry real safety risks including muscle damage and liver injury that are often not appreciated by people buying them as "natural" alternatives.
  • Understanding what supplements can and cannot do empowers people to make informed decisions, have better conversations with their GP, and direct their effort and money towards approaches with robust evidence.
3

What your doctor might discuss with you

If you mention cholesterol supplements to your GP, they will likely want to explore why you're considering them — and whether your overall cardiovascular risk has been properly assessed. Here is what the evidence shows for the most commonly used supplements, and what a doctor might say about each one.

🌿

Plant Sterols & Stanols (Phytosterols)

Strongest Evidence

What they are: Plant-derived compounds found naturally in small amounts in vegetable oils, nuts, and grains. Added to products like Benecol and Flora ProActiv drinks, spreads, and yoghurts — and also sold as tablet supplements.

How they work: They compete with cholesterol for absorption in the gut. Because they are structurally similar to cholesterol, they "take its place" in the digestive process, meaning less dietary and bile cholesterol is absorbed into the bloodstream.

The evidence: This is the best-evidenced supplement for LDL reduction. A large meta-analysis of 124 studies found that consuming around 2 grams per day was associated with an LDL reduction of approximately 7–12%. The British Dietetic Association confirms these figures. The effect is real, consistent, and well-replicated across dozens of trials.

The important caveat: Lowering LDL with plant sterols has not been shown to reduce the risk of heart attacks or strokes in large outcome trials. UK guidelines therefore do not currently recommend them specifically for cardiovascular disease prevention — the evidence supports the cholesterol-lowering effect, but the downstream benefit remains unproven. They are not a substitute for prescribed medication in those at high risk.

✓ LDL ↓ 7–12% → Triglycerides: minimal effect → HDL: minimal effect
🍚

Red Yeast Rice

Caution Advised

What it is: Rice fermented with the mould Monascus purpureus. The fermentation produces a compound called monacolin K — which is chemically identical to the prescription statin lovastatin.

How it works: In exactly the same way as a statin — it inhibits the enzyme (HMG-CoA reductase) that the liver uses to produce cholesterol. This is not a "natural" alternative to a statin; it is, in biochemical terms, a statin.

The evidence on cholesterol: Studies have shown LDL reductions in the range of 15–25% with preparations containing sufficient monacolin K. However, the amount of active ingredient varies enormously between products.

Safety concerns — these are significant: Because monacolin K is identical to a prescription drug, it carries the same risk of side effects: muscle pain, muscle breakdown (rhabdomyolysis), and liver damage. In 2025, the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) concluded there is no dose of monacolin K from red yeast rice that can be considered safe. UK regulations (MHRA) may classify products with high monacolin content as unlicensed medicines. Products available online may contain unregulated and unpredictable amounts. NICE does not recommend red yeast rice as an evidence-based cardiovascular strategy.

Regulatory position: In the EU (and followed by the UK), red yeast rice supplements are now restricted to a maximum of 3 mg of monacolins per daily dose — a level at which the cholesterol-lowering effect is significantly reduced. The EU revoked the previous health claim for monacolin K in August 2024.

↓ LDL (if high monacolin content) ⚠ Muscle damage risk ⚠ Liver injury risk
🐟

Omega-3 / Fish Oil

Nuanced

What it is: Supplements containing eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), the omega-3 fatty acids found in oily fish. Widely sold for "heart health."

On cholesterol specifically: Standard fish oil supplements do not lower LDL cholesterol — and at some doses may actually increase it slightly. This is important for people who buy fish oil expecting a cholesterol-lowering benefit.

On triglycerides: Fish oil does meaningfully lower triglycerides. A large 2023 meta-analysis of 90 randomised trials involving over 72,000 participants found a clear, dose-dependent reduction in triglycerides. High-dose prescribed omega-3 preparations (such as icosapent ethyl) are used clinically for this purpose.

On heart attack and stroke risk: This is genuinely complex. Eating oily fish twice a week is associated with lower cardiovascular mortality. Supplement trials give more mixed results. A large 2024 BMJ Medicine observational study found fish oil may increase risk of atrial fibrillation in those without established heart disease, while potentially benefiting those who already have it. The British Heart Foundation does not recommend fish oil supplements for general heart disease prevention. In specific high-risk contexts — particularly very high triglycerides — prescribed omega-3 preparations may be used under medical supervision.

✓ Triglycerides ↓ (dose-dependent) → LDL: no benefit / possible slight ↑ → HDL: modest ↑ in some studies
🧄

Garlic Extract

Weak Evidence

What it is: Concentrated garlic in capsule form, often standardised to contain allicin, thought to be the key active compound. Widely marketed for heart and cholesterol health.

The evidence: The evidence is not encouraging. A well-designed Stanford University trial found that taking raw garlic or garlic supplements almost daily for six months had no meaningful effect on LDL cholesterol levels. The 2022 Journal of the American College of Cardiology study mentioned above found that garlic supplements actually slightly increased LDL compared to placebo over 28 days.

Garlic in food — as part of a Mediterranean-style diet — remains a sensible dietary choice. As an isolated supplement for cholesterol management, the evidence is not there.

→ LDL: no consistent benefit → No meaningful cardiovascular outcome data
💊

Niacin (Vitamin B3)

Not Recommended

What it is: Niacin (nicotinic acid) is a B vitamin that at high doses can affect cholesterol levels. It can raise HDL ("good") cholesterol and lower triglycerides, and was at one point used as a prescribed medication.

The problem: This effect is only seen at prescription doses significantly higher than those available in over-the-counter supplements. At lower doses, it has no meaningful cholesterol effect. At the doses that do have an effect, niacin can cause intense skin flushing and other side effects — and large trials have not shown it to reduce heart attacks or strokes when added to statin therapy.

A newer concern: A 2024 study in Nature Medicine found that excess niacin intake may raise the risk of cardiovascular events through a previously unknown pathway involving inflammation. This finding is important context for anyone considering high-dose niacin supplementation.

→ Supplement doses: no meaningful effect ⚠ High-dose: side effects, cardiovascular concern
🍊

Bergamot & Polyphenol Extracts

Early Evidence

What they are: Extracts from citrus fruits, particularly bergamot (the flavouring in Earl Grey tea), marketed for cholesterol management. Also includes various polyphenol-based supplements.

The evidence: Some studies have shown modest cholesterol-lowering effects. A 2022 network meta-analysis found bergamot was among the more effective of several supplements studied. However, the evidence base remains limited compared to plant sterols or statins — most trials are small and short. This is an area of genuine ongoing research rather than established medicine.

→ LDL: modest reduction in some studies → Outcome trials: insufficient data

⚠️
The "natural" label is not the same as "safe"

Red yeast rice is often chosen as a "natural" alternative to statins. But the active compound, monacolin K, is chemically identical to the prescription statin lovastatin. The risks — including serious muscle breakdown and liver damage — are the same. The key difference is that supplements are not regulated in the same way as medicines, so you cannot be certain how much active ingredient you are actually getting.

4

What the research shows

What might your doctor say?

  • Your overall cardiovascular risk matters more than any single number. A GP will usually discuss your full risk profile using a tool like QRISK3 before recommending any cholesterol-lowering strategy.
  • If cholesterol reduction is the goal, the evidence strongly favours statins over supplements for those at meaningful risk. Statins typically reduce LDL by 30–50% and have decades of evidence showing reduced heart attacks and strokes.
  • Plant sterols may be a reasonable addition if you have mildly elevated cholesterol and low overall cardiovascular risk — though they shouldn't replace dietary and lifestyle changes.
  • Red yeast rice should be discussed honestly. If your doctor is aware you're taking it, they'll want to know because it can interact with other medications and carries the same risks as a prescribed statin.
  • Fish oil prescribed at high doses has a specific role in treating very high triglycerides. Over-the-counter supplements for general heart health are not currently recommended by UK guidelines.
  • Always tell your GP and pharmacist about any supplements you take — they can interact with prescription medicines in ways that may be harmful.

The "Six Supplements" Trial (2022) ⚑

Researchers at Cleveland Clinic randomly assigned 190 adults aged 40–75 to receive either a low-dose statin (rosuvastatin 5 mg), a placebo, or one of six popular supplements — fish oil, cinnamon, garlic, turmeric, plant sterols, or red yeast rice — for 28 days. The statin reduced LDL by an average of 37.9%. All six supplements performed no differently from placebo on LDL reduction. Garlic actually slightly increased LDL versus placebo.

Laffin LJ, Bruemmer D, Garcia M, et al. Comparative Effects of Low-Dose Rosuvastatin, Placebo, and Dietary Supplements on Lipids and Inflammatory Biomarkers. Journal of the American College of Cardiology. 2023;81(1):1–12. doi:10.1016/j.jacc.2022.10.013

JACC — Full text PubMed

Plant Sterols — the 124-Study Meta-Analysis ⚑

A large meta-analysis examining 124 randomised controlled trials found that consuming 0.6–3.3 g per day of plant sterols or stanols reduced LDL cholesterol by an average of 6–12%, with the effect plateauing above 3 g per day. The British Dietetic Association cites 2 g per day as associated with a 7.5–12% LDL reduction. Importantly, no large trial has yet demonstrated that this LDL reduction translates into fewer heart attacks or strokes.

Ras RT, Geleijnse JM, Trautwein EA. LDL-cholesterol-lowering effect of plant sterols and stanols across different dose ranges: a meta-analysis of randomised controlled studies. British Journal of Nutrition. 2014;112(2):214–219. doi:10.1017/S0007114514000750

PubMed British Journal of Nutrition — Full text

EFSA Safety Opinion on Red Yeast Rice (2025) ⚑

In 2025, the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) concluded that there is no dose of monacolin K from red yeast rice that can be considered safe as a food supplement. Individual cases of serious adverse reactions — including rhabdomyolysis (severe muscle breakdown) and acute liver injury — have been reported at doses as low as 3 mg per day. The EU revoked the health claim for monacolin K in August 2024. UK regulations (MHRA) may treat products with significant monacolin content as unlicensed medicines.

EFSA Panel on Nutrition, Novel Foods and Food Allergens (NDA). Scientific Opinion on additional scientific data related to the safety of monacolins from red yeast rice. EFSA Journal. 2025;23(3):e9276. doi:10.2903/j.efsa.2025.9276

EFSA Journal — Full text PubMed Central

Omega-3 and Triglycerides — 90-Trial Meta-Analysis (2023) ⚑

A major meta-analysis published in the Journal of the American Heart Association pooled data from 90 randomised trials involving 72,598 participants. It found that omega-3 supplementation (EPA and DHA) reduced triglycerides in a near-linear dose-dependent fashion — but did not lower LDL cholesterol, and at low doses may modestly increase it. The triglyceride-lowering effect was most pronounced at doses above 2 g per day and in people with elevated triglycerides at baseline. This supports the use of high-dose prescribed omega-3 preparations for hypertriglyceridaemia rather than standard OTC supplements for general cholesterol management.

Wang T, Zhang X, Zhou N, et al. Association Between Omega-3 Fatty Acid Intake and Dyslipidemia: A Continuous Dose-Response Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials. Journal of the American Heart Association. 2023;12(11):e029512. doi:10.1161/JAHA.123.029512

JAHA — Full text PubMed PubMed Central

Fish Oil and Atrial Fibrillation (BMJ Medicine, 2024) ⚑

A large UK Biobank cohort study including over 400,000 adults, published in BMJ Medicine, found that regular use of fish oil supplements was associated with an increased risk of developing atrial fibrillation and stroke in people who had no pre-existing cardiovascular disease, while appearing to reduce the risk of progressing from atrial fibrillation to a more serious heart attack in those already with heart conditions. This is a significant observational finding, though it cannot prove causation, and it adds to the complexity of the fish oil picture.

Chen G, Qian ZM, Zhang J, et al. Regular use of fish oil supplements and course of cardiovascular diseases: prospective cohort study. BMJ Medicine. 2024;3(1):e000451. doi:10.1136/bmjmed-2022-000451

BMJ Medicine — Full text PubMed Central

Statins vs Supplements — the Bigger Picture

Statins — when used appropriately for people at meaningful cardiovascular risk — have one of the most robust evidence bases in all of medicine. Over decades and in trials involving hundreds of thousands of people, they have been shown to substantially reduce heart attacks, strokes, and cardiovascular deaths. No cholesterol-lowering supplement has an evidence base remotely comparable for clinical outcomes. This does not mean supplements have no role — but it does mean the comparison is not equivalent.

National Institute for Health and Care Excellence. Cardiovascular disease: risk assessment and reduction, including lipid modification. NICE Guideline CG181. London: NICE; 2014 (updated 2023).

NICE CG181 — Full guideline
💡
The difference between changing a number and changing an outcome

Plant sterols genuinely lower LDL on a blood test. But lowering LDL is only valuable if it reduces the risk of a heart attack or stroke. For statins, this link has been demonstrated in large, long-term trials. For plant sterol supplements, we have the number change — but not yet the proven outcome benefit. This distinction matters when weighing up the value of a supplement versus prescribed treatment.

Putting it all together

The supplement market is built on a powerful idea: that there must be a gentler, more "natural" path to the same health benefit as prescription medicine. For cholesterol, this idea is appealing — but the evidence tells a more complicated story.

Plant sterols stand out as the supplement with the best evidence for genuinely lowering LDL cholesterol, and they are safe at recommended doses. Fish oil has a clear and real effect on triglycerides, though not on LDL, and the picture on heart disease prevention is genuinely mixed. Red yeast rice is effectively an unregulated statin — with the same mechanism and the same risks, but without the quality controls that apply to licensed medicines.

For other popular supplements — garlic, cinnamon, turmeric, and similar — the evidence does not support their use for cholesterol management, despite their widespread marketing for exactly that purpose.

The hardest truth is that for people at meaningful risk of heart disease, the available evidence strongly favours properly prescribed and monitored medication — alongside the lifestyle changes that have been shown to make a real difference. Supplements can be part of a health-conscious approach to life, but they are not a substitute for understanding your cardiovascular risk and making decisions with your GP based on the full picture.

Knowing what works, what doesn't, and what to watch out for is exactly the kind of evidence-based understanding that puts you in the best position to look after your heart.
