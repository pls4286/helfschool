Dementia is not one disease — it is an umbrella term (1 of 2)
Five main types, each with a distinct cause, pattern and presentation
🧠
Alzheimer's disease — around 65% of cases
Abnormal protein deposits (amyloid plaques and tau tangles) damage nerve cells progressively. Memory for recent events is typically the first symptom.
Most common form · protein deposits are the mechanism
🫀
Vascular dementia — around 20% of cases
Caused by reduced blood supply to the brain — often following stroke or gradual small vessel disease. Can worsen in steps rather than steadily.
Second most common · cardiovascular risk factors matter
👁️
Dementia with Lewy bodies
Protein deposits cause visual hallucinations, Parkinsonism (tremor and stiffness), and fluctuating alertness — a distinctive pattern that is frequently misdiagnosed.
Often confused with Alzheimer's or Parkinson's disease
Dementia is not one disease — it is an umbrella term (2 of 2)
Including the most common form in people under 65 — and an important intermediate stage
💬
Frontotemporal dementia — most common under 65
Affects personality, behaviour and language before memory. Changes are often subtle and initially mistaken for stress or mental health difficulties.
Early onset · language and behaviour affected first
🔀
Mixed dementia — more common than recognised
Two or more types coexisting — most commonly Alzheimer's with vascular dementia. More prevalent in older adults; better identified now with brain imaging.
Increasingly recognised with improved imaging and biomarkers
⚖️
Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) — not dementia
A recognised intermediate stage — measurable cognitive decline not yet affecting daily life. Around 10–15% of people with MCI progress to dementia per year.
MCI is clinically distinct from normal ageing and from dementia

Hearing loss — the single largest modifiable risk factor
Livingston et al, Lancet 2024 · PAR = 9% — more than any other single modifiable factor
9%
of dementia cases linked to untreated hearing loss in midlife
Highest population-attributable fraction of any single modifiable risk factor
🧩
Cognitive load
Struggling to hear forces the brain to work harder — resources that would otherwise support memory, attention and reasoning are diverted to processing sound.
🧠
Brain structure
Reduced auditory input is associated with structural brain changes over time, including accelerated shrinkage in areas important for memory and cognition.
🤝
Social withdrawal
Hearing loss drives social isolation — an independent risk factor in its own right. Hearing aids appear to reduce dementia risk, particularly in those with multiple risk factors.

NICE NG97 — Dementia: assessment, management and support (2018, reviewed 2025)
📋
Cognitive assessment — MoCA, 6-CIT, MMSE
Standardised tests assess memory, attention, language and reasoning — providing a structured, repeatable baseline to track cognitive change over time.
MoCA
6-CIT
🩸
Blood tests — ruling out reversible causes
Thyroid function, B12, folate, glucose, calcium. An underactive thyroid or B12 deficiency can cause significant cognitive slowing — both are entirely treatable.
Reversible
causes
🔬
Brain imaging and memory clinic referral
MRI or CT excludes treatable structural causes and identifies the dementia subtype. Referral to memory services for formal neuropsychological assessment and diagnosis.
MRI · CT
referral
Assessment and management in UK practice (2 of 2)
NICE NG97 · Symptomatic medication and post-diagnosis planning
💊
Medications — two classes for Alzheimer's disease
Cholinesterase inhibitors can modestly slow progression in mild to moderate Alzheimer's. A second drug class is used for more advanced disease. Neither is curative.
Symptomatic
only
📝
Post-diagnosis planning
Lasting power of attorney — a legal document allowing a trusted person to make decisions if capacity is lost. DVLA notification. Care planning. Connection to specialist support services.
LPA
DVLA
🧩
Non-pharmacological support
Cognitive stimulation therapy (CST) is recommended by NICE as a group-based intervention for mild to moderate dementia. Physical activity, social engagement and structured routine all support wellbeing.
CST

Incidence declining
Age-specific dementia rates are falling in high-income countries / evidence prevention is already working
Attributable to improved education · better cardiovascular management · reduced smoking

Age-specific dementia incidence — new cases per 1,000 at a given age — has declined in high-income countries. Better cardiovascular management, reduced smoking, and improved education are already reducing risk.
