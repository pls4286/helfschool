▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — Big picture
Vertigo is one of the most common and treatable conditions in primary care — and the statistics around it are striking. Around 2.4% of people will experience BPPV — the most common form of vertigo — at some point in their lives. Of those, 86% either sought medical help or experienced significant disruption to their daily lives.
— pause —
And yet only 8% received effective treatment. That's the treatment gap this article is about — most people with a highly treatable condition never receive the five-minute procedure that would resolve the problem.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 3–4 — Dizziness vs Vertigo
Dizziness is one of the most common reasons people see their GP — but the word covers several very different experiences, and distinguishing between them really matters.
— pause —
Dizziness is a broad umbrella term. It includes lightheadedness — that feeling you might faint — and a general sense of unsteadiness. But vertigo is something more specific. Vertigo is the distinct sensation that either you or the world around you is spinning or rotating — when no actual movement is occurring.
— pause —
Vertigo also has two main origins. Peripheral vertigo — the most common — comes from the vestibular system, the balance mechanism in the inner ear. Central vertigo is less common but more serious, coming from the brainstem or cerebellum — the part of the brain that co-ordinates balance. There's a bedside clinical test called HINTS that trained doctors use to distinguish between the two — but it requires specific training and isn't available in every surgery. NICE guidance is clear: when it can't be done, refer immediately.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–6 — Main causes
The four main causes of peripheral vertigo are quite different from each other — and duration is the key clue.
— pause —
BPPV — Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo — causes brief, intense spinning lasting seconds, triggered by head position changes: rolling over in bed, looking up, bending forward. Displaced calcium crystals in the inner ear. The most common single cause of vertigo.
— pause —
Vestibular neuritis typically follows a viral illness and causes a single prolonged episode of severe spinning lasting hours to days — without any hearing loss. The body recovers as the brain compensates over weeks.
— pause —
Ménière's disease causes recurring episodes with a characteristic pattern — vertigo, fluctuating hearing loss, ringing in the ears, and a sense of pressure or fullness. Episodes last minutes to hours.
— pause —
Labyrinthitis is similar to vestibular neuritis but involves both balance and hearing — that's the key distinguishing feature. And importantly: all four of these peripheral causes have recognised, effective treatments.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 7 — BPPV mechanism
BPPV is a mechanical problem — and it has a highly effective mechanical solution.
— pause —
The inner ear contains tiny calcium crystals called otoconia — sometimes called ear stones. When they become displaced and migrate into the semicircular canals — the fluid-filled tubes that detect head rotation — they continue moving after the head has stopped. The brain gets a false signal of ongoing movement, and the result is that brief but intense spinning sensation.
— pause —
A clinician can confirm the diagnosis using the Dix-Hallpike test — moving the patient from sitting to lying with the head turned to one side. The treatment is the Epley manoeuvre — a sequence of guided head and body movements that takes about five minutes and repositions the displaced crystals. It's one of the most effective procedures in all of primary care medicine.
— pause —
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 8 — Red flags
Before I get to the research — when is vertigo a time-sensitive presentation?
— pause —
Certain patterns are clinically recognised as potentially indicating a stroke affecting the brainstem or cerebellum. These include: vertigo with a sudden new severe headache or neck stiffness; vertigo with double vision, slurred speech, or difficulty swallowing; new weakness alongside vertigo; sudden complete inability to walk; or spontaneous vertical eye flickering at rest — not triggered by a positional test. These patterns are clinically time-sensitive, and 111 and urgent services exist for them.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 9 — Research: treatment evidence
On the evidence. The Cochrane systematic review of the Epley manoeuvre concluded that it is safe and effective — superior to sham manoeuvres — and produces equivalent results whether it's done in primary care or a specialist clinic. Around 36% of people experience a recurrence within 4 years, but the manoeuvre can simply be repeated.
— pause —
For vestibular neuritis recovery and other inner ear conditions, vestibular rehabilitation — physiotherapy-based graded exercises — has moderate to strong evidence for its efficacy. A Cochrane review of 39 studies covering 2,441 participants found significant improvements in dizziness frequency, activity limitation, and daily life. The exercises work by helping the brain adapt to the changed signals from a damaged vestibular system.
— pause —
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 10 — Research: treatment gap
And then there's the treatment gap. A large population study found that despite 86% of people with BPPV seeking medical help, only 8% received effective treatment. A UK audit traced patients from their first GP visit to eventual treatment and found an average wait of 93 weeks — with 58 of those weeks within primary care alone. These are people with a highly treatable condition, waiting nearly two years for a five-minute procedure.

So — a few things worth holding onto. If your vertigo is brief, triggered by head position changes, and not accompanied by any neurological symptoms, BPPV is the most likely cause — and it's highly treatable. Note how long episodes last, because duration is the clearest clue to the cause.
— pause —
If your vertigo comes with new neurological symptoms — visual changes, speech difficulty, sudden inability to walk — that pattern is clinically time-sensitive and 111 exists for it.
— pause —
Most people living with treatable vertigo have never received the assessment and intervention that would help them. That's exactly the gap that good health education can help to close.
