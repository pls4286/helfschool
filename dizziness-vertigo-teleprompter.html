Segment 2 — Dizziness vs Vertigo (~45 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 2: Dizziness vs Vertigo

Dizziness is one of the most common reasons people see their GP — but the word covers several very different experiences, and distinguishing between them really matters.

Dizziness is a broad umbrella term. It includes lightheadedness — that feeling you might faint — and a general sense of unsteadiness. But vertigo is something more specific. Vertigo is the distinct sensation that either you or the world around you is spinning or rotating — when no actual movement is occurring.

Vertigo also has two main origins. Peripheral vertigo — the most common — comes from the vestibular system, the balance mechanism in the inner ear. Central vertigo is less common but more serious, and comes from the brainstem or cerebellum — the part of the brain at the back of the skull that co-ordinates balance. That distinction shapes everything that follows.

Segment 3 — Main causes (~50 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 3: Main causes

The four main causes of peripheral vertigo are quite different from each other — and duration is the key clue.

BPPV — Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo — causes brief, intense spinning lasting seconds, triggered by head position changes: rolling over in bed, looking up, bending forward. Displaced calcium crystals in the inner ear. The most common single cause of vertigo.

Vestibular neuritis typically follows a viral illness and causes a single prolonged episode of severe spinning lasting hours to days — without any hearing loss. The body recovers as the brain compensates over weeks.

Ménière's disease causes recurring episodes with a characteristic pattern — vertigo, fluctuating hearing loss, ringing in the ears, and a sense of pressure or fullness. Episodes last minutes to hours.

Labyrinthitis is similar to vestibular neuritis but involves both balance and hearing — that's the key distinguishing feature.

Segment 4 — BPPV mechanism and treatment (~55 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 4: BPPV mechanism

BPPV is a mechanical problem — and it has a highly effective mechanical solution.

The inner ear contains tiny calcium crystals called otoconia — sometimes called ear stones. Normally they sit in a specific part of the inner ear. When they become displaced and migrate into the semicircular canals — the fluid-filled tubes that detect head rotation — they continue moving after the head has stopped. The brain gets a false signal of ongoing movement, and the result is that brief but intense spinning sensation.

A GP or physiotherapist can confirm the diagnosis using the Dix-Hallpike test — moving the patient from sitting to lying with the head turned to one side. If it reproduces the vertigo and triggers characteristic eye movements, that's a positive test.

The treatment is the Epley manoeuvre — a sequence of guided head and body movements that takes about five minutes and repositions the displaced crystals. It's one of the most effective procedures in all of primary care medicine.

Segment 5 — Red flags (~40 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 5: Red flags

Before I get to the research, I want to be clear about when vertigo is a time-sensitive presentation.

Certain patterns are clinically recognised as potentially indicating a stroke affecting the brainstem or cerebellum — what doctors call posterior circulation stroke. These include: vertigo with a sudden new severe headache; vertigo with double vision, slurred speech, or difficulty swallowing; sudden complete inability to walk; or spontaneous eye flickering — nystagmus — that happens at rest rather than being triggered by a positional test.

There is a clinical examination called HINTS that trained doctors use to help distinguish inner ear vertigo from a stroke — but it requires specific training and isn't available in every GP surgery. NICE guidance is clear: if the assessment can't be done, refer immediately. 111 and urgent services exist for this pattern.

Segment 6 — Research (~60 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 6: Research — treatment

Now — what does the evidence actually show?

For BPPV, the Cochrane systematic review of the Epley manoeuvre — covering 11 randomised controlled trials and 745 patients — concluded that the Epley manoeuvre is safe and effective, superior to sham manoeuvres, and produces equivalent results whether it's done in primary care or a specialist clinic. Around 36% of people experience a recurrence within 4 years — but the manoeuvre can simply be repeated.

For vestibular neuritis recovery and other inner ear conditions, vestibular rehabilitation — physiotherapy-based graded exercises — has moderate to strong evidence for its efficacy. A Cochrane review of 39 studies covering 2,441 participants found significant improvements in dizziness frequency, activity limitation, and daily life. The exercises work by helping the brain adapt to the changed signals from a damaged vestibular system.

▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 7: Treatment gap

And then there's the treatment gap — which is striking. A large population study found that despite 86% of people with BPPV seeking medical help, only 8% received effective treatment. A UK audit traced 20 patients from their first GP visit to eventual treatment and found an average wait of 93 weeks — with 58 of those weeks within primary care alone. These are people with a highly treatable condition, waiting nearly two years for a five-minute procedure.

If your vertigo comes with new neurological symptoms — visual changes, speech difficulty, sudden inability to walk — that pattern is clinically time-sensitive and 111 exists for it.
