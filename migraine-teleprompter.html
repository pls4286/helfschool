▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — Big picture
Migraine affects around 18.3% of women and 7.6% of men in England in any given year. That makes it one of the most prevalent neurological conditions seen in primary care.
— pause —
But here's what often surprises people: the Global Burden of Disease study ranks migraine as the third highest cause of disability worldwide in adults under 50. Above diabetes. Above stroke. Above dementia in that age group.
— pause —
And yet it is substantially under-diagnosed. Over 40% of people who meet the clinical criteria for migraine have never received a formal diagnosis — many attributing their attacks to sinus problems or tension. That means a large number of people are living with a treatable neurological condition and not getting targeted treatment.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 3–4 — What drives attacks
The triggers for migraine are individual — but some patterns come up consistently. Hormonal fluctuation is the most common trigger in women, particularly around menstruation. Disrupted sleep — too little or too much — is another. Stress, the "let-down" after a period of stress, dehydration, skipped meals, and alcohol — especially red wine — are all recognised.
— pause —
And a clinically important one that's often missed: medication overuse. Using acute pain treatments on more than 10 days a month upregulates central pain pathways and paradoxically makes attacks more frequent. It's called medication overuse headache — and recognising it is an important part of management.
— pause —
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–6 — What the evidence supports
On what helps. The most important practical point for acute attacks is treat early. Acute treatments work best taken at the onset of an attack — waiting until the pain is severe reduces efficacy significantly. The NICE first-line triptan, combined with an anti-inflammatory painkiller, is clinically superior to either alone.
— pause —
For people with four or more attacks a month, preventive treatment is considered. First-line oral options include a beta-blocker, an anticonvulsant, and a low-dose antidepressant — chosen based on the individual's clinical profile. And throughout all of this, a headache diary — recording attack dates, duration, triggers, and medication days — is the clinical foundation that guides treatment decisions.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 7 — Research cards
On the evidence. The landmark meta-analysis of oral triptans — Ferrari and colleagues, published in the Lancet in 2001 — covered 53 randomised controlled trials and over 24,000 patients. For the standard triptan dose tested, 59% of patients achieved a meaningful headache response at 2 hours, and 29% were completely pain-free. All seven oral triptans outperformed placebo. This is the evidence base underpinning triptan prescribing worldwide.
— pause —
On prevention — the STRIVE trial, published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2017, tested a CGRP receptor antibody — a drug developed specifically to block the signalling protein that drives migraine attacks. 50% of patients on the higher dose achieved at least a 50% reduction in their monthly migraine days, compared to 27% on placebo. These were the first drugs developed specifically for migraine prevention, rather than repurposed from other conditions.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 8–9 — Mechanisms
On the science. Migraine begins with a wave of neuronal activity called cortical spreading depression — which generates the aura in those who experience it and initiates the attack cascade. This activates the trigeminovascular system, which releases a signalling protein called CGRP — calcitonin gene-related peptide — dilating blood vessels and sensitising pain pathways around the brain.
— pause —
Triptans work by binding to serotonin receptors, blocking further CGRP release, and constricting the dilated vessels. CGRP antibodies work differently — they prevent the CGRP cascade from starting at all. That's why they're so promising as preventives: they interrupt the underlying mechanism, not just the symptoms.

So — migraine is not just a bad headache. It's a neurological condition with a well-understood biology, a strong evidence base for both acute and preventive treatment, and a newer generation of specifically targeted therapies now available on the NHS.
— pause —
Most people living with migraine have not yet received optimal care — and that's exactly the gap that good health education, and honest conversations with a GP, can help to close.
