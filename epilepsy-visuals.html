Epilepsy — What It Is
and How It's Managed

Around 630,000 people in the UK live with epilepsy. For most, medication controls seizures well — but for more than 30%, seizures persist despite treatment.

Seizures — what they are and how they differ (1 of 2)
How a seizure presents depends entirely on where in the brain it starts and how far it spreads
🎯
Focal seizures — one area of the brain
Begin in one localised brain area. Symptoms reflect the function of that region — rhythmic jerking, déjà vu, a rising stomach sensation, or altered awareness. Awareness may be preserved or impaired.
⚡
Generalised seizures — both hemispheres
Involve both hemispheres of the brain from the outset. Consciousness is typically lost. Pattern varies by subtype — from brief blank staring to full convulsions with muscle stiffening and jerking.
〰️
Focal to bilateral — when seizures spread
A focal seizure can spread to involve both hemispheres, producing a generalised tonic-clonic seizure. An aura — itself a focal seizure — often precedes this spread and gives a brief warning.
Types of generalised seizure (2 of 2)
Each subtype has a distinct pattern — recognition matters for correct classification and treatment
💥
Tonic-clonic — the most recognised type
Stiffening (tonic phase) then rhythmic jerking (clonic phase). Loss of consciousness. Post-ictal confusion and fatigue typically follow. Previously called grand mal.
👁️
Absence and myoclonic seizures
Absence: brief blank staring lasting seconds, most common in children. Myoclonic: sudden muscle jerks, often on waking. Consciousness is typically preserved with myoclonic seizures.
⬇️
Atonic seizures — drop attacks
Sudden loss of muscle tone — the person falls without warning. Also called drop attacks. Risk of head injury is high. May require protective measures including specialist headgear.
Assessment and diagnosis in UK practice (1 of 2)
NICE NG217 — Epilepsies in children, young people and adults (April 2022, updated January 2025)
📋
Clinical history — the most important diagnostic tool
A detailed account of events — including from a witness — is the foundation of diagnosis. NICE NG217 recommends prompt specialist referral after a first suspected seizure.
Specialist
referral
📡
EEG — recording the brain's electrical activity
Records brain electrical activity via scalp sensors. Supports diagnosis and seizure classification. A normal result does not exclude epilepsy — it can be normal between episodes.
Not
conclusive
🔬
Brain imaging — MRI recommended for most
MRI is recommended for most people newly diagnosed with epilepsy to identify structural causes and guide treatment choice. CT is used where MRI is not available or appropriate.
MRI
first-line
Assessment and diagnosis in UK practice (2 of 2)
Misdiagnosis is a significant clinical problem — and NICE NG217 addresses it directly
⚠️
Misdiagnosis — more than 1 in 4 referrals
A UK specialist clinic study found 26.1% of patients referred with epilepsy did not have it. Incomplete history-taking and EEG misinterpretation were equally responsible for the errors.
26.1%
rate
🔄
NEAD — the most common alternative diagnosis
The most common alternative is NEAD — episodes resembling seizures but not caused by abnormal brain electrical activity. Management and treatment differ entirely from epilepsy.
Not
epilepsy
🏥
Why specialist diagnosis matters
NICE NG217 states diagnosis should be made by an epilepsy specialist, not in primary care. Treatment choices — and their consequences — depend entirely on getting the diagnosis right.
NG217
NICE
Anti-seizure medications — what UK practice uses
NICE NG217 guides first-line treatment by seizure type and epilepsy syndrome
💊
Focal epilepsy — first-line anti-seizure medication
Two anti-seizure medication classes are first-line for focal epilepsy. Choice depends on age, sex and medical history. These medications reduce seizures — they do not cure epilepsy.
First-line
focal
💊
Generalised epilepsy — broader options, one restricted
A broader range of anti-seizure medications is used for generalised epilepsy. One historically effective class is now significantly restricted due to pregnancy safety concerns.
Broader
restricted
💊
Add-on treatments — when first-line medication fails
Further anti-seizure medication classes can be added when initial treatment is ineffective. Each subsequent drug trial carries a progressively lower probability of achieving seizure freedom.
Adjunctive
therapy
⚠️
A critical pregnancy safety issue — MHRA Pregnancy Prevention Programme
One anti-seizure medication class is teratogenic — capable of causing serious harm to a developing foetus, including structural birth defects and neurodevelopmental disorders. The MHRA operates a specific Pregnancy Prevention Programme restricting its use in girls and women of childbearing potential unless no effective alternative exists.
When medication is not enough — drug-resistant epilepsy (1 of 2)
Defined as failure of two or more adequate drug trials · affects more than 30% of people with epilepsy
🔬
Epilepsy surgery — the best chance of seizure freedom
Where seizure-generating tissue can be safely removed, surgery offers the best chance of long-term seizure freedom. Evidence is strongest for temporal lobe epilepsy with a structural cause on MRI.
Best
outcome
⚡
Vagus nerve stimulation — an implanted device
A device implanted under the skin delivers regular electrical pulses to the vagus nerve. VNS does not cure epilepsy but can reduce seizure frequency and severity in carefully selected patients.
Implanted
device
🥑
Ketogenic diet therapy — medically supervised
A medically supervised very high-fat, very low-carbohydrate diet alters brain metabolism in ways that can reduce seizure frequency. Evidence is strongest in children with specific epilepsy syndromes.
Specialist
supervised
When medication is not enough — drug-resistant epilepsy (2 of 2)
Emerging neurostimulation therapies · the 11% threshold · SUDEP awareness
🧠
Responsive neurostimulation and deep brain stimulation
Devices detecting seizure onset and delivering a targeted electrical response are available at specialist centres for selected patients. The evidence base is developing and NICE guidance is evolving.
Specialist
centres
📊
The 11% threshold — why early decisions matter
After two adequate drug failures, probability of the next medication achieving seizure freedom falls to approximately 11%. This threshold defines drug-resistant epilepsy and should trigger specialist referral.
~11%
success
🛡️
SUDEP — seizure control is the key protective factor
Good seizure control is the most powerful protective factor against SUDEP. NICE NG217 requires all people with epilepsy to be informed about this risk — awareness supports the steps that reduce it.
Control
protects
