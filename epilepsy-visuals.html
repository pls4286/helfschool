Epilepsy — What It Is

Seizures — what they are and how they differ
How a seizure presents depends entirely on where in the brain it starts and how far it spreads
🎯 Focal seizures One hemisphere
Begin in a localised area of one side of the brain. Awareness may be preserved or impaired. Symptoms reflect the function of the area involved — rhythmic jerking of a limb, déjà vu, a rising stomach sensation, or altered awareness. May spread to become generalised.
May stay local Aura common Can evolve to generalised
⚡ Generalised seizures Both hemispheres
Involve both hemispheres of the brain from the outset. Loss of consciousness occurs with most types. The pattern of the seizure depends on the type — ranging from brief blank staring to full convulsions.
Loss of consciousness No aura Multiple subtypes
📋 Types of generalised seizure
Tonic-clonic
Stiffening (tonic) then rhythmic jerking (clonic). The most widely recognised type. Previously called grand mal.
Absence
Brief blank staring — seconds only, then resumes. Eyes may flutter. No response to speech. Most common in children.
Myoclonic
Sudden brief muscle jerks — often on waking. May involve arms, legs or the whole body. Consciousness usually preserved.
Atonic
Sudden loss of muscle tone — the person drops or falls without warning. Also called drop attacks. Risk of injury is high.
Assessment and diagnosis in UK practice
NICE NG217 — Epilepsies in children, young people and adults (April 2022, updated January 2025)
📋
Clinical history — the most important diagnostic tool
A detailed account of events — including from a witness — is the foundation of diagnosis. The pattern, onset, duration and recovery all inform seizure classification. NICE NG217 recommends prompt specialist referral after a first suspected seizure.
Specialist
referral
📡
EEG — recording the brain's electrical activity
An EEG (electroencephalogram — sensors on the scalp recording brain electrical patterns) can support diagnosis and help classify seizure type. A normal EEG does not exclude epilepsy — it can be normal between seizures even in confirmed cases.
Not
conclusive
🔬
Brain imaging — MRI recommended for most
MRI (magnetic resonance imaging — a detailed scan using magnetic fields) is recommended for most people newly diagnosed with epilepsy to identify structural causes such as hippocampal sclerosis, tumour, or cortical dysplasia, and to guide treatment choice.
MRI
first-line
🏥
Misdiagnosis — a significant clinical problem
A UK specialist clinic study found a misdiagnosis rate of 26.1%, most commonly due to incomplete history or EEG misinterpretation. The most common alternative diagnosis is NEAD — episodes resembling seizures not caused by abnormal electrical brain activity.
26.1%
misdiagnosed
Anti-seizure medications — what UK practice uses
NICE NG217 guides first-line treatment by seizure type and epilepsy syndrome
💊
Focal epilepsy — first-line options
Lamotrigine (Lamictal) and levetiracetam (Keppra) are the most commonly used first-line ASMs for focal seizures. Choice depends on individual factors including age, sex, and other medical conditions. These medications reduce seizure frequency — they do not cure epilepsy.
Lamotrigine
Levetiracetam
💊
Generalised epilepsy — broader options
Sodium valproate (Epilim) has historically been highly effective across generalised syndromes but is now significantly restricted due to pregnancy safety concerns. Carbamazepine (Tegretol) is effective for focal epilepsy but can worsen generalised seizure types.
Valproate
Carbamazepine
💊
Add-on treatments — when first-line fails
Lacosamide (Vimpat) and topiramate (Topamax) are among the options used as adjunctive therapy — added when initial medications are ineffective. Each subsequent drug carries a progressively lower probability of achieving seizure freedom.
Lacosamide
Topiramate
⚠️
Sodium valproate — a critical safety issue in pregnancy
Valproate is teratogenic — capable of causing serious harm to a developing foetus, including structural birth defects and neurodevelopmental disorders. The MHRA Valproate Pregnancy Prevention Programme restricts its use in girls and women of childbearing potential to cases where no effective alternative exists and all programme requirements are met.
When medication is not enough — drug-resistant epilepsy
Defined as failure of two or more adequate, appropriate anti-seizure medication trials · affects more than 30% of people with epilepsy
🔬
Epilepsy surgery — resection or disconnection
Where the brain tissue generating seizures can be identified and safely removed, surgery offers the best chance of long-term seizure freedom. Most evidence supports surgery for temporal lobe epilepsy where a structural cause is identified on MRI. Requires detailed pre-surgical evaluation at a specialist centre.
Best
outcome
⚡
Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS)
A device implanted under the skin delivers regular electrical pulses to the vagus nerve in the neck. VNS does not cure epilepsy but can reduce seizure frequency and severity in carefully selected patients who are not surgical candidates. Typically used alongside medication.
Implanted
device
🥑
Ketogenic diet therapy
A medically supervised diet very high in fat and very low in carbohydrate alters brain metabolism in ways that can reduce seizure frequency — particularly in children with certain epilepsy syndromes. Requires close supervision by a specialist dietitian and epilepsy team.
Specialist
supervised
🧠
Responsive neurostimulation and deep brain stimulation
Emerging technologies — including devices that detect seizure onset and deliver a targeted electrical response — are available at specialist centres for highly selected patients. Evidence base is developing. NICE guidance on their use is evolving.
Specialist
centres
What the research shows — treatment outcomes
Chen et al, JAMA Neurology 2018 · Kwan & Brodie, NEJM 2000
JAMA Neurology 2018
~50%
become seizure-free on their first anti-seizure medication
63.7% ultimately seizure-free · 30-year cohort · 1,795 patients · first drug is most likely to work

A 30-year longitudinal cohort study followed 1,795 patients with newly diagnosed epilepsy. Around half (50.5%) achieved seizure freedom on their first ASM. A further 11.6% achieved it on the second regimen, and 4.4% on the third. Overall, 63.7% were seizure-free by the end of the study. The study confirms that the first medication is the most likely to succeed — and that the probability of seizure control falls significantly with each subsequent drug failure. Early accurate diagnosis and appropriate first-line drug choice are therefore critically important.

Chen Z et al. JAMA Neurol. 2018;75(3):279–286. doi:10.1001/jamaneurol.2017.3949. PMID: 29279892.
NEJM 2000
>30%
develop drug-resistant epilepsy despite adequate treatment
After 2+ drug failures — probability of seizure freedom from further medication falls to ~11%

The landmark Glasgow cohort study prospectively followed 525 patients with newly diagnosed epilepsy. It established that more than 30% fail to achieve adequate seizure control with medication. Critically, if the first drug failed due to lack of efficacy, the probability of a subsequent drug achieving seizure freedom was only 11%. This paper defined the clinical concept of drug-resistant epilepsy and directly informs the pathway toward surgical and other specialist options. People with a structural or unknown cause for their epilepsy, and those with many seizures before starting treatment, were most likely to develop drug resistance.

Kwan P, Brodie MJ. N Engl J Med. 2000;342(5):314–319. doi:10.1056/NEJM200002033420503. PMID: 10660394.
What the research shows — misdiagnosis and SUDEP
Smith et al, QJM 1999 · Thurman et al, Epilepsia 2014
QJM 1999 — UK study
26.1%
of patients referred with epilepsy were subsequently found not to have it
Walton Centre specialist clinic · 324 patients · incomplete history and EEG misinterpretation equally responsible

A retrospective analysis of 324 patients referred to a specialist UK neurology centre found an overall misdiagnosis rate of 26.1% — more than one in four. Incomplete history-taking and misinterpretation of the EEG were equally responsible. Among those with a mistaken epilepsy diagnosis, consequences included unnecessary anti-seizure medication with associated side-effects, driving restrictions, and employment difficulties. This study underpins the NICE NG217 recommendation that diagnosis should be made by an epilepsy specialist, not in primary care — and that the clinical history remains the most important diagnostic tool.

Smith D, Defalla BA, Chadwick DW. QJM. 1999;92(1):15–23. doi:10.1093/qjmed/92.1.15. PMID: 10209668.
Epilepsia 2014
Seizure control is the key protective factor
Good control substantially reduces SUDEP risk
~1 in 1,000/yr overall · substantially higher with uncontrolled tonic-clonic seizures

A systematic review of epidemiological SUDEP data established an overall crude annual incidence of approximately 1.16 cases per 1,000 patients with epilepsy. The risk is not evenly distributed — it is substantially higher in people with frequent, uncontrolled tonic-clonic seizures, and substantially lower in those who are seizure-free. NICE NG217 now requires that all people with epilepsy are informed about SUDEP — a shift from previous practice that reflects recognition that knowledge of risk supports the steps that reduce it: medication adherence, avoiding known triggers, and seizure monitoring during sleep.

Thurman DJ, Hesdorffer DC, French JA. Epilepsia. 2014;55(10):1479–1485. doi:10.1111/epi.12666. PMID: 24903551.
