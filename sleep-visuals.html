What your doctor can actually do about sleep
📔
Step 1 · The starting point
Two-week sleep diary
Bedtime · time to fall asleep · night-time waking · rise time · how rested you felt. Simple, powerful — and it reveals patterns memory cannot.
🫁
Screen for
Obstructive sleep apnoea
Loud snoring + daytime sleepiness warrants screening. Tools: Epworth Sleepiness Scale, STOP-Bang. Treatment (CPAP) can be life-changing.
🧠
First-line treatment
CBT-I · cognitive behavioural therapy
NICE first-line for chronic insomnia at any age. Works better than sleeping tablets long-term. Face-to-face, group or digital apps available.
💊
The honest picture
Sleeping tablets (Z-drugs, benzodiazepines)
Licensed for up to 4 weeks. Real risks long-term: tolerance, dependence, falls, fractures. Place for short crises — not a long-term answer.
🌙
Flag if relevant
Menopause · shift work · older age
Each brings specific sleep challenges worth raising directly. HRT may help menopausal sleep disruption. Shift work needs structured scheduling advice.
🩺
Also consider
Treatable underlying causes
Thyroid problems, reflux, restless legs, chronic pain, medications — all can disrupt sleep. A proper assessment looks for these rather than just treating symptoms.
What the evidence actually shows
~7h
lowest-risk sleep duration
Yin et al. · sleep duration & CVD
JAHA · 2017 · meta-analysis · 3.5M+ people
U-shaped relationship: lowest cardiovascular and mortality risk at ~7 hours, rising on either side.
+12% / +30%
mortality risk
(short / long sleep)
Cappuccio et al. · all-cause mortality
SLEEP · 2010 · meta-analysis
Short sleep 12% higher death risk · long sleep 30% higher — both independently predict mortality.
7–8h
lowest-risk sleep for T2DM
Shan et al. · sleep & type 2 diabetes
Diabetes Care · 2015 · meta-analysis
U-shape: each hour less below 7h = +9% T2DM risk · each hour more above 8h = +14% T2DM risk.
+30%
dementia risk
persistent short sleep
Sabia et al. · Whitehall II 25-yr follow-up
Nature Communications · 2021
Persistent short sleep (≤6h) from age 50 onwards = ~30% higher dementia risk 25 years later, independent of other factors.
Lasting
improvement in sleep
van Straten et al. · CBT-I meta-analysis
Sleep Medicine Reviews · 2018 · 87 RCTs
CBT-I produces consistent improvements in falling asleep, staying asleep, and sleep quality — and unlike sleeping tablets, effects persist long after treatment ends. Basis for the NICE first-line recommendation.
📋 Action 1
Today
📔
Start a two-week sleep diary
Before you change anything, see the pattern. A simple pen-and-paper diary for two weeks gives you — and your GP — something concrete to work with.
Bedtime Time to fall asleep Night-waking Rise time How rested you felt
📋 Action 2
This week
🫁
If you snore loudly and feel tired by day — get screened
Obstructive sleep apnoea is common, widely missed, and genuinely treatable. Booking a GP appointment is the single most useful thing you can do if the pattern fits.
Loud snoring Witnessed pauses in breathing Gasping / choking Daytime sleepiness Morning headaches
📋 Action 3
First — not tablets
🧠
If poor sleep has gone on for a long time — ask about CBT-I
Cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia is the NHS first-line treatment. It works at least as well as sleeping tablets short-term — and much better long-term. Ask your GP, or look into digital CBT-I programmes.
