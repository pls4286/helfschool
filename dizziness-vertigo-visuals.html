Dizziness and
Vertigo
One of the most common and treatable conditions in primary care — yet most people who have it never receive effective treatment.

Dizziness vs Vertigo — not the same thing · 1 of 2
💫
Dizziness — a broad umbrella term
Covers lightheadedness, unsteadiness, and imbalance. Many causes — cardiovascular, medication-related, anxiety, and inner ear origin. Not a single condition.
Broad
🌀
Vertigo — a specific spinning sensation
A specific sensation of spinning or rotating when no movement is occurring — often with nausea and involuntary eye movements. Clinically distinct from general dizziness.
Specific
🎯
Why the distinction matters
The origin — inner ear or brain — determines the diagnosis, urgency, and management. Getting the distinction right shapes everything that follows in assessment.
Origin = key
Dizziness vs Vertigo — peripheral vs central · 2 of 2
👂
Peripheral vertigo — inner ear origin
Arising from the vestibular system — the inner ear and its nerve. Includes BPPV, vestibular neuritis, labyrinthitis, and Ménière's disease. Most causes are treatable.
Inner ear
🧠
Central vertigo — brain origin
Arising from the brainstem or cerebellum — the brain's balance co-ordinator. Less common but more serious — causes include stroke affecting the posterior circulation.
Urgent
🔍
HINTS examination
A bedside test distinguishing inner ear vertigo from stroke — requires specific training. When unavailable, NICE guidance recommends immediate referral via stroke pathway.
Specialist test
The main causes of peripheral vertigo · 1 of 2
🪨
BPPV — Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo
Brief intense spinning triggered by head position changes. Displaced calcium crystals in the inner ear semicircular canals. The most common single cause of vertigo.
Seconds
🦠
Vestibular Neuritis
Viral inflammation of the vestibular nerve. A single severe episode lasting hours to days — without hearing loss. The brain compensates over several weeks.
Hours–days
🔊
Ménière's Disease
Recurring episodes: vertigo, fluctuating hearing loss, ear ringing, and fullness. Episodes last minutes to hours — caused by increased fluid pressure in the inner ear.
Mins–hours
The main causes of peripheral vertigo · 2 of 2
👂
Labyrinthitis
Inflammation of the entire inner ear — affecting both balance and hearing. Differs from vestibular neuritis where hearing is preserved.
Hours–days
⏱️
Duration is the key clinical clue
Seconds points to BPPV. Hours to days points to vestibular neuritis or labyrinthitis. Minutes to hours suggests Ménière's disease. Duration narrows diagnosis before any test.
Duration
✅
Peripheral causes are treatable
Unlike central vertigo, all four peripheral causes have recognised and effective treatments — from repositioning for BPPV to vestibular rehabilitation exercises.
Treatable
BPPV — a mechanical problem with a mechanical solution
🪨
Crystals become displaced
Tiny calcium crystals — otoconia — break loose from the inner ear and migrate into the semicircular canals, the fluid-filled tubes that detect head rotation.
Step 1
⚡
False signals — the spinning sensation
Displaced crystals continue moving after the head stops — sending a false signal of ongoing rotation and producing the brief intense spinning sensation.
Step 2
🎯
Test confirms · Manoeuvre resolves
The Dix-Hallpike test confirms the diagnosis. The Epley manoeuvre — five minutes of guided repositioning — moves crystals back to where they cause no symptoms.
Step 3
When vertigo is clinically time-sensitive
⚡
Sudden severe vertigo + new headache
Sudden vertigo with a new severe headache or neck stiffness — clinically time-sensitive to exclude bleeding around the brain. 111 and urgent GP services exist.
Urgent
🧠
Neurological symptoms alongside vertigo
Vertigo alongside double vision, slurred speech, difficulty swallowing, or new weakness — patterns clinically recognised as potentially indicating a stroke affecting the brainstem.
Urgent
👁️
Spontaneous eye flickering or inability to walk
Spontaneous vertical eye flickering at rest, or sudden complete inability to walk not explained by position — both warrant urgent clinical assessment.
Urgent
Research · what treatment evidence shows
Cochrane Review
The Epley manoeuvre
is safe & effective for BPPV / superior to sham manoeuvre
36% recur within 4 years · re-treatment works · equivalent results in primary care or specialist settings
Epley manoeuvre for posterior canal BPPV
Hilton MP, Pinder DK · Cochrane Database · 2014;(12):CD003162
Safe and effective — superior to sham manoeuvres. Produces equivalent results in primary care or specialist settings. Around 36% experience recurrence within 4 years, but re-treatment works.
Cochrane Review
Vestibular rehabilitation
has moderate to strong evidence for its efficacy
39 studies · 2,441 participants · dizziness, activity limits & daily life all improved
Vestibular rehabilitation for unilateral peripheral disorders
McDonnell MN, Hillier SL · Cochrane Database · 2015;(1):CD005397
Significant improvements in dizziness frequency, activity limitation, and participation in daily life. Most valuable for vestibular neuritis recovery where the brain's compensation process is incomplete.
Research · the treatment gap
Key Finding
8%
received effective treatment / despite 86% seeking medical help · BPPV population study
von Brevern et al · JNNP · 2007 · prevalence twice as high in women
BPPV treatment gap — population study
von Brevern M et al · J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry · 2007;78(7):710–715
Despite 86% seeking medical help, most underwent investigations rather than the positional tests and repositioning that would have resolved the problem. Prevalence is twice as high in women.
UK Audit
93
weeks
average wait from first GP visit to effective treatment / vs immediate at first presentation
58 weeks within primary care alone · Norfolk & Norwich · Fife & FitzGerald · 2005 ⚑
BPPV delay audit — UK primary care
Fife D, FitzGerald JE · Int J Audiol · 2005;44(1):50–57
58 of the 93 weeks of delay occurred within primary care alone. Most had classical BPPV symptoms that could have been identified and treated at first presentation.
