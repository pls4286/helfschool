Dizziness vs Vertigo — not the same thing
Understanding the distinction shapes diagnosis and treatment
🌀
Dizziness
A broad umbrella term covering lightheadedness (feeling faint), unsteadiness, and imbalance. Many different causes — cardiovascular, medication-related, anxiety, and inner ear.
Broad symptom category
⚡
Vertigo
A specific sensation — you or your surroundings are spinning or rotating when no actual movement is occurring. Often with nausea and involuntary eye movements (nystagmus). A distinct clinical entity.
Specific spinning sensation
👂
Peripheral vertigo
Arising from the vestibular system — the inner ear and its nerve. Includes BPPV, vestibular neuritis, labyrinthitis, and Ménière's disease. Most causes are treatable.
Inner ear origin · common
🧠
Central vertigo
Arising from the brainstem or cerebellum (the brain region co-ordinating balance). Less common but more serious — causes include stroke affecting the posterior circulation. Requires urgent assessment.
Brain origin · red flag pattern
The main causes of peripheral vertigo
Distinguished by duration, triggers, and associated symptoms
🪨
BPPV — Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo
Brief intense spinning triggered by head position changes (rolling in bed, looking up). Displaced calcium crystals in the semicircular canals of the inner ear. Most common single cause of vertigo.
Seconds
🦠
Vestibular Neuritis
Inflammation of the vestibular nerve after viral illness. A single prolonged episode of severe spinning lasting hours to days — without hearing loss. Improves as the brain compensates over weeks.
Hours–days
🔊
Ménière's Disease
Recurring episodes of vertigo with fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus (ringing), and ear fullness or pressure. Episodes last minutes to hours. Caused by increased fluid pressure in the inner ear.
Mins–hours
👂
Labyrinthitis
Inflammation of the entire inner ear — affecting both balance and hearing. Causes vertigo alongside hearing changes, distinguishing it from vestibular neuritis where hearing is unaffected.
Hours–days
BPPV — what happens and how it's treated
A mechanical problem with a highly effective mechanical solution
1
Otoconia become displaced
Tiny calcium carbonate crystals (otoconia — "ear stones") break loose from the utricle of the inner ear and migrate into the semicircular canals — the fluid-filled tubes that detect head rotation.
2
Head movement triggers false signals
When the head moves, displaced crystals continue moving after it stops — sending a false signal of ongoing rotation. The brain receives conflicting inputs, producing the brief intense spinning sensation.
3
Dix-Hallpike test confirms the diagnosis
A clinician moves the patient from sitting to lying with the head turned to one side. A positive test — triggering vertigo and nystagmus (involuntary eye flickering) — confirms BPPV and identifies the affected ear.
4
The Epley manoeuvre repositions the crystals
A sequence of guided head and body movements — around 5 minutes in the GP surgery or with a physiotherapist — moves displaced crystals back to where they cause no symptoms. Cochrane evidence confirms safe and effective.
When vertigo is a time-sensitive presentation
Central vertigo patterns that warrant urgent assessment
⚠️ Patterns clinically recognised as time-sensitive
  • Sudden onset of severe vertigo with new severe headache or neck stiffness
  • Vertigo with double vision (diplopia), slurred speech (dysarthria), or difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)
  • New weakness or numbness of the face or limbs alongside vertigo
  • Sudden complete inability to stand or walk, not explained by positional triggers
  • Spontaneous vertical nystagmus — upward or downward eye flickering at rest, not triggered by the Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre
HINTS examination — Head Impulse / Nystagmus / Test of Skew — is a bedside oculomotor examination that helps trained clinicians distinguish peripheral from central acute vestibular syndrome. It requires specific training and is not universally available in every GP surgery. NICE NG127 recommends immediate referral via local stroke pathways where a trained clinician is not available.
📞
111 and urgent services exist for these patterns
Vertigo with new neurological symptoms is clinically recognised as time-sensitive. 111 and urgent GP services exist for this presentation pattern.
Urgent
What the research shows — treatment
Hilton & Pinder, Cochrane 2014 · McDonnell & Hillier, Cochrane 2015
Cochrane review
The Epley manoeuvre
is a safe & effective treatment for BPPV
Non-invasive · GP surgery or physio · 36% recur within 4 years but re-treatment works

The Cochrane review of the Epley manoeuvre for posterior canal BPPV found it safe and effective — superior to sham manoeuvres and exercises alone. It can be performed in primary care with equivalent results to specialist settings. Around 36% of people experience a recurrence within 4 years, but the manoeuvre can be repeated. One of the most effective and cost-efficient treatments in all of primary care medicine.

Hilton MP, Pinder DK. Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2014;(12):CD003162.
Cochrane review
Vestibular rehabilitation
has moderate to strong evidence for its efficacy
Dizziness frequency, activity limits & daily life improved vs control

The Cochrane review of vestibular rehabilitation — a physiotherapy programme of graded exercises helping the brain compensate for inner ear damage — found moderate to strong evidence of benefit across 39 studies and 2,441 participants. Dizziness frequency, activity limitation, and participation in daily life all improved significantly. Most valuable for vestibular neuritis recovery where the brain's compensation process is incomplete.

McDonnell MN, Hillier SL. Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2015;(1):CD005397.
What the research shows — the treatment gap
von Brevern et al, JNNP 2007 · Fife & FitzGerald, Int J Audiol 2005
Treatment gap
8%
of people with BPPV received effective treatment
Despite 86% seeking medical help — most never receive the Epley manoeuvre

A large population study found that despite 86% of people with BPPV seeking medical help or experiencing disruption to their daily lives, only 8% received effective treatment. Most underwent costly investigations rather than the simple positional tests and repositioning manoeuvres that would have confirmed and resolved the problem. The condition affects twice as many women as men, and prevalence rises with age.

von Brevern M et al. J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry. 2007;78(7):710–715.
UK audit finding
93 weeks
average wait — first GP visit to effective treatment
58 weeks within primary care alone · Norfolk & Norwich NHS Trust

A UK audit tracing 20 patients from initial primary care presentation to eventual effective treatment found an average wait of 93 weeks — with 58 weeks of that delay occurring within primary care alone. Most had classical BPPV symptoms that could have been identified and treated at first presentation. This gap represents an avoidable burden: months of disabling dizziness, increased fall risk, and NHS resources spent on unnecessary investigations.

Fife D, FitzGerald JE. Int J Audiol. 2005;44(1):50–57. PMID 15796102.

Know your vertigo

What the evidence means for understanding your condition

Note how long episodes last — seconds, hours, or days each point to a different cause
🪨
If positional and brief, BPPV is likely — a simple test and manoeuvre can resolve it
⚠️
New neurological symptoms alongside vertigo are clinically time-sensitive — 111 exists for this
🏃
After vestibular neuritis, early movement helps — prolonged rest delays the brain's recovery
