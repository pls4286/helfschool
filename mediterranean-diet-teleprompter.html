▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — The plate
The Mediterranean diet isn't a rulebook. It's a pattern of eating that developed across the coastal regions of southern Europe — and it's now one of the most thoroughly studied dietary patterns in medicine.
— pause —
The pattern is built around vegetables and fruit — roughly a third of the plate. Whole grains alongside. Oily fish at least twice weekly. Legumes — lentils and chickpeas — regularly in place of meat. And throughout everything, extra-virgin olive oil as the main cooking and dressing fat, in place of butter. The olive oil shift matters more than most people realise. It moves fat intake from saturated to monounsaturated — and that shift is one of the most important features of the whole pattern.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Three tiers
The pattern is sometimes described in three tiers.
— pause —
At every meal, the bulk of the plate: vegetables, fruit, whole grains, legumes, nuts, olive oil. This is the foundation. Several times a week: oily fish — salmon, mackerel, sardines — at least twice. Eggs, poultry, and moderate dairy on the other days.
— pause —
Occasionally, not daily: red meat, processed meat, added sugar, refined grains, ultra-processed food. Not excluded — just infrequent. The key finding from the research is that the pattern is characterised by abundance, not restriction. And studies consistently show that even partial adherence — moving in the direction without following every element — produces meaningful benefit.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — PREDIMED trial
So what does the evidence actually show? The landmark study is PREDIMED — one of the largest dietary intervention trials ever conducted.
— pause —
Seven thousand four hundred and forty-seven people at high cardiovascular risk, randomised to Mediterranean diet with olive oil, Mediterranean diet with nuts, or standard low-fat dietary advice. The trial was stopped after four point eight years — because the benefit was already too clear to continue ethically.
— pause —
The Mediterranean diet group had approximately thirty percent fewer major cardiovascular events — heart attacks, strokes, and cardiovascular deaths — compared to the low-fat control. Published in the New England Journal of Medicine. The strongest dietary evidence in preventive medicine.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 5 — Three benefit cards
What makes the Mediterranean diet unusual is that the evidence extends well beyond cardiovascular disease.
— pause —
Type 2 diabetes: a meta-analysis of ten studies and over 136,000 participants found twenty-three percent lower risk of developing type 2 diabetes with higher adherence. Brain health: a 2025 meta-analysis published in GeroScience found up to thirty percent lower risk of Alzheimer's disease, and around eleven percent lower risk of dementia overall.
— pause —
Blood pressure and cholesterol also improve — the pattern's high potassium content from vegetables counters sodium, and the replacement of saturated fat with olive oil and nuts improves LDL cholesterol.
