Segment 2 — What is dementia? (~50 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 2–3: Types of dementia

Let me start with something really important: dementia is not a single disease. It's an umbrella term — a group of conditions that all cause progressive decline in brain function. And the type matters because the causes and patterns are different.

Alzheimer's disease accounts for around 65% of cases — it's caused by abnormal protein deposits in the brain that damage nerve cells over time. Memory for recent events is usually the first thing to go.

Vascular dementia — around 20% of cases — happens when the brain's blood supply is disrupted. Often after a stroke or a series of small vessel changes. It can worsen in steps rather than steadily.

Dementia with Lewy bodies involves protein deposits that cause a very characteristic pattern — visual hallucinations, tremor and stiffness similar to Parkinson's, and fluctuating alertness. It's frequently misdiagnosed.

Frontotemporal dementia tends to affect personality and language before memory — and it's the most common form in people under 65. Changes are often subtle and mistaken for stress or mental health difficulties.

And there's one more important distinction — mild cognitive impairment, or MCI. This is a recognised intermediate stage where memory or thinking is measurably affected but daily life isn't yet significantly disrupted. MCI is not dementia, but around 10 to 15% of people with MCI do go on to develop it each year.

Segment 3 — 14 risk factors (~55 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 4: 14 risk factors across the life course

Now here's the genuinely hopeful part. The 2024 Lancet Commission on Dementia — the world's most comprehensive review of this evidence — concluded that 45% of all dementia cases are potentially preventable. Nearly half. By addressing 14 modifiable risk factors across the life course.

In early life, the biggest factor is education — it builds what researchers call cognitive reserve, the brain's resilience to future damage.

In midlife, the key factors include hearing loss — at 9% the single biggest individual risk factor — high blood pressure, obesity, excess alcohol, head injury, and now high LDL cholesterol, which was added as a new risk factor in 2024.

In later life, the factors include smoking, depression, social isolation, physical inactivity, diabetes, air pollution, and untreated vision loss — also added in 2024.

The message from this evidence is clear: it's never too early to start reducing risk — and it's never too late either. Addressing risk factors in your 60s and 70s still makes a real difference.

Segment 4 — Hearing loss (~40 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 5: Hearing loss — the largest single risk factor

I want to spend a moment on hearing loss specifically — because it's probably the most surprising finding in this whole field, and the most immediately actionable.

Untreated hearing loss accounts for 9% of all dementia cases globally — more than any other single modifiable factor. The evidence for why is compelling. When you struggle to hear, your brain has to work much harder to process sound — resources that would otherwise support memory and reasoning are diverted elsewhere. Over time, reduced auditory input is also associated with structural brain changes. And hearing loss drives social withdrawal, which is itself an independent risk factor.

The 2024 Lancet Commission states that the evidence for hearing aids reducing dementia risk is now stronger than it was four years ago. Hearing aids appear to reduce dementia risk, particularly in those with multiple risk factors.

If you have hearing loss, getting it assessed and treated isn't just about hearing better. It may be one of the most powerful things you can do for your long-term brain health.

Segment 5 — Assessment and management (~45 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 6–7: Assessment and management

So what happens in UK practice when someone presents with concerns about their memory?

A GP will take a careful history — including talking to someone who knows the person well, because early cognitive change is often more apparent to others than to the person themselves. Standardised cognitive tests — including the MoCA and others — provide a structured, repeatable baseline.

Blood tests check for fully reversible causes of cognitive slowing: an underactive thyroid, B12 deficiency, anaemia. These are important to exclude because they're treatable. Brain imaging — MRI or CT — may follow, and referral to a memory clinic for formal assessment.

If Alzheimer's disease is confirmed, a class of medication called cholinesterase inhibitors can modestly slow symptom progression. A second drug class is available for more advanced disease. Neither stops the underlying process, but they can make a meaningful difference to daily functioning.

Post-diagnosis planning is just as important: lasting power of attorney, DVLA notification, connecting with the Alzheimer's Society and specialist support services. And NICE recommends cognitive stimulation therapy — a group-based programme — as a non-pharmacological option for mild to moderate dementia.

Segment 6 — Research and close (~55 seconds)
▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 8: Research — prevention

The research picture for dementia has shifted considerably in the last decade. The headline finding — 45% of cases potentially preventable through modifiable risk factors — represents one of the most significant public health messages of our time. And of that 45%, the single largest share is linked to untreated hearing loss. That one finding alone has practical, immediate implications for millions of people.

▶ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 9: Research — the UK picture

The scale in the UK is stark. Dementia is the country's leading cause of death — it's been the leading cause of death for UK women since 2011, and now heads the overall table. With no disease-modifying treatment currently available on the NHS, prevention is the most powerful tool we have.

And there's an important signal that the prevention message is already working. Age-specific dementia incidence — the rate of new cases per 1,000 people of a given age — is declining in high-income countries. That's evidence that better cardiovascular management, reduced smoking, and improved education are already making a real-world difference.
