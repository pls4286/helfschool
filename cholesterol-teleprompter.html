Segment 1 — What is cholesterol?

"Cholesterol is a waxy, fat-like substance that's essential for life. Every cell membrane in your body contains cholesterol. Your body needs it to make hormones, vitamin D, and bile acids that help digest fat."

"Here's the important bit — there are different types of cholesterol. When we talk about 'good' and 'bad' cholesterol, we're really talking about the proteins that carry cholesterol around your bloodstream — called lipoproteins." PAUSE

⬛ Coming Up — Advance Visuals Now
Screen 2: Cholesterol types · HDL vs LDL cards · good vs bad explanation

"HDL cholesterol — high-density lipoprotein — is called 'good cholesterol' because it carries cholesterol away from your arteries back to your liver, where it's broken down or recycled. Think of it as a cleanup crew."

"LDL cholesterol — low-density lipoprotein — is called 'bad cholesterol' because when there's too much of it, it can build up in the walls of your arteries. Over time, this forms plaques that narrow and harden your arteries." PAUSE

Segment 2 — What should your levels be?

"The target levels depend on your cardiovascular risk, but as a general guide: total cholesterol should be below 5 millimoles per litre, and LDL cholesterol below 3 millimoles per litre."

⬛ Coming Up — Advance Visuals Now
Screen 3: Target levels table · Total, LDL, HDL targets · high risk patients

"For HDL — the good cholesterol — higher is better. We want to see levels above 1.0 in men and above 1.2 in women. If you've already had a heart attack or stroke, the targets are stricter — LDL should be below 1.8." PAUSE

"Here's something important — cholesterol levels are largely determined by your genes, not your diet. Your liver makes about 80% of your cholesterol. Dietary cholesterol — from eggs, for example — has much less impact than we once thought."

Segment 3 — Why does it matter?

"High cholesterol matters because it's one of the major risk factors for heart disease and stroke. In the UK, around 60% of adults have raised cholesterol. That's nearly 40 million people."

⬛ Coming Up — Advance Visuals Now
Screen 4: Statistics · 60% UK adults · 2.3× higher risk · 40% contribution to heart attacks

"People with high cholesterol have more than double the risk of developing heart disease compared to those with optimal levels. Cholesterol contributes to about 40% of heart attacks globally." PAUSE

"The problem with cholesterol is that it usually has no symptoms. You can't feel high cholesterol. You need a blood test to know your levels. That's why regular health checks are so important, especially if you're over 40."

Segment 4 — What the research shows

"The evidence on cholesterol treatment is overwhelming. The Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' study — the largest meta-analysis ever conducted — looked at data from over 170,000 people. They found that for every 1 millimole reduction in LDL cholesterol, major cardiovascular events dropped by 22%."

⬛ Coming Up — Advance Visuals Now
Screen 5: Research cards · 22% reduction statins · 50% genetic risk · 36% stroke prevention

"Perhaps even more convincing is the genetic evidence. People born with naturally low LDL cholesterol — due to genetic variants — have about 50% lower risk of coronary heart disease over their lifetime. This tells us that cholesterol itself is causal, not just associated with heart disease." PAUSE

"The research also shows that statins work for primary prevention — that means preventing first heart attacks in people who haven't had one yet. A Cochrane review found statins reduced the risk of stroke by 36% in people without existing heart disease."

"And contrary to some media reports, statins are remarkably safe. Serious side effects are rare — muscle problems occur in less than 1 in 1000 people. The benefits far outweigh the risks for most people." PAUSE

Segment 5 — Putting it together

"So — cholesterol is mostly determined by genetics, not diet. LDL cholesterol is the main culprit in heart disease. High cholesterol is very common but usually symptomless."
