▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — HDL vs LDL
Cholesterol is a fatty substance that the body produces naturally — and it's essential. Every cell membrane contains it. It's needed to make hormones, vitamin D, and bile acids that digest fat. The problem arises when certain types accumulate in the bloodstream at too high a level.
— pause —
Cholesterol is carried in the blood by proteins, forming particles called lipoproteins. The two most important are HDL and LDL. HDL — the so-called good cholesterol — carries cholesterol away from artery walls back to the liver for disposal. LDL — the so-called bad cholesterol — deposits cholesterol into artery walls, driving the build-up of plaques that narrow and harden arteries. That process is called atherosclerosis.
— pause —
The key measure in UK clinical practice is now non-HDL cholesterol — essentially total cholesterol minus HDL. For most healthy adults, the target is below 4.0 mmol/L.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Where cholesterol causes harm
Why does raised LDL cholesterol matter? Because it drives progressive damage across the cardiovascular system — coronary heart disease, stroke, peripheral arterial disease, and aortic aneurysm. And for roughly 1 in 250 UK adults, a genetic condition called familial hypercholesterolaemia means LDL has been dangerously elevated since birth — dramatically increasing the risk of early heart disease.
— pause —
In England, around 59% of adults have a total cholesterol of 5 mmol/L or above. It produces no symptoms — it is detectable only through a blood test. This is why cholesterol testing is a recognised part of routine cardiovascular risk assessment in UK healthcare, particularly from age 40 onwards.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — Doctor's toolkit
In UK practice, cholesterol is measured as part of a full lipid panel — total cholesterol, HDL, non-HDL, and triglycerides. Under current NICE guidelines, fasting before the test is no longer routinely required. The results are interpreted in the context of overall cardiovascular risk, assessed using QRISK3 — a validated risk calculator that factors in age, blood pressure, family history, and other clinical variables.
— pause —
Where cholesterol is elevated, management typically involves lifestyle measures — reducing saturated fat intake, regular aerobic exercise, weight management, reduced alcohol, and smoking cessation are all clinically recognised as improving lipid profiles.
— pause —
Where medication is indicated, the NICE NG238 pathway identifies atorvastatin as first-line treatment. For secondary prevention — that is, people who have already had a cardiovascular event — the dose is higher and the LDL target is below 2.0 mmol/L. Where LDL targets aren't reached on statin alone, ezetimibe is added.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–7 — Research evidence
The evidence base for LDL lowering is among the strongest in cardiovascular medicine. The Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' meta-analysis — pooling data from over 170,000 participants across randomised trials — found that each 1 mmol/L reduction in LDL cuts major cardiovascular events by approximately 22%. The benefit was consistent across age groups, sexes, and starting cholesterol levels.
— pause —
Genetic research adds further weight. People born with naturally lower LDL — due to inherited genetic variants — have around 55% lower lifetime risk of coronary heart disease per 1 mmol/L lower LDL. This tells us that LDL is causally related to heart disease, not merely associated — and that earlier reduction produces proportionally greater benefit.
— pause —
On supplements — the SPORT trial compared low-dose rosuvastatin against six commonly used supplements and placebo. The statin produced around a 38% LDL reduction. Fish oil, garlic, cinnamon, turmeric, plant sterols, and red yeast rice produced no significant reduction. The 2025 ESC/EAS guidelines reviewed all available evidence and reached the same conclusion.

Cholesterol is largely determined by genetics, not diet alone. Raised LDL produces no symptoms. The evidence for LDL lowering — through lifestyle and medication — is robust and consistent. No supplement provides the LDL reduction that a statin delivers.
