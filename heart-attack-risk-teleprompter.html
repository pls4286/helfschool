Segment 1 — What is cardiovascular risk assessment?
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 2: QRISK example · 12% risk score · explanation of probability

"When your GP talks about your risk of a heart attack or stroke, they're not guessing. They're using a structured, evidence-based approach that combines several pieces of information about you — your age, blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking status, and other factors."

"This is called cardiovascular risk assessment, and it's one of the most important tools in preventive medicine. Rather than waiting for something to go wrong, it allows your doctor to identify people who would benefit most from treatment — before a heart attack or stroke happens." PAUSE

"In the UK, the main tool used for this is called QRISK3. It gives a result expressed as a percentage — for example, a score of 12% means you have roughly a 12 in 100 chance of having a heart attack or stroke in the next ten years."

"This isn't a certainty — it's a probability, based on how people with a similar profile to yours have fared in large population studies. The score is used as a starting point for a conversation between you and your doctor, not as a verdict." PAUSE

Segment 2 — What factors are included?
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 3: Risk factors grid · 6 cards showing key QRISK3 inputs

"QRISK3 looks at multiple risk factors and combines them mathematically. The main ones include your age and gender — risk increases with age, and men generally have higher risk than women until after the menopause."

"Your blood pressure — both the top and bottom numbers. Your cholesterol ratio — that's your total cholesterol divided by your HDL cholesterol. Your smoking status — current smoking significantly increases the calculated risk." PAUSE

"It also includes your BMI — body mass index — which reflects obesity-related cardiovascular risk. And your medical history — things like diabetes, kidney disease, family history of heart disease, and several other conditions."

"The clever bit is how QRISK3 weights these factors differently depending on your age, gender, and other characteristics. It's not just a simple checklist — it's a sophisticated mathematical model based on data from millions of people." PAUSE

Segment 3 — What do the results mean?
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 4: Treatment thresholds · <10%, 10%+, 20%+ with treatment recommendations

"So what do you do with the result? Generally, if your calculated risk is below 10%, you're considered low risk. Your doctor will focus on lifestyle advice — diet, exercise, not smoking."

"If your risk is 10% or above, that's when your doctor will start discussing whether you might benefit from medication — usually a statin to lower your cholesterol. The higher your risk, the stronger the recommendation becomes." PAUSE

"At 20% or above, you're considered high risk, and there's usually a strong recommendation for statin therapy alongside lifestyle changes. But it's always a discussion — the final decision is always yours."

"Remember — these are population-based probabilities. They can't tell you what will definitely happen to you as an individual, but they give your doctor a way to target treatment to the people most likely to benefit." PAUSE

Segment 4 — What the research shows
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 5: Research findings · 4 cards with key study results

"The evidence supporting risk-based treatment is very strong. Studies show that QRISK3 is more accurate at predicting cardiovascular events than previous risk calculators. It's been validated in large UK populations and performs well."

"More importantly, treating patients based on calculated risk works. A Cochrane review found that risk-based treatment reduces cardiovascular events by about 30% compared to not treating based on risk." PAUSE

"We know from the Cholesterol Treatment Trialists study that each 1 millimole reduction in LDL cholesterol with statins reduces major cardiovascular events by 22%. That's a substantial benefit."

"And lifestyle interventions work too. Combined lifestyle changes — diet, exercise, weight loss, stopping smoking — can reduce cardiovascular risk by 40 to 50%. Sometimes that's enough to avoid medication entirely." PAUSE
