▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — QRISK3 explained
When a GP discusses the risk of a heart attack or stroke, this isn't based on intuition. It's a structured, evidence-based calculation that combines multiple pieces of information — age, blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking status, medical history, and other factors — to produce a personalised risk estimate.
— pause —
This is called cardiovascular risk assessment, and it is one of the most important tools in preventive medicine. Rather than waiting for something to go wrong, it allows identification of people who would benefit most from lifestyle modification or medication — before a heart attack or stroke happens.
— pause —
In the UK, the main tool is called QRISK3. It produces a result expressed as a percentage. A score of 12%, for example, represents roughly a 12 in 100 chance of having a heart attack or stroke in the next ten years — based on how people with a similar clinical profile have fared in large UK population studies. This is a probability estimate, not a certainty. And it's a starting point for a conversation between patient and doctor — not a verdict.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Key risk factors
QRISK3 draws on a wide range of clinical variables. The main ones include age and sex — risk approximately doubles every decade from midlife, and men tend to reach any given risk threshold around a decade earlier than women.
— pause —
Blood pressure is a major contributor — every 20 mmHg rise in systolic pressure approximately doubles cardiovascular mortality risk. The total:HDL cholesterol ratio captures LDL burden. Smoking status is captured — with current smoking associated with two to four times higher cardiovascular risk. And diabetes is included — type 2 diabetes approximately doubles cardiovascular risk.
— pause —
Alongside these, the calculator incorporates medical conditions like atrial fibrillation — an irregular heart rhythm that significantly increases stroke risk — chronic kidney disease, rheumatoid arthritis, and severe mental illness. Family history of early heart disease is also factored in. QRISK3 was validated in almost 8 million UK primary care records.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — Doctor's toolkit
So what does the score mean in practice? Below 10% — lower risk. Lifestyle measures are the clinical focus. At 10% or above — NICE guideline NG238 identifies this as the threshold at which statin therapy is discussed alongside lifestyle measures. Atorvastatin 20mg is the recommended first-line medication for primary prevention. At 20% or above, the clinical evidence strongly supports both statin therapy and lifestyle modification together.
— pause —
These thresholds aren't rigid rules — they're the starting point for a clinical conversation. The full picture includes the patient's values, preferences, and the balance of benefits and risks of any intervention. The NHS Health Check programme offers free cardiovascular risk assessment to everyone aged 40–74 in England every five years — including QRISK3 calculation, blood pressure, cholesterol, and BMI.
— pause —
Beyond the calculator, clinical judgement plays a role. Family history of premature heart disease, abdominal obesity, physical inactivity, and South Asian ethnicity are all factors that a GP may consider alongside the calculated score — particularly where the result sits close to a treatment threshold.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–7 — Research evidence
The evidence for risk-based cardiovascular prevention is strong. The INTERHEART study — involving nearly 30,000 people across 52 countries — found that just nine modifiable factors account for approximately 90% of the population risk of a first heart attack. The finding was consistent across all ages, sexes, and world regions.
— pause —
The treatment evidence is equally compelling. The Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' analysis — 26 trials, 170,000 participants — found each 1 mmol/L reduction in LDL cholesterol cut major cardiovascular events by approximately 22%. Each 10 mmHg reduction in systolic blood pressure reduced stroke risk by approximately 27%.
— pause —
And perhaps most importantly — genetic predisposition does not determine cardiovascular outcome. A study of approximately 55,000 people found that those with the highest genetic risk who maintained a favourable lifestyle had approximately 50% lower coronary event rates than those with the same genetic risk but an unfavourable lifestyle. Inherited risk is real — but it is modifiable.
