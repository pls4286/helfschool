Prostate cancer is the most common cancer in UK males.

Around 57,900 new diagnoses every year — that's about 160 men every single day. And the lifetime risk for a UK male is around 1 in 6.

Survival has been transformed. Today, 78.9% of men diagnosed survive ten years or more. In the early 1970s, fewer than 1 in 4 did.

Around 12,200 men still die from it each year — the second most common cause of cancer death in UK males.

▶ ADVANCE NOW — then readSlides 3–4 — What is prostate cancer?

The prostate is a walnut-sized gland sitting just below the bladder in men — it surrounds the urethra and produces part of the fluid in semen.

Prostate cancer happens when cells in that gland start dividing uncontrolled. Almost all cases are slow-growing. But some are more aggressive and can spread.

When a biopsy is taken, the cancer is graded using ISUP grade groups — 1 to 5, with 1 being least aggressive. And staging tells us how far it's spread — localised, locally advanced, or metastatic.

▶ ADVANCE NOW — then readSlides 5–6 — Risk factors and the PSA test

The three main risk factors are age — risk rises sharply after 50 — ethnicity, with clinically recognised higher incidence rates in Black men — and family history, particularly a first-degree relative with prostate cancer.

Importantly, prostate cancer is not linked to any modifiable lifestyle cause. It cannot currently be prevented.

Now — the PSA test. It measures a protein from the prostate. An elevated result can prompt investigation. But here's what's crucial to understand: around 3 in 4 men with a raised PSA will not have cancer. PSA is an imperfect marker. It prompts investigation — it is not a diagnosis.

▶ ADVANCE NOW — then readSlides 7–8 — Diagnosis pathway

Prostate cancer often causes no symptoms at all. When symptoms do occur — urinary difficulty, blood in the urine or semen, or bone pain — or when PSA is elevated, a man is referred urgently to urology.

The first investigation, per NICE guidelines, is an mpMRI scan — a detailed MRI of the prostate. This identifies suspicious areas and guides whether a biopsy is actually needed.

If a biopsy confirms cancer, all cases are reviewed at a multidisciplinary team meeting where the grade, stage, and PSA are combined to risk-stratify the cancer and agree a treatment plan.

▶ ADVANCE NOW — then readSlide 9 — Treatment: localised disease

For low-risk localised disease, the first option is often active surveillance — close monitoring with PSA, MRI, and repeat biopsy. Treatment starts only if the cancer progresses.

For higher-risk localised disease, the main options are radical prostatectomy — surgical removal of the prostate — or radiotherapy. Both can potentially cure the cancer — but both carry significant side effects that differ between them.

▶ ADVANCE NOW — then readSlide 10 — Treatment: advanced disease

For advanced and metastatic disease, hormone therapy — which suppresses testosterone — is the cornerstone of treatment. It's given by injection or daily tablet.

For fit men with newly diagnosed metastatic prostate cancer, adding chemotherapy to hormone therapy from the outset improves survival — as the STAMPEDE trial showed.

▶ ADVANCE NOW — then readSlide 11 — Research: ProtecT + CAP

The ProtecT trial, published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2023, followed 1,643 UK men for 15 years comparing monitoring, surgery, and radiotherapy for localised prostate cancer.

Prostate cancer-specific mortality was 2.7% overall — and statistically similar across all three groups. The key differences between treatments are about side effects, not survival.

The CAP trial, published in JAMA in 2024, randomised over 415,000 UK men to a single PSA test or standard care. At 15 years, prostate cancer mortality was 0.69% in the screened group versus 0.78% without screening — the first UK trial evidence that a single PSA test reduces prostate cancer deaths.

▶ ADVANCE NOW — then readSlide 12 — Research: STAMPEDE

The STAMPEDE trial, published in The Lancet in 2016, showed that adding chemotherapy to hormone therapy extended median overall survival in metastatic prostate cancer from 71 months to 81 months — establishing early chemotherapy alongside hormone therapy as standard care for fit men with newly diagnosed metastatic disease.
