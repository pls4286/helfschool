Migraine
What the evidence shows about triggers, treatment, prevention, and the science behind it.
Dr Paul · Retired NHS GP · helf.school
The scale of migraine in the UK
18.3%
of women in England affected
compared to 7.6% of men in any given year. Peak prevalence in the third and fourth decades of life.
Steiner et al · Cephalalgia · 2003
3rd
highest cause of disability
worldwide in adults under 50 — ranked by Global Burden of Disease. Ahead of diabetes, stroke, and dementia in this age group.
GBD 2015 Study
⚠️
Over 40% of those meeting clinical criteria have never received a formal diagnosis — many living with a treatable neurological condition without targeted care.
What drives attacks · 1 of 2
🔄
Hormonal fluctuation
Oestrogen fluctuation — especially around menstruation — is the most common trigger in women. Migraine often improves significantly after menopause when hormone levels stabilise.
↑↓ Oestrogen
😴
Sleep disruption
Both too little and too much sleep are recognised triggers. Irregular sleep schedules are independently associated with higher attack frequency — consistency matters.
Too little/much
😰
Stress and let-down
Both acute psychological stress and the relaxation phase after sustained stress — the "let-down" effect — are commonly reported triggers that lower the attack threshold.
Stress axis
What drives attacks · 2 of 2
🍽️
Dehydration and skipped meals
Even mild dehydration and missed meals can lower the threshold for an attack. Consistent meal timing and fluid intake are practical, evidence-informed management strategies.
Threshold ↓
🍷
Alcohol — especially red wine
Tyramine and histamine in red wine are among the most consistently reported dietary triggers. Beer and spirits are also cited, though individual responses vary considerably.
Tyramine
💊
Medication overuse
Using acute pain treatments on more than 10 days per month upregulates central pain pathways, progressively worsening attack frequency — a clinically important pattern.
>10 days/mo
What the evidence supports · 1 of 2
⏱️
Treat attacks early
Acute treatments work best when taken at attack onset. Waiting until pain is severe significantly reduces efficacy — earlier treatment means better and faster relief.
Act early
💊
Triptan + NSAID combination
NICE first-line triptan combined with an anti-inflammatory painkiller is clinically superior to either alone — better pain relief and lower rate of headache returning.
1st line
📔
Headache diary
Recording attack dates, duration, triggers, and medication days is the clinical foundation for diagnosis, treatment planning, and monitoring response to preventive treatment.
Track it
What the evidence supports · 2 of 2
🛡️
Preventive treatment at ≥4 attacks/month
Evidence-based oral preventives are available — including a beta-blocker, an anticonvulsant, and a low-dose antidepressant — prescribed based on individual clinical profile.
≥4/month
💉
CGRP monoclonal antibodies
A newer preventive drug class targeting the CGRP pathway — NHS-licensed for those who have not responded to two standard preventive treatment options.
Targeted
⚠️
Limit acute treatment days
Triptans and opioids: no more than 10 days per month. Simple painkillers: no more than 15 days. Overuse itself worsens headache frequency over time.
≤10 days
Research · triptans & CGRP prevention
Key Finding
59%
2-hour headache response / sumatriptan vs 29% on placebo
53 RCTs · 24,089 patients · all 7 oral triptans · Ferrari et al · The Lancet · 2001
Oral triptans — meta-analysis
Ferrari MD et al · The Lancet · 2001;358:1668–1675
Sumatriptan 100mg achieved 59% headache response and 29% complete pain freedom at 2 hours. All seven oral triptans outperformed placebo — establishing triptans as the primary migraine-specific acute treatment.
Key Finding
50%
≥50% reduction in migraine days / erenumab 140mg vs 27% on placebo
955 adults · episodic migraine · 6 months · STRIVE trial · Goadsby et al · NEJM · 2017
STRIVE trial — erenumab prevention
Goadsby PJ et al · N Engl J Med · 2017;377:2123–2132
50% achieved ≥50% fewer monthly migraine days vs 27% on placebo — a 3.7-day mean reduction. The first CGRP antibody trial for migraine prevention, demonstrating targeted efficacy for this class.
How migraine works · 1 of 2
🌊
Cortical spreading depression
Neuronal excitation followed by suppression spreading across the cortex — the likely driver of aura and the trigger that starts the attack cascade.
→ Aura
⚡
Trigeminal activation
The trigeminovascular system — the pain network around the brain and its blood vessels — is activated, releasing inflammatory proteins that sensitise pain pathways.
→ Pain signals
🔴
CGRP release
Calcitonin gene-related peptide — a signalling protein — causes blood vessel widening and amplifies pain signalling. CGRP levels are consistently elevated during attacks.
→ Vasodilation
How treatments target the mechanism · 2 of 2
💊
How triptans work
Triptans bind to serotonin receptors, blocking CGRP release and constricting dilated blood vessels. They also inhibit pain signal transmission along the trigeminal pathway.
↓ CGRP, pain
💉
How CGRP antibodies work
Block the CGRP receptor or the peptide itself — preventing the pain cascade from initiating. Monthly dosing provides sustained receptor blockade between attacks.
→ Prevention
🔄
Medication overuse mechanism
Repeated acute treatment upregulates central pain pathways and lowers the attack threshold — withdrawal of the overused drug reduces this sensitisation progressively over weeks.
→ Sensitisation
