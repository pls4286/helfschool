Migraine
What the evidence shows about triggers, acute treatment, prevention, and the science behind it.
Dr Paul · Retired NHS GP · helf.school
The big picture · migraine in the UK
18.3%

affected in any given year — compared to 7.6% of men. Peak prevalence is in the third and fourth decades of life.
Steiner et al · Cephalalgia · 2003
3rd
highest cause of disability
worldwide in adults under 50 — ranked by the Global Burden of Disease Study. Ahead of diabetes, stroke, and dementia in this age group.
GBD 2015 · ICHD-3
⚠️
Migraine is substantially under-diagnosed — over 40% of those meeting clinical criteria have never received a formal diagnosis.
What drives and triggers attacks
🔄
Hormonal fluctuation
Oestrogen fluctuation — especially around menstruation — is the most common trigger in women. Migraine often improves after menopause.
↑ Oestrogen ↓
😴
Sleep disruption
Both too little and too much sleep are recognised triggers. Irregular sleep schedules are independently associated with higher attack frequency.
↑ / ↓ Sleep
😰
Stress and let-down
Acute psychological stress and the relaxation phase after a period of sustained stress (the "let-down" effect) are both commonly reported triggers.
Stress axis
🍽️
Dehydration and skipped meals
Even mild dehydration and missed meals can lower the threshold for an attack — consistent meal and fluid timing is a practical management strategy.
Threshold ↓
🍷
Alcohol (especially red wine)
Tyramine and histamine in red wine are among the most commonly reported dietary triggers. Beer and spirits are also cited, though effects are individual.
Tyramine
💊
Medication overuse
Using acute treatments more than 10 days per month (triptans/opioids) upregulates central pain pathways, progressively worsening attack frequency.
>10 days/mo
What the evidence supports
⏱️
Early acute treatment
Triptans and analgesics work best when taken at attack onset — waiting until pain is severe significantly reduces efficacy.
Act early
💊
Sumatriptan (Imigran) + NSAID
NICE first-line triptan; combination with an NSAID is clinically superior to either alone — better pain relief and lower recurrence.
1st line
📔
Headache diary
Records attack dates, duration, triggers, and medication days — the clinical foundation for diagnosis, treatment planning, and monitoring.
Track it
🛡️
Preventive treatment
Considered at ≥4 attacks per month. First-line oral options: propranolol (Inderal), topiramate (Topamax), amitriptyline (Tryptizol).
≥4/month
💉
CGRP monoclonal antibodies
Erenumab (Aimovig), fremanezumab (Ajovy), galcanezumab (Emgality) — licensed on NHS for those who have failed ≥2 standard preventives.
Targeted
⚠️
Avoid medication overuse
Triptans and opioids: limit to ≤10 days per month. Simple analgesics: ≤15 days per month. Overuse worsens long-term headache frequency.
≤10 days

Oral triptans — meta-analysis of 53 trials
Ferrari MD et al · The Lancet · 2001;358:1668–1675
53 RCTs, 24,089 patients. Sumatriptan 100mg achieved 59% headache response (pain reduced to mild or none) and 29% complete pain freedom at 2 hours. All seven oral triptans demonstrated superiority over placebo. This meta-analysis established the evidence base for triptans as the primary migraine-specific acute treatment in clinical guidelines worldwide.
Key Finding
50%
patients ≥50% reduction in migraine days
erenumab 140mg vs 27% placebo · 3.7-day reduction from 8.3 baseline days
STRIVE trial — erenumab for episodic migraine
Goadsby PJ et al · N Engl J Med · 2017;377:2123–2132
955 adults with episodic migraine randomised to monthly erenumab (70mg or 140mg) or placebo for 6 months. At 140mg, 50% achieved ≥50% reduction in monthly migraine days vs 27% on placebo. Mean reduction: 3.7 days from 8.3 baseline. The first phase III trial of a CGRP-pathway antibody — establishing a specifically targeted class for migraine prevention.
How migraine works — and how treatments target it
🌊
Cortical spreading depression
A wave of neuronal excitation followed by suppression spreads across the cortex — the likely driver of aura and an early trigger of the attack cascade.
→ Aura
⚡
Trigeminal activation
The trigeminovascular system is activated, releasing inflammatory neuropeptides — including CGRP — that sensitise pain pathways around the brain.
→ Pain signals
🔴
CGRP release
Calcitonin gene-related peptide causes vasodilation and amplifies pain signalling — levels are consistently elevated during attacks and normalise after resolution.
→ Vasodilation
💊
How triptans work
Bind to 5-HT1B/1D receptors — blocking CGRP release, causing vasoconstriction of dilated vessels, and inhibiting pain signal transmission in the trigeminal pathway.
→ ↓ CGRP, pain
💉
How CGRP antibodies work
Block the CGRP receptor or the peptide itself — preventing the cascade from initiating. Monthly dosing provides sustained receptor blockade between attacks.
→ Prevention
🔄
Medication overuse mechanism
Repeated acute treatment upregulates central pain pathways and lowers the attack threshold — withdrawal of the overused drug reduces sensitisation over weeks.
→ Sensitisation
