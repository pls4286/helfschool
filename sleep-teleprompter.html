Segment 1 — How much sleep do you actually need?

"Let's start with the question everyone asks first. How much sleep do I actually need?" PAUSE

"For most adults, the NHS recommendation is between seven and nine hours a night. That's it. It's not eight. Eight is just the average — some people genuinely do fine on seven, others need every minute of nine."

"What matters more than hitting an exact number is how you feel during the day. If you're constantly tired, reaching for caffeine just to function, or falling asleep when you sit still — you're probably not getting enough good-quality sleep, whatever the clock says." PAUSE

"Those numbers shift across life. Newborns need up to seventeen hours a day. Teenagers really do need eight to ten — their brains are rewiring. Older adults often sleep a bit less, and that's usually normal, not a problem to fix."

Segment 2 — Sleep is not a pause button

"Here's something worth knowing. Sleep is not the hours you lose. Biologically, it's almost the opposite. Sleep is one of the most active, tightly regulated things your body does." PAUSE

"While you're asleep, your brain is clearing out waste products, moving memories from short-term storage into long-term storage, and resetting the hormones that control your appetite, your blood pressure, and your blood sugar for the next day."

"A good night isn't one flat state. You cycle through four stages, over and over. Light sleep — the doorway. Deeper light sleep — the bulk of the night. Deep sleep, also called slow-wave sleep — that's when the body physically repairs itself. And then REM — dreaming sleep — when the brain processes emotion and consolidates learning." PAUSE

"Each cycle takes about ninety minutes, and most adults get through four to six of them a night. Here's the important bit: most of your deep sleep comes in the first half of the night, and most of your REM comes in the second half. Which is why — when you cut sleep short in the morning — the thing you lose most is REM."

Segment 3 — Why more isn't better

"You might assume that if seven to nine hours is the target, then more sleep is always better. It isn't." PAUSE

"When researchers pool together large studies — millions of people followed for years — they consistently find a J-shaped curve. Risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, even death — rises on both sides of the optimum."

"Too little sleep — under seven hours — raises risk. Too much sleep — over nine — also raises risk. The lowest point sits right around seven hours a night. That's where the data lands, again and again."

"Now — I'll give you the honest picture on long sleep. A lot of the long-sleep signal in the data isn't long sleep causing harm. It's long sleep being a marker of something else going on — an underlying illness, a depressive episode, a chronic condition that's making someone sleep more. So long sleep is a flag, not necessarily a cause." PAUSE

"Short sleep, on the other hand, does have direct causal effects. Even a single week of restricted sleep, in otherwise healthy adults, produces measurable changes in blood sugar control, blood pressure, and appetite hormones."

Segment 4 — Five ways poor sleep catches up with you

"So — what does persistent poor sleep actually do? Five big ones, in the research." PAUSE

"One — your heart. Short sleep is linked to higher blood pressure, higher inflammation, more atherosclerosis — the furring of the arteries — and more heart attacks and strokes over time."

"Two — your metabolism. Short sleep reduces insulin sensitivity within days. In large meta-analyses, sleeping under seven hours or over eight hours is linked to a meaningfully higher risk of developing type 2 diabetes."

"Three — your brain. This one really matters. Deep sleep is when the brain clears out waste, including some of the proteins that build up in Alzheimer's disease. The Whitehall II study in the UK followed nearly 8,000 people for 25 years, and found that persistent short sleep in midlife was linked to a 30% higher dementia risk, independent of other factors." PAUSE

"Four — your mood. Insomnia and depression run both ways. Poor sleep worsens depression and anxiety — and is an independent risk factor for developing them. Treating sleep is now seen as part of treating mood, not a side issue."

"Five — overall mortality. The largest meta-analyses show that both persistent short sleep and persistent long sleep track with higher all-cause mortality." PAUSE

Segment 5 — What your doctor can actually do

"If sleep is a real problem for you — the good news is, this is treatable. Here's what a good GP consultation actually looks like." PAUSE

"First — a two-week sleep diary. Simple as it sounds. Bedtime, time to fall asleep, any waking, rise time, how rested you felt. Do it before your appointment. It's a more powerful bit of information than anything a tracker will give you." PAUSE

"Second — if you snore loudly and feel tired by day, your GP should screen for obstructive sleep apnoea. This is extraordinarily common — recent UK estimates suggest something like one in five adults may have it, and most are undiagnosed. Treatment — usually with a CPAP mask at night — can be genuinely life-changing." PAUSE

"Third — for long-standing insomnia, the NHS first-line treatment is not sleeping tablets. It's a talking therapy called CBT-I — cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia. It works at least as well as tablets short-term, and much better long-term, because the effects last after you stop. You can get it face-to-face, in a group, or through digital apps on the NHS."

"And fourth — on sleeping tablets. I want to be honest with you. Z-drugs and benzodiazepines — zopiclone, zolpidem, and the older benzodiazepines — are licensed for up to four weeks. Used long-term, they lose their effect, they cause dependence, and they significantly raise the risk of falls and fractures, particularly in older people. If you're on them long-term, that's nothing to feel judged about — speak to your GP about a gentle, planned taper."

Segment 6 — The evidence, very briefly

"Quick tour of the key evidence. PAUSE The Yin meta-analysis in the Journal of the American Heart Association in 2017 — over three and a half million people — is where the seven-hour optimum comes from."

"The Cappuccio group at the University of Warwick, in the journal SLEEP in 2010, showed short sleep comes with a 12% higher death risk, and long sleep a 30% higher risk." PAUSE

"The Shan meta-analysis, Diabetes Care 2015 — each hour below seven is linked to 9% more type 2 diabetes, each hour above eight is linked to 14% more."

"The Whitehall II dementia study, in Nature Communications in 2021 — 25 years of follow-up, 30% higher dementia risk with persistent short sleep from age 50."

"And the van Straten CBT-I meta-analysis in Sleep Medicine Reviews in 2018 — 87 trials — is the evidence base behind the NICE first-line recommendation." PAUSE

Segment 7 — Three practical things to do

"Three things to take away. PAUSE If your sleep is something you want to improve — here's where to start." PAUSE

"One — today. Start a two-week sleep diary. Pen and paper. Five things each morning — roughly when you went to bed, how long it took you to fall asleep, whether you woke in the night, when you got up, and how rested you felt. After two weeks you'll see a pattern you didn't know was there."
