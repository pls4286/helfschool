▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — How much sleep · 7–9 hours
Let's start with the question everyone asks first. How much sleep does an adult actually need?
— pause —
For most adults, the NHS recommendation is between seven and nine hours a night. That's it. It's not eight. Eight is just the average — some people genuinely do fine on seven, others need every minute of nine. What matters more than hitting an exact number is how someone feels during the day. Constant tiredness, reliance on caffeine just to function, falling asleep when sitting still — these are recognised markers of insufficient or poor-quality sleep, whatever the clock says.
— pause —
Those numbers shift across life. Newborns need up to seventeen hours a day. Teenagers need eight to ten — their brains are rewiring. Older adults often sleep a bit less, and that's usually within normal range rather than a problem requiring intervention.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Sleep architecture
Here's something worth knowing. Sleep is not the hours you lose. Biologically, it's almost the opposite — sleep is one of the most active, tightly regulated things the body does.
— pause —
During sleep, the brain clears out waste products, consolidates memories from short-term into long-term storage, and resets the hormones that regulate appetite, blood pressure, and blood sugar. A good night isn't one flat state. There are four stages, cycling over and over. Light sleep — the doorway. Deeper light sleep — the bulk of the night. Deep sleep, or slow-wave sleep — when the body physically repairs itself. And REM — dreaming sleep — when the brain processes emotion and consolidates learning.
— pause —
Each cycle takes about ninety minutes, and most adults complete four to six cycles a night. Most deep sleep comes in the first half of the night, most REM in the second. Which is why cutting sleep short in the morning specifically reduces REM.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — The J-curve
The assumption that more sleep is always better doesn't hold up in the evidence.
— pause —
When researchers pool large studies — millions of people followed for years — a J-shaped curve consistently emerges. Risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, even all-cause mortality — rises on both sides of the optimum. Too little sleep — under seven hours — raises risk. Too much sleep — over nine — also raises risk. The lowest point sits right around seven hours a night. That pattern appears again and again across independent datasets.
— pause —
On long sleep — a lot of that signal in the data isn't long sleep causing harm. It's long sleep being a marker of something else — an underlying illness, depression, a chronic condition. Long sleep is a flag worth noting, not necessarily a cause. Short sleep, on the other hand, does have direct causal effects. Even a single week of restricted sleep in otherwise healthy adults produces measurable changes in blood sugar control, blood pressure, and appetite hormones.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–6 — Five impact areas
What does persistent poor sleep actually do? Five big areas in the research.
— pause —
One — the heart. Short sleep is linked to higher blood pressure, higher inflammation, more atherosclerosis, and more cardiovascular events over time. Two — metabolism. Short sleep reduces insulin sensitivity within days. In large meta-analyses, sleeping under seven hours or over eight hours is linked to a meaningfully higher risk of type 2 diabetes. Three — the brain. This one is particularly significant. Deep sleep is when the brain clears out waste — including some of the proteins that accumulate in Alzheimer's disease. The Whitehall II study followed nearly 8,000 people for 25 years, finding that persistent short sleep in midlife was linked to a 30% higher dementia risk, independent of other factors.
— pause —
Four — mood. Insomnia and depression are bidirectionally linked. Poor sleep worsens depression and anxiety and is an independent risk factor for developing them. Sleep is now seen as part of managing mood, not a side issue. Five — overall mortality. The largest meta-analyses show that both persistent short sleep and persistent long sleep track with higher all-cause mortality.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 7–8 — Doctor's toolkit
When sleep difficulties are raised in UK practice, a structured assessment is the starting point — not a prescription pad.
— pause —
A two-week sleep diary is one of the most clinically useful tools — bedtime, time to fall asleep, any waking, rise time, how rested. The pattern it reveals is often more informative than anything a tracker produces. Where loud snoring is present alongside daytime tiredness, UK practice is to screen for obstructive sleep apnoea — a condition that is very common, very underdiagnosed, and very treatable. Recent UK estimates suggest something like one in five adults may have it.
— pause —
For long-standing insomnia, the NHS first-line treatment is not sleeping tablets. It's cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia — CBT-I. It works at least as well as tablets short-term, and much better long-term, because the effects persist after treatment ends. NHS access is available through GPs, NHS Talking Therapies, and digital programmes like Sleepio in some UK regions. On sleeping tablets — z-drugs and benzodiazepines are licensed for up to four weeks. Used long-term, they lose their effect and carry a significantly higher risk of falls and fractures, particularly in older adults. A planned, gradual reduction is something GPs routinely support.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 9–10 — Research evidence
A quick tour of the key evidence.
— pause —
The Yin meta-analysis in the Journal of the American Heart Association in 2017 — over three and a half million people — is where the seven-hour optimum comes from. The Cappuccio group at Warwick, in SLEEP in 2010, showed short sleep is associated with a 12% higher death risk, long sleep with a 30% higher risk. The Shan meta-analysis in Diabetes Care 2015 — each hour below seven is linked to 9% more type 2 diabetes, each hour above eight to 14% more. The Whitehall II dementia study in Nature Communications 2021 — 25 years of follow-up, 30% higher dementia risk with persistent short sleep from age 50. And the van Straten CBT-I meta-analysis in Sleep Medicine Reviews 2018 — 87 trials — is the evidence base behind the NICE first-line recommendation.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 11 — What the evidence shows
So — what does the evidence tell us?
— pause —
First, the core finding. Around 7 hours is the J-curve low point — where cardiovascular and all-cause mortality risk is lowest across millions of people in pooled data. And from the Whitehall II study — persistent short sleep from midlife is linked to 30% higher dementia risk 25 years later, independent of other risk factors.
— pause —
On mechanism — short sleep reduces insulin sensitivity within days. That's a direct metabolic effect, not just tiredness. And on treatment — CBT-I outperforms sleeping tablets on every long-term outcome. The effects persist after treatment ends. Tablets lose their effect and carry real dependence and fall risk with longer use.
— pause —
In practice — a two-week sleep diary is the right starting point. It reveals patterns that memory simply cannot, and gives your GP something concrete to work with. And loud snoring with daytime tiredness is a recognised sign of OSA — very common, very underdiagnosed, and highly treatable through primary care.
