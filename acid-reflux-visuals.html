Acid Reflux —
the burning truth
What it is, what causes it, and what the evidence says works — from lifestyle through to medication.

How acid reflux actually happens
✓ Normal
✗ GORD
🔒
LOS — lower oesophageal sphincter
Muscular valve at the base of the gullet. Opens for food, then closes tightly. In GORD it fails — letting acid back up.
🔥
Heartburn
Burning behind the breastbone — acid irritating the oesophageal lining. Nothing to do with the heart.
🏥
GORD
Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease — when reflux is frequent enough to cause symptoms or damage.
What pushes the LOS open — risk factors (1 of 2)
⚖️
Excess weight
Abdominal pressure pushes stomach contents upwards — the biggest modifiable risk factor for GORD.
→ Pressure
🕳️
Hiatus hernia
Part of the stomach slides up through the diaphragm, disrupting the sphincter mechanism.
→ Anatomy
🚬
Smoking
Weakens the LOS and reduces saliva — which normally neutralises stray acid in the oesophagus.
→ Weakens LOS
What pushes the LOS open — risk factors (2 of 2)
🤰
Pregnancy
Hormonal relaxation of the sphincter plus a growing uterus raising abdominal pressure. Often resolves after delivery.
→ Hormones
💊
Medications
NSAIDs, calcium channel blockers, some antidepressants, and bisphosphonates can all worsen reflux.
→ Drug effect
🍽️
Meal timing & size
Large meals, eating late, and lying flat soon after eating are all associated with increased acid escape.
→ Behaviour

Most GORD never reaches Barrett's. Barrett's affects ~10–15% of those with chronic, longstanding symptoms, and the annual adenocarcinoma risk from Barrett's is around 0.33% per year — real but low. NHS surveillance catches changes early when treatment is most effective.
What your doctor might do — stepped approach (1 of 2)
⚖️
Lifestyle first — weight, meal timing, alcohol, smoking
Weight loss is the most evidence-based lifestyle step. Late meals, large portions, and lying flat are all associated with increased reflux. A raised bed head reduces night-time symptoms.
1st line
💊
Antacids & alginates — Gaviscon, Rennies
Immediate relief; safe in pregnancy; best for mild or infrequent symptoms. Over-the-counter.
As needed
🟡
H2 receptor antagonists — famotidine (Pepcid)
Reduce acid production. Useful when PPIs not tolerated, or for night-time breakthrough acid while on PPI.
Step up
What your doctor might do — stepped approach (2 of 2)
🟠
Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) — omeprazole (Losec), lansoprazole (Zoton/Prevacid)
Most potent acid suppression. 30–60 min before meals — the proton pump is activated by eating. Lowest effective dose long-term.
1st pharmac
🔬
Endoscopy if alarm features
Difficulty swallowing · unexplained weight loss · vomiting blood · persistent vomiting — clinically recognised as time-sensitive. 111 or urgent GP access exists for this.
111 / urgent
🛡️
Barrett's surveillance · surgery in selected cases
Periodic endoscopy for confirmed Barrett's. Fundoplication (anti-reflux surgery) considered for severe, refractory GORD — evidence equivalent to long-term PPI.
Specialist
What the evidence shows · treatment
Key Finding
Higher
healing & relief / PPIs vs H2-receptor antagonists
all severity grades · 134 trials · 35,978 participants
Cochrane — PPIs vs H2-receptor antagonists
Cochrane Database · 134 trials · 35,978 participants
PPIs produced significantly higher healing rates and symptom relief across all severity grades versus H2-receptor antagonists — the evidence underpinning their first-line use in GORD.
Key Finding
~85%
remission / surgery vs esomeprazole (Nexium/Emozul)
equivalent outcomes at 5 years · LOTUS · 554 patients
LOTUS — anti-reflux surgery vs esomeprazole (Nexium/Emozul)
Galmiche et al. · JAMA · 2011 · 554 patients · 5-year follow-up
Both arms achieved ~85–90% remission at 5 years — equivalent outcomes. Surgery works as well as long-term medication; the choice depends on individual circumstances.
What the evidence shows · lifestyle & risk
Key Finding
~3.5 kg
symptom reduction / even modest weight loss
GORD frequency · Nurses' Health Study · ~10,000 women
Jacobson et al. — weight & GORD symptoms
NEJM · 2006 · Nurses' Health Study · ~10,000 women
Even modest weight loss (~3.5 kg) significantly reduced reflux symptom frequency. Weight is the most evidence-based lifestyle intervention for GORD.
Key Finding
0.33%
annual cancer risk from Barrett's oesophagus
much lower than earlier estimates · 57 studies · 58,000+ patients
Desai et al. — Barrett's cancer progression risk
Gut · 2012 · meta-analysis · 57 studies · 58,000+ patients
Annual adenocarcinoma risk in Barrett's is ~0.33% per year — much lower than earlier estimates. Risk is real but low — supports proportionate surveillance rather than alarm.
