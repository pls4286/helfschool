▶ Slides 2–3 — What is breast awareness? · ADVANCE NOW — then read
So what is breast awareness — and how is it different from what you might have heard before? For many years, women were encouraged to perform formal monthly breast self-examination — a specific technique, at a set time each month. The clinical evidence, though, found no reduction in breast cancer mortality from that kind of scheduled routine. So NICE — the body that sets UK clinical guidelines — now describes a different approach. Not a technique. Not a schedule. Just familiarity with how your own body normally looks and feels. And that's the key — your normal. Because what's normal varies enormously between people. Breast tissue often becomes more tender or lumpier before a period and settles again afterwards. It's clinically normal for one breast to be a slightly different size or shape from the other. After the menopause, breast tissue often feels softer and less dense. All of that is within the range of normal. What breast awareness is really about is building enough familiarity with your own body that anything genuinely new or different becomes noticeable.
▶ Slides 4–5 — Changes worth assessment · ADVANCE NOW — then read
So what kinds of changes are clinically recognised as worth assessment? There are six main ones — and I want to be clear: most breast changes have a benign explanation. Knowing these doesn't mean every change is significant. It means knowing what falls outside the range of normal variation. First — a new lump or area of thickening in the breast, chest, or armpit, particularly one that feels harder than the surrounding tissue or doesn't change with the menstrual cycle. Second — a change in size, shape, or outline of one or both breasts — any new visible difference that wasn't there before. Third — a change in skin texture: dimpling, puckering, a rash, or an orange-peel surface that clinicians call peau d'orange. Then: changes to a nipple — new inversion, discharge that isn't breast milk, or a change in position or appearance. Persistent pain in one area that doesn't follow the menstrual cycle and doesn't go away. And redness, warmth, or unexplained swelling — because not all clinically significant changes come as a detectable lump.
▶ Slide 6 — NHS Screening Programme · ADVANCE NOW — then read
Now — breast awareness and the NHS Breast Screening Programme do different things, and they complement each other rather than replace each other. The screening programme invites everyone aged 50 to 70 for mammography — an X-ray of breast tissue — every three years. Mammography can detect changes too small to feel — that's its clinical value. It finds things early, before symptoms develop. But routine screening is every three years. Breast awareness is ongoing. It's what fills the gap between appointments — and it's relevant for everyone, not just those within the screening age range.
▶ Slide 7 — Research · ADVANCE NOW — then read
The clinical guidance on this is clear. NICE NG101 — the national guideline on breast cancer diagnosis and management — does not recommend formal monthly self-examination. Large randomised trials found no survival advantage from a scheduled checking routine compared with no routine at all. What NICE describes instead is breast awareness. Familiarity with your own normal. Noticing change. Not a technique. Not a calendar. Just knowing your body.
▶ Slide 8 — What the evidence shows · ADVANCE NOW — then read
So — to bring this together. Breast awareness is about knowing what's normal for you — not following a set routine or technique. Six types of change are clinically recognised as worth assessment — from new lumps and skin texture changes to nipple changes and persistent localised pain. The NHS screening programme invites those aged 50 to 70 for mammography every three years — breast awareness is what works alongside it, not instead of it. And the NHS has a useful free video guide to checking technique — available at nhs.uk. Anything personally relevant is a conversation for you to have with your GP or healthcare professional.
