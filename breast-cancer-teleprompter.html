Breast cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer in UK women. Around 59,000 new cases every year — and about 1 in 7 women in the UK will receive a diagnosis at some point in their lifetime.

The first thing to understand is that breast cancer is not one disease. It falls into distinct types based on receptor status — which proteins the cancer cells carry.

ER-positive disease — the most common, around 80% of cases — uses oestrogen to fuel growth, and responds well to hormone-blocking treatments.

HER2-positive disease — in approximately 15 to 20% of cases — overproduces a growth protein and responds to targeted antibody treatment given alongside chemotherapy.

And triple-negative breast cancer — in 10 to 15% of cases — lacks all three receptors, making it the most challenging to treat.

Receptor status determines everything — which treatment is offered, how long it runs, and what the likely response will be.

These figures tell an important story — not just about scale, but about progress.

Mortality has fallen by 46% since the early 1970s. And 85% of breast cancers in England are now diagnosed at Stage I or II — the stages where five-year survival is 90% or above.

Stage at diagnosis is the single most powerful driver of outcome. Stage I — almost 100% five-year survival. Stage IV — the picture is very different. Early detection genuinely saves lives.

So how is breast cancer diagnosed? The standard UK pathway is called the triple assessment — three components that together give a complete picture.

First, clinical examination — the breast and nearby lymph nodes assessed by a trained clinician.

Second, imaging — mammography for women aged 40 and over; ultrasound for younger women where denser breast tissue is harder to read on X-ray.

Third, biopsy — a tissue sample that confirms the diagnosis and determines receptor status. Biopsy is the only way to know for certain whether cancer is present — and which type.

The NHS Breast Screening Programme invites women aged 50 to 70 for mammography every three years — specifically to detect cancers before symptoms appear, when treatment is most effective.

Treatment for breast cancer is increasingly personalised — determined by the type of cancer, its stage, and its receptor status.

For most early breast cancers, surgery is the cornerstone. Breast-conserving surgery removes the tumour and a margin of tissue. Mastectomy removes the whole breast. Radiotherapy reduces the risk of the cancer returning locally.

For ER-positive disease — the majority of cases — hormone-blocking treatment follows. A selective oestrogen receptor modulator for premenopausal women; aromatase inhibitors for postmenopausal women. Typically for five to ten years.

For HER2-positive disease, targeted antibody treatment is added to chemotherapy for a full year. For triple-negative disease, systemic chemotherapy is the main systemic option.

The evidence base for breast cancer treatment is among the strongest in oncology.

The Marmot review of 2012 — a meta-analysis of 11 randomised controlled trials — found that women invited to screening had a 20% reduction in breast cancer mortality. Around 1,300 deaths prevented each year by the NHS programme.

The same review found that for every 10,000 women invited to screening for 20 years, 129 cases represent overdiagnosis — cancers that would not have caused problems during the woman's lifetime. That tension between benefit and harm is a clinically recognised and ongoing discussion.

On treatment — the Early Breast Cancer Trialists' Collaborative Group analysed more than 21,000 women. Five years of hormone-blocking treatment reduces breast cancer mortality by about a third throughout the first 15 years in ER-positive disease.

A separate meta-analysis of nearly 32,000 women found that five years of aromatase inhibitors reduces 10-year mortality by about 40% compared with no endocrine treatment.

So — what does all of this tell us?

The NHS Breast Screening Programme invites women aged fifty to seventy every three years. The Marmot review of eleven trials showed a twenty per cent reduction in breast cancer mortality in women invited to screening.

On awareness — changes in shape, skin, nipple, or any new lump are changes worth raising with a GP or healthcare professional.

Receptor status — ER-positive, HER2-positive, or triple-negative — determines the treatment pathway. It's determined at biopsy and shapes everything that follows.

On the big picture: mortality has fallen forty-six per cent since the early 1970s. Stage one breast cancer has around one hundred per cent five-year survival.
