Myth vs Evidence
Two things people commonly get wrong
Common misconceptions — and what the published evidence actually shows
📱 The claim
"Blood in my stool is nearly always piles — it's not worth mentioning to a doctor."
✓ The evidence
Blood in or on the stool is one of the primary red flag symptoms that triggers urgent bowel cancer investigation under NICE guidance. Rectal bleeding without an obvious cause, particularly combined with any change in bowel habit, warrants clinical assessment.
NICE NG12 · Suspected cancer: recognition and referral, 2023
📱 The claim
"The NHS bowel screening test is unpleasant and invasive — not worth bothering with."
✓ The evidence
The NHS FIT test is a home stool test — no hospital visit, no preparation required. Clinical trial evidence shows bowel screening reduces cancer incidence by 18–31%. Around 40% of eligible adults still don't complete the test when sent one.
Bretthauer M et al. NEJM 2022 · PMID 36214590
Section 1 · What is it? (1 of 2)
What is bowel cancer?
A cancer of the large bowel — and why how it develops matters
🫀
A cancer of the colon or rectum
Bowel cancer begins when cells lining the large bowel divide out of control, forming a malignant tumour. It can arise in the colon or the rectum — which together make up the large bowel.
🔬
More than 95% are adenocarcinomas
Adenocarcinoma — glandular cell cancer — accounts for over 95% of all bowel cancers. Rarer subtypes make up the remainder.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
🌱
Most develop slowly from polyps over 10–15 years
Most bowel cancers develop slowly from polyps over 10 to 15 years. Removing those polyps during screening can prevent cancer from ever developing.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
Section 1 · What is it? (2 of 2)
Risk factors and molecular testing
Why diet matters — and why every tumour is tested at diagnosis
🥩
Diet and lifestyle drive most cases
Processed meat, obesity, physical inactivity, alcohol, and smoking all raise bowel cancer risk. Around 75% of cases have no significant family history.
Bouvard V et al. Lancet Oncol 2015 · PMID 26514947
📮
The NHS FIT screening test
The NHS FIT test detects blood in stool from a home sample — no hospital visit needed. A positive result leads to colonoscopy.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
🧬
Every tumour is molecularly tested at diagnosis
Every bowel cancer is tested for KRAS, NRAS, BRAF, and MSI status. Results determine whether targeted therapy, immunotherapy, or chemotherapy is most appropriate.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
Section 2 · Why does it matter?
The scale — and the stage gap
Why bowel cancer demands attention — and why earlier diagnosis changes everything
📊
~44,000 new cases per year — 4th most common UK cancer
Around 120 new bowel cancer diagnoses every day. Rates are rising in under-50s, with around 2,600 cases in this age group each year.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
⚠️
48 deaths every day — the 2nd biggest cancer killer
Around 17,700 bowel cancer deaths per year — the UK's 2nd biggest cancer killer. Overall five-year survival is around 60%.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
🎯
Stage 1: more than 90% survive 5+ years
Stage 1 five-year survival exceeds 90% — but falls to 8% at stage 4. Around 26% are still found at stage 4.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
Section 3 · What your doctor might discuss (1 of 2)
Getting to a diagnosis
From first symptoms to confirmed staging
🔍
Red flags that prompt urgent investigation
NICE NG12 flags persistent change in bowel habit, blood in stool, unexplained anaemia, abdominal lump, and weight loss as triggers for urgent assessment.
NICE NG12 · Suspected cancer: recognition and referral, 2023
🏥
FIT test → colonoscopy → biopsy
A positive FIT leads to colonoscopy — a camera examination allowing biopsy and polyp removal in one procedure. Tissue confirms the cancer type.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
📋
Staging determines the treatment plan
Staged 1 to 4 based on tumour depth, lymph nodes, and spread. Stage determines whether treatment aims to cure or to control.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
Section 3 · What your doctor might discuss (2 of 2)
Treatment — matched to the tumour's biology
Surgery, chemotherapy, and targeted drugs are selected by molecular profile
⚕️
Surgery — the primary treatment for early disease
Surgery is the primary treatment for early-stage disease, with the procedure depending on location. For rectal cancer, chemoradiotherapy before surgery reduces recurrence.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
💊
Chemotherapy for stage 3 and metastatic disease
FOLFOX — oxaliplatin plus fluorouracil — is standard for stage 3. For metastatic disease, targeted agents are added based on the molecular profile.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
🛡️
Immunotherapy for MSI-H tumours
Around 3–5% of metastatic cases carry MSI-H status, making them highly sensitive to immunotherapy. A checkpoint inhibitor is now the standard first-line treatment.
André T et al. NEJM 2020 · PMID 33264544
Section 4 · What the research shows (1 of 2)
Immunotherapy and screening — the evidence
Key Finding

median PFS / immunotherapy vs 8.2 months on chemotherapy
MSI-H/dMMR metastatic CRC · first-line · KEYNOTE-177
KEYNOTE-177 — immunotherapy leads in MSI-H bowel cancer

Immunotherapy was compared with standard chemotherapy as first-line treatment in patients with MSI-H/dMMR metastatic bowel cancer. Progression-free survival was 16.5 months versus 8.2 months on chemotherapy, with overall survival also significantly better.

André T et al. N Engl J Med 2020;383(23):2207–2218 · PMID 33264544
Key Finding
18%
lower bowel cancer incidence / colonoscopy screening vs no screening
85,000+ adults · 10-year follow-up · NordICC trial
NordICC — colonoscopy screening reduces bowel cancer incidence

The NordICC trial invited over 85,000 adults to colonoscopy screening or no screening and followed them for 10 years. Bowel cancer incidence was 18% lower in the invited group — rising to 31% lower among those who actually attended.

Bretthauer M et al. N Engl J Med 2022;387(17):1547–1556 · PMID 36214590
Section 4 · What the research shows (2 of 2)
Diet and bowel cancer risk
Key Finding
+18%
higher bowel cancer risk / for each additional 50g of processed meat per day
IARC Working Group · 800+ studies · Lancet Oncol 2015
IARC — processed meat classified as a Group 1 carcinogen

An IARC Working Group classified processed meat as a Group 1 carcinogen for bowel cancer after reviewing over 800 studies. Each additional 50g per day — roughly two rashers of bacon — raises risk by approximately 18%, starting from low intake levels. This dose-response relationship begins from zero, meaning any regular daily intake carries more risk than none.

Bouvard V et al. Lancet Oncol 2015;16(16):1599–1600 · PMID 26514947
NICE NG12 · IARC · Cancer Research UK
