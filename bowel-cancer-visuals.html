Myth vs Evidence
Two things people commonly get wrong
Common misconceptions — and what the published evidence actually shows
📱 The claim
"Blood in my stool is nearly always piles — it's not worth mentioning to a doctor."
✓ The evidence
Blood in or on the stool is one of the primary red flag symptoms that triggers urgent bowel cancer investigation under NICE guidance. Rectal bleeding without an obvious cause, particularly combined with any change in bowel habit, warrants clinical assessment.
NICE NG12 · Suspected cancer: recognition and referral, 2023
📱 The claim
"The NHS bowel screening test is unpleasant and invasive — not worth bothering with."
✓ The evidence
The NHS FIT test is a home stool test — no hospital visit, no preparation required. Clinical trial evidence shows bowel screening reduces cancer incidence by 18–31%. Around 40% of eligible adults still don't complete the test when sent one.
Bretthauer M et al. NEJM 2022 · PMID 36214590
Section 1 · What is it? (1 of 2)
What is bowel cancer?
A cancer of the large bowel — and why how it develops matters
🫀
A cancer of the colon or rectum
Bowel cancer begins when cells in the lining of the large bowel — either the colon or the rectum — start dividing in an uncontrolled way, forming a malignant tumour. The colon absorbs water from undigested food; the rectum stores waste before elimination.
🔬
More than 95% are adenocarcinomas
Adenocarcinoma — cancer of the glandular cells lining the bowel wall — accounts for over 95% of all bowel cancers. Neuroendocrine tumours, lymphomas, and gastrointestinal stromal tumours make up the remainder, each treated differently.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
🌱
Most develop slowly from polyps over 10–15 years
Most bowel cancers develop from benign polyps (adenomas) — small growths on the bowel lining. This slow development is precisely why screening works: removing polyps before they turn cancerous can prevent bowel cancer from developing at all.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
Section 1 · What is it? (2 of 2)
Risk factors and molecular testing
Why diet matters — and why every tumour is tested at diagnosis
🥩
Diet and lifestyle drive most cases
Processed meat, obesity, physical inactivity, alcohol, and smoking all independently increase bowel cancer risk. Around 75% of cases occur in people with no significant family history — lifestyle is the dominant driver.
Bouvard V et al. Lancet Oncol 2015 · PMID 26514947
📮
The NHS FIT screening test
The faecal immunochemical test (FIT) detects microscopic blood in stool from a home sample — no hospital visit required. Offered every 2 years to eligible adults aged 50–74 in England. A positive FIT leads to colonoscopy for further investigation.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
🧬
Every tumour is tested at diagnosis
KRAS, NRAS, BRAF mutations and MSI status are tested in every bowel cancer. These results determine whether anti-EGFR targeted drugs, immunotherapy with pembrolizumab, or standard chemotherapy is the right first-line approach.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
Section 2 · Why does it matter?
The scale — and the stage gap
Why bowel cancer demands attention — and why earlier diagnosis changes everything
📊
~44,000 new cases per year — 4th most common cancer
More than 120 new bowel cancer diagnoses every day in the UK. Rates are broadly stable but rising in adults under 50, with around 2,600 under-50 cases each year. It is the 4th most common cancer overall.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
⚠️
48 deaths every day — the 2nd biggest cancer killer
Around 17,700 bowel cancer deaths per year in the UK (2022–2024). It is the 2nd most common cause of cancer death after lung cancer, accounting for 10% of all cancer deaths. Overall five-year survival is around 60%.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
🎯
Stage 1: more than 90% survive 5+ years
Stage 1 five-year survival exceeds 90%. Stage 4 survival is approximately 8%. That gap exists because 26% of bowel cancers are still diagnosed at stage 4 in current UK data — where the cancer has already spread to other organs.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
Section 3 · What your doctor might discuss (1 of 2)
Getting to a diagnosis
From first symptoms to confirmed staging
🔍
Red flags that prompt urgent investigation
NICE NG12 identifies: persistent change in bowel habit (6+ weeks), blood in stool without obvious cause, unexplained iron deficiency anaemia, a lump in the abdomen or back passage, unexplained weight loss. These warrant urgent clinical assessment.
NICE NG12 · Suspected cancer: recognition and referral, 2023
🏥
FIT test → colonoscopy → biopsy
A positive FIT leads to colonoscopy — a direct camera examination of the entire large bowel allowing biopsy and polyp removal in the same procedure. A tissue biopsy confirms the diagnosis under the microscope and enables full molecular testing.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
📋
Staging determines the treatment plan
Staged 1–4 using the TNM system. CT of chest, abdomen, and pelvis identifies distant spread; MRI of the pelvis is standard for rectal cancer staging. Stage is the single most important factor in deciding whether treatment aims to cure or to control.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
Section 3 · What your doctor might discuss (2 of 2)
Treatment — matched to the tumour's biology
Surgery, chemotherapy, and targeted drugs are selected by molecular profile
⚕️
Surgery — the primary treatment for early disease
Right or left hemicolectomy, anterior resection, or APR — the specific operation depends on tumour location. For rectal cancer, neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy before surgery shrinks the tumour and reduces local recurrence rates.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
💊
Chemotherapy for stage 3 and metastatic disease
FOLFOX (oxaliplatin plus 5-fluorouracil) is standard adjuvant chemotherapy for stage 3. For metastatic disease, it is combined with bevacizumab or anti-EGFR antibodies (cetuximab, panitumumab) — selected according to the tumour's molecular profile.
NICE NG151 · Colorectal cancer, 2023
🛡️
Immunotherapy for MSI-H tumours
Around 3–5% of metastatic bowel cancers carry MSI-H or dMMR status — a DNA repair defect that makes them highly sensitive to immunotherapy. Pembrolizumab (Keytruda) is now a preferred first-line option for this subgroup, with substantially better outcomes than chemotherapy.
André T et al. NEJM 2020 · PMID 33264544
Section 4 · What the research shows (1 of 2)
Immunotherapy and screening — the evidence
Key Finding
16.5
months
median PFS / pembrolizumab vs 8.2 months on chemotherapy
MSI-H/dMMR metastatic CRC · first-line · KEYNOTE-177

In KEYNOTE-177, pembrolizumab (Keytruda) was compared with standard chemotherapy as first-line treatment in MSI-H/dMMR metastatic bowel cancer. Progression-free survival was 16.5 months versus 8.2 months. Overall survival was also significantly better. Pembrolizumab is now the preferred first-line option for this molecular subgroup.

André T et al. N Engl J Med 2020;383(23):2207–2218 · PMID 33264544
Key Finding
18%
lower bowel cancer incidence / colonoscopy screening vs no screening
85,000+ adults aged 55–64 · 10-year follow-up · NordICC trial

The NordICC trial invited over 85,000 adults to colonoscopy screening or no screening. After 10 years, bowel cancer incidence was 18% lower in the invited group — rising to 31% lower among those who actually attended for colonoscopy. The NHS uses FIT as the first-line test, with positive results leading to colonoscopy.

Bretthauer M et al. N Engl J Med 2022;387(17):1547–1556 · PMID 36214590
Section 4 · What the research shows (2 of 2)
Diet and bowel cancer risk
Key Finding
+18%
higher bowel cancer risk / for each additional 50g of processed meat per day
IARC Working Group · 800+ studies reviewed · Lancet Oncol 2015

An IARC Working Group classified processed meat as a Group 1 carcinogen for bowel cancer after reviewing over 800 studies. Each additional 50g per day — roughly two rashers of bacon — raises risk by approximately 18%. This is a dose-response relationship starting from low intake levels: even going from 0 to 50g daily carries higher risk than consuming none.

Bouvard V et al. Lancet Oncol 2015;16(16):1599–1600 · PMID 26514947
