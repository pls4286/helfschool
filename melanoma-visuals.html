Melanoma · What the evidence shows
Common misconceptions
Two widely held beliefs that don't reflect the clinical picture
🌞
"A tan shows your skin is healthy"
The evidence
A tan is the skin's visible response to DNA damage caused by UV radiation. The evidence shows that UV radiation — from sun exposure and sunbeds — is responsible for around 86% of melanoma cases in the UK. There is no such thing as a safe tan.
🔬
"Melanoma only affects people with fair skin"
The evidence
Melanoma occurs across all skin tones. Acral lentiginous melanoma — developing on the palms, soles, and under the nails — is diagnosed more frequently in people with darker skin, and can be harder to spot. Awareness of all skin types matters.
What is it? · 1 of 2
Melanoma — the basics
A cancer of the pigment-producing cells, with a critical dependence on early detection
🔬
What melanoma is
Melanoma arises from melanocytes — the pigment-producing cells in the skin. It is the most dangerous skin cancer because it can spread to lymph nodes and distant organs. Unlike basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma, melanoma has a much higher potential to metastasise (spread) if not caught early.
🔡
The ABCDE rule
A clinical checklist for spotting lesions that need assessment: Asymmetry · Border (irregular or uneven) · Colour (multiple shades or uneven) · Diameter (wider than 6mm — roughly the size of a pencil eraser) · Evolution (any change over time). An "ugly duckling" — a mole that simply looks different from the others — is also a recognised warning sign.
📋
Four main subtypes
Superficial spreading melanoma is the most common — it grows outward before downward. Nodular melanoma grows rapidly into deeper skin from the start. Lentigo maligna melanoma develops on sun-exposed areas in older adults. Acral lentiginous melanoma appears on palms, soles, or under nails, and is more common in people with darker skin.
What is it? · 2 of 2
Risk factors and gene testing
UV exposure is the single largest modifiable risk — and gene testing now shapes treatment from the start
☀️
UV exposure — the key risk
Melanoma risk is more closely linked with intermittent high-intensity UV — sunbathing, sunbeds, or holidays in strong sunshine — than with everyday outdoor exposure. Fair skin, a history of severe sunburn (especially in childhood), many moles, and a family history of melanoma all increase risk.
🧬
The BRAF gene mutation
Approximately half of all skin melanomas carry a mutation in the BRAF gene — most commonly BRAF V600E — which drives uncontrolled cell growth. NICE guidance now recommends BRAF gene testing at the time of diagnosis for higher-risk disease, because its presence determines whether gene-targeted drug treatment is a treatment option.
📊
Stage at diagnosis matters most
Melanoma is staged 1 to 4 based on how deeply the tumour has grown into the skin, whether it has reached nearby lymph nodes, and whether it has spread to distant organs. Stage at diagnosis is the single most important factor determining outcome — which is why early recognition is so clinically significant.
Why does it matter?
The scale — and the stage gap
Melanoma survival depends heavily on the stage at which it is found
📈
A rising diagnosis rate
Around 18,300 people are diagnosed with melanoma in the UK each year — roughly 48 every day. That rate has risen by around 31% over the past decade, linked to ageing, changing sun habits, and improved detection. Despite rising cases, mortality rates have remained stable — reflecting better treatment.
🟢
Stage 1 — around 100% survive 5 years
When melanoma is caught at stage 1 — confined to the skin, not yet spread — around 100% of people survive 5 years or more. At stage 2 this falls to around 85%. At stage 3, where cancer has reached nearby lymph nodes, it is around 75%. Early detection is the single most powerful factor in outcome.
🔬
Modern treatment has changed the outlook
In the 1970s, fewer than half of people diagnosed with melanoma survived 10 years. By 2018, more than 9 in 10 did. The arrival of effective immunotherapy and gene-targeted treatments over the past 15 years has dramatically changed outcomes for advanced disease — making melanoma one of oncology's greatest success stories.
What your doctor might do · 1 of 2
Diagnosis and staging
From initial assessment through to full staging for higher-risk disease
🔍
Dermatoscopy and excision biopsy
A concerning lesion is first assessed clinically, increasingly with dermatoscopy — a handheld magnifying device that reveals skin structure below the surface. If the picture is uncertain, an excision biopsy is performed: the lesion plus a small margin of surrounding tissue is removed and examined by a pathologist, who confirms or excludes melanoma and measures its depth (Breslow thickness).
🧬
BRAF gene testing — now at diagnosis
NICE guidance now recommends BRAF gene testing at the time of diagnosis for stage IIA and above — not only at the point of advanced disease. Knowing BRAF status early means gene-targeted treatment can begin promptly if the disease progresses, without the delay of retrieving and re-testing samples later.
🏥
Sentinel lymph node biopsy and staging scans
For melanomas above a certain depth, a sentinel lymph node biopsy determines whether cancer has reached the nearest lymph nodes. CT, PET-CT, and MRI scans assess spread to other parts of the body. Together, these investigations establish the stage of the melanoma and guide the treatment plan.
What your doctor might do · 2 of 2
Treatment pathways
Surgery for early-stage disease; immunotherapy and gene-targeted treatment for advanced disease
🏥
Surgery — the mainstay for early disease
The primary treatment for early-stage melanoma is surgical removal with a defined margin of surrounding tissue. The width of this margin is determined by the Breslow thickness. For most stage 1 and stage 2 melanomas, surgery is curative. Some patients with higher-risk resected disease may receive adjuvant (preventive) treatment afterwards.
💊
Immunotherapy — activating the immune system
Immunotherapy — treatment that helps the body's immune system recognise and attack cancer cells — has transformed the treatment of advanced melanoma. Checkpoint inhibitors, given by infusion, block immune-suppression signals that cancer cells use to hide. They can be given alone or as a combination. Some patients achieve durable long-term responses.
🧬
Gene-targeted therapy — for BRAF-mutant disease
For patients whose melanoma carries a BRAF gene mutation, gene-targeted treatment directly blocks the abnormal protein that drives cancer growth. This combination of a BRAF inhibitor plus a MEK inhibitor is taken as oral tablets. It is now used both in advanced disease and as adjuvant treatment after surgery in high-risk stage III disease.

KEYNOTE-006 — Immunotherapy vs older treatment

The KEYNOTE-006 trial compared a checkpoint inhibitor immunotherapy against an older immune treatment in advanced melanoma. Immunotherapy more than doubled median survival — 32.7 months versus 15.9 months. It is now a standard first-line treatment for advanced disease.

Robert C et al. Lancet Oncol 2019. PMID 31345627
5-Year Overall Survival
34%
with gene-targeted therapy / in BRAF-mutant metastatic melanoma
BRAF V600-mutant disease · COMBI-d and COMBI-v trials pooled
COMBI Trials — Gene-targeted treatment

A pooled analysis of two large trials showed 34% of patients with BRAF-mutant metastatic melanoma were alive at 5 years on gene-targeted combination treatment. This represented a fundamental change from the pre-treatment era, when very few patients with advanced disease survived this long.

Robert C et al. N Engl J Med 2019. PMID 31166680

CheckMate 067 — Combination immunotherapy

The CheckMate 067 trial compared combination immunotherapy — blocking two different immune-suppression signals simultaneously — against an older single immune treatment. At 5 years, 52% of patients on combination treatment were alive, compared with 26% on the older treatment alone. Before the immunotherapy era, median survival in advanced melanoma was measured in months. These results represent one of the most dramatic improvements in cancer survival seen in any tumour type.

Larkin J et al. N Engl J Med 2019. PMID 31562797
🌞
The evidence is clear on one thing
Melanoma caught early has around 100% five-year survival
🔍
The ABCDE signs are worth knowing
Clinical evidence shows that Asymmetry, irregular Border, multiple Colours, Diameter greater than 6mm, and Evolution — any change — are the features associated with melanomas that need assessment.
☀️
UV exposure is the single biggest preventable risk
The evidence shows that 86% of UK melanoma cases are linked to UV radiation — from sun exposure and sunbeds. Consistent sun protection, particularly in intense sun, is the most evidence-supported protective measure.
🏥
Modern treatment has transformed advanced disease
Immunotherapy and gene-targeted treatment have produced 5-year survival rates of 34–52% in advanced melanoma — outcomes that were not achievable a decade ago. Clinical trials continue to improve on these results.
