▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — SPORT trial headline
Walk into any pharmacy and there's an entire aisle of supplements promising to support cholesterol, protect your heart, promote healthy lipid levels. Fish oil, plant sterols, red yeast rice, garlic, niacin, bergamot. The options are overwhelming — and they're not cheap.
— pause —
The honest answer is nuanced. Some supplements have genuinely meaningful evidence behind them. Others have been repeatedly shown to perform no better than placebo. And a small number carry real safety concerns that are widely underappreciated.
— pause —
The critical distinction — and this is important — lowering cholesterol numbers on a blood test is not the same as reducing heart attack or stroke risk. Even if a supplement lowers LDL, it may not translate into the clinical benefits that well-studied medicines like statins have demonstrated.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 3–4 — Six supplements
Let me take you through the main ones. Plant sterols and stanols — this is the best-evidenced supplement for LDL reduction. Around 2 grams per day is associated with an LDL reduction of approximately 7–12%. The effect is real and consistent. But — lowering LDL with plant sterols has not been shown to reduce heart attacks or strokes in large outcome trials.
— pause —
Red yeast rice — the active compound, monacolin K, is chemically identical to a prescription statin drug. It works the same way. It carries the same potential for muscle damage and liver injury. The European Food Safety Authority concluded in January 2025 that there is no safe dose of monacolin K as a food supplement.
— pause —
Fish oil — does lower triglycerides at high doses. But it doesn't lower LDL — it may slightly raise it. And large observational data show a possible increased risk of atrial fibrillation in those without existing cardiovascular disease.
— pause —
Garlic, cinnamon, turmeric — the 2022 SPORT trial found these performed no better than placebo at lowering LDL over 28 days. In food — they have their place. As isolated supplements for cholesterol — the evidence is not there.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–6 — What actually moves the number
In UK practice, when supplements are raised in a clinical conversation, the discussion will typically turn first to whether overall cardiovascular risk has been properly assessed — since that determines what kind of intervention is actually warranted.
— pause —
For anyone at significant cardiovascular risk, the evidence base points to statins, lifestyle change, and blood pressure management — not supplements. The evidence for statins is in a different league entirely.
— pause —
Red yeast rice is particularly relevant to clinical conversations — because it carries the same risks as a prescribed statin and can interact with other medications. It is the same active compound, without the regulatory safeguards of a prescription medicine. ▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 7–9 — Research evidence
The SPORT trial — randomised, placebo-controlled, head-to-head. A low-dose statin (5mg) versus six popular supplements. The statin reduced LDL by 37.9%. The supplements performed at placebo level — or worse. This is clean, controlled evidence.
— pause —
On niacin — a 2024 study in Nature Medicine found that excess niacin intake may actually raise the risk of cardiovascular events through a previously unknown inflammatory pathway. Important context for anyone considering high-dose niacin.
— pause —
On plant sterols — the evidence for LDL reduction is real across 124 randomised trials. The evidence that this translates into fewer heart attacks or strokes is not yet established. The number changes. The clinical outcome has not been demonstrated.
— pause —
On bergamot — some studies have shown modest cholesterol-lowering effects, but the evidence base remains limited — most trials are small and short. This is an area of ongoing research, not established medicine.
