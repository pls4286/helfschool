Segment 1 — What is it?

"Walk into any pharmacy and there's an entire aisle of supplements promising to support cholesterol, protect your heart, promote healthy lipid levels. Fish oil, plant sterols, red yeast rice, garlic, niacin, bergamot. The options are overwhelming. And they're not cheap."

"The honest answer is nuanced. Some supplements have genuinely meaningful evidence behind them. Others have been repeatedly shown to perform no better than placebo. And a small number carry real safety concerns that are widely underappreciated." PAUSE

"The critical distinction — and this is important — lowering cholesterol numbers on a blood test is not the same as reducing heart attack or stroke risk. Even if a supplement lowers LDL, it may not translate into the clinical benefits that well-studied medicines like statins have demonstrated."

Segment 2 — The supplements one by one
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 3: Supplement comparison table · LDL / triglycerides / heart events / safety

"Let me take you through the main ones. Plant sterols and stanols — this is the best-evidenced supplement for LDL reduction. Around 2 grams per day is associated with an LDL reduction of approximately 7–12%. The effect is real and consistent. But — lowering LDL with plant sterols has not been shown to reduce heart attacks or strokes in large outcome trials." PAUSE

"Red yeast rice — the active compound, monacolin K, is chemically identical to lovastatin (Mevacor) — a prescription statin drug. It works the same way. It carries the same potential for muscle damage and liver injury. The European Food Safety Authority concluded in January 2025 that there is no safe dose of monacolin K as a food supplement."

"Fish oil — does lower triglycerides at high doses. But it doesn't lower LDL — it may slightly raise it. And large observational data show a possible increased risk of atrial fibrillation in those without existing cardiovascular disease." PAUSE

"Garlic, cinnamon, turmeric — the 2022 JACC SPORT trial found these performed no better than placebo at lowering LDL over 28 days. In food — great. As isolated supplements for cholesterol — the evidence is not there."

Segment 3 — What your doctor might do
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 4: Red yeast rice warning card · monacolin K = lovastatin

"In UK practice, when supplements are raised with a GP, the conversation will typically turn first to whether overall cardiovascular risk has been properly assessed — since that determines what kind of intervention is actually warranted."

"For anyone at significant cardiovascular risk, the discussion involves statins, lifestyle change, and blood pressure management — not supplements. The evidence base for statins is in a different league entirely." PAUSE

"Red yeast rice is particularly relevant to clinical conversations — because it carries the same risks as a prescribed statin and can interact with other medications. It's the same active compound, without the regulatory safeguards. Anything personally relevant here is a conversation for you to have with your GP or healthcare professional."

Segment 4 — What the research shows
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 5: Research cards · JACC 2023 · Nature Medicine 2024 · plant sterols meta-analysis

"The SPORT trial — randomised, placebo-controlled, head-to-head. Rosuvastatin (Crestor) 5mg versus six popular supplements. The statin reduced LDL by 37.9%. The supplements performed at placebo level — or worse. This is clean, controlled evidence."

"On niacin — a 2024 study in Nature Medicine found that excess niacin intake may actually raise the risk of cardiovascular events through a previously unknown inflammatory pathway. Important context for anyone considering high-dose niacin." PAUSE

"On bergamot — some studies have shown modest cholesterol-lowering effects. But the evidence base remains limited — most trials are small and short. This is an area of ongoing research, not established medicine."
