Segment 1 — What is it?

"Walk into any pharmacy and you'll find an entire aisle of supplements promising to support cholesterol, protect your heart, promote healthy lipid levels. Fish oil, plant sterols, red yeast rice, garlic, niacin, bergamot. The options are overwhelming. And they're not cheap."

"The honest answer is nuanced. Some supplements have genuinely meaningful evidence behind them. Others have been repeatedly shown to perform no better than placebo. And a small number carry real safety concerns that are widely underappreciated." PAUSE

"The critical distinction — and this is important — lowering cholesterol numbers on a blood test is not the same as reducing heart attack or stroke risk. Even if a supplement lowers LDL, it may not translate into the clinical benefits that well-studied medicines like statins have demonstrated."

Segment 2 — The supplements one by one

"Let me take you through the main ones. Plant sterols and stanols — this is the best-evidenced supplement for LDL reduction. Around 2 grams per day is associated with an LDL reduction of approximately 7–12%. The effect is real and consistent. But — lowering LDL with plant sterols has not been shown to reduce heart attacks or strokes in large outcome trials." PAUSE

"Red yeast rice — I want to be clear about this one. The active compound, monacolin K, is chemically identical to lovastatin — a prescription statin drug. It works the same way. It carries the same potential for muscle damage and liver injury. But unlike a prescription statin, you can't be certain how much active ingredient you're actually getting. This is not a safe natural alternative to statins."

"Fish oil — does lower triglycerides at high doses. But it doesn't lower LDL — it may actually slightly raise it. And large trials have produced mixed results on cardiovascular outcomes. There's also a possible increased risk of atrial fibrillation at high doses." PAUSE

"Garlic, cinnamon, turmeric — a landmark 2022 JACC study found six of the most popular heart health supplements performed no better than placebo at lowering LDL over 28 days. Garlic actually slightly increased LDL compared to placebo. In food — great. As an isolated supplement for cholesterol — the evidence is not there."

Segment 3 — What your doctor will say

"If you mention supplements to your GP, they will likely want to explore why you're considering them — and whether your overall cardiovascular risk has been properly assessed. That's the more important question."

"For anyone at significant cardiovascular risk, the conversation should be about statins, lifestyle change, and blood pressure management — not supplements. The evidence base for statins is in a different league entirely." PAUSE

"If you're choosing plant sterols as part of a healthy diet — fine, the evidence supports a modest LDL-lowering effect. If you're taking red yeast rice as a 'natural' alternative to statins — please tell your GP. The risks are the same. The regulatory oversight is not."

Segment 4 — What the research shows

"The 2022 JACC study I mentioned — it tested six of the most popular supplements against placebo and against low-dose rosuvastatin. The statin reduced LDL by 35%. The supplements performed at placebo level — or worse."

"On niacin — a 2024 study in Nature Medicine found that excess niacin intake may actually raise the risk of cardiovascular events through a previously unknown pathway involving inflammation. This is important context for anyone considering high-dose niacin." PAUSE

"On bergamot — some studies have shown modest cholesterol-lowering effects. A 2022 network meta-analysis placed it among the more effective supplements studied. But the evidence base remains limited — most trials are small and short. This is an area of ongoing research, not established medicine."
