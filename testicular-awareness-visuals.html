What is testicular awareness?
🧠
Know your normal
Getting to know what's normal — size, shape, weight and feel — so any change stands out against that baseline
👤
Self-noticed, not clinician-found
Most testicular cancers are first noticed by the man himself — not identified at a routine medical examination
⏱️
Minutes a month
Takes only a few minutes a month and requires no equipment or clinical training to do effectively

What does a normal testicle look and feel like?
🔵
Smooth and firm
Each testicle should feel smooth and firm — no hard lumps or unusual areas on the surface
⚖️
Size variation is normal
One testicle being slightly larger or hanging lower than the other is a normal anatomical variation
📍
The epididymis
The soft raised ridge at the back of each testicle is normal anatomy — important to recognise, not confuse with a lump
Changes Worth Assessment · 1 of 2
Changes that are worth having assessed
🔴
Painless lump or swelling
A lump or swelling on a testicle — even pea-sized. Absence of pain does not reduce its clinical significance
📈
One testicle getting larger
A progressive change in size — distinct from the normal minor difference between the two sides
⬇️
Heavy or dragging sensation
A new or increasing feeling of heaviness, fullness, or dragging weight in the scrotum
Changes Worth Assessment · 2 of 2
Changes that are worth having assessed
🔶
Ache or dull pain
Persistent or recurring ache or dull pain in a testicle, the scrotum, or the groin
⚠️
Change in the epididymis
A hard, tender, or changed area in the soft tube at the back of each testicle — the epididymis
❓
Anything new or different
Any change that feels new or different from usual — if it's unfamiliar to you, it's worth having assessed

How to check your testicles
NHS guidance · around once a month · during or after a warm bath or shower
🚿
When to check
Around once a month — best during or after a warm bath or shower when the scrotum is relaxed and easier to examine
👐
The technique
Gently roll each testicle between thumb and fingers, feeling the surface carefully for any new lumps or changes
✅
Feel behind each testicle
Feel behind for the epididymis and note if it feels as usual — then check the other side
What the Research Shows
Survival from testicular cancer in the UK
Key Finding
97.6%
10-year survival / testicular cancer in men aged 15–44 in the UK
UK national data · Cancer Research UK · 2018
Testicular cancer is one of the most treatable cancers in the UK. Among men aged 15 to 44 at diagnosis, 97.6% survive for 10 years or more — reflecting effective treatment and early stage at diagnosis. Across all ages, 10-year survival stands at 96.5%.
