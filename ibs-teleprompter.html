▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — Scale and subtypes
IBS affects around one in ten people in the UK. It is one of the most common reasons people see their GP — and one of the most consistently undertreated.
— pause —
IBS is characterised by recurrent abdominal pain and changes in bowel habit — diarrhoea, constipation, or both — without any structural abnormality to explain them. Blood tests come back normal. Scans look normal. That is sometimes taken to mean nothing is wrong. It doesn't mean that. It means the problem lies somewhere we can't easily image. IBS is now formally classified as a disorder of gut-brain interaction. The symptoms are driven by abnormal communication between the gut and the brain — not damage, not inflammation, but a system that isn't working as it should. Normal investigations do not mean the person is fine.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 9 — Gut-brain mechanisms
IBS isn't a single disease with a single cause. It's a syndrome arising from several overlapping mechanisms.
— pause —
Altered gut motility — the gut moves too fast, causing urgency and diarrhoea, or too slowly, causing constipation and bloating. Visceral hypersensitivity — the nerves of the gut are oversensitive. Normal gut activity that most people wouldn't notice is experienced as pain. Altered gut microbiome — the composition of gut bacteria differs in many people with IBS, particularly after a gut infection. Around 10% of IBS cases follow a documented gut infection — the infection resolves but the gut doesn't return to normal.
— pause —
And the gut-brain connection — this is bidirectional. Anxiety and low mood worsen gut symptoms. Gut symptoms worsen mental health. This is not psychosomatic — it's real two-way communication along the vagus nerve. It's why psychological therapies produce genuine physical gut improvement.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — Stepped approach
IBS is a clinical diagnosis — made from the history, not from tests. Before confirming the diagnosis, a GP will usually do a limited set of investigations to rule out conditions that can mimic it. A full blood count, CRP, and coeliac serology — coeliac disease looks exactly like IBS-D and is missed surprisingly often. And faecal calprotectin — a stool test that reliably distinguishes IBS from inflammatory bowel disease.
— pause —
Management is stepped. Lifestyle and dietary advice first — regular meals, soluble fibre, reducing caffeine and alcohol. Then a dietitian-led low-FODMAP diet — the strongest dietary evidence for IBS, effective in around 50 to 70% of people. Then symptom-specific medications — antispasmodics, loperamide for IBS-D, laxatives for IBS-C. And one specific point about coeliac testing: it requires gluten in the diet at the time of testing. Starting a gluten-free diet beforehand gives a false-negative result — and the diagnosis can be missed entirely.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–8 — Research evidence cards
The evidence base for IBS has strengthened considerably over the last decade. The ATLANTIS trial — published in The Lancet in 2023, 463 patients, 55 GP practices in England — found that low-dose amitriptyline was significantly superior to placebo for IBS over six months. Number needed to treat: 6. No specialist referral required. This is a treatment GPs can prescribe. It's a clear signal from primary care evidence that hasn't yet fully translated into practice.
— pause —
On diet: the low-FODMAP approach produces symptom improvement in around 50 to 70% of people. A Monash University trial comparing gut-directed hypnotherapy, low-FODMAP, and the combination found all three produced comparable, durable improvements at six months — with hypnotherapy additionally improving anxiety and quality of life. The gut-brain axis research is consistent: treatments that address anxiety and gut-brain communication — hypnotherapy, CBT, low-dose amitriptyline — produce real gut improvement. The mechanisms are biological, not placebo.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 10 — CTA
A few things to take from this. IBS is a genuine, recognised disorder with a real biological basis. A proper explanation of what it is, a structured management plan, and access to the treatments that work — that is the standard of care. A consultation ending with "everything looks normal" is not sufficient on its own.
— pause —
Faecal calprotectin distinguishes IBS from IBD and should be part of the initial workup. The low-FODMAP pathway needs dietitian input to work properly — unguided restriction can cause nutritional harm. Gut-directed hypnotherapy and CBT have strong trial evidence and are available on the NHS. And if first-line approaches haven't worked, the ATLANTIS trial data supports low-dose amitriptyline — a conversation for you to have with your GP or healthcare professional.
— pause —
Alarm features — blood in the stool, unexplained weight loss, nocturnal symptoms, new symptoms after 60, family history of bowel cancer — are clinically recognised as time-sensitive. 111 or urgent GP access exists for this.
