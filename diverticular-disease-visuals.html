Three stages — three different experiences
🤫
Diverticulosis — silent pouches
Diverticula present but causing no symptoms. Found incidentally on colonoscopy or scan. No treatment, no dietary restrictions, no follow-up required.
75%
😖
Diverticular disease — symptomatic
Lower left abdominal pain, bloating, altered bowel habit. Pain typically relieved by passing wind or stool. Managed with lifestyle: fibre, fluids, exercise.
25%
🤒
Diverticulitis — inflamed or infected
Persistent lower left pain with fever, nausea, and tenderness. Can be uncomplicated — managed at home — or complicated, involving abscess, perforation, or peritonitis.
~5%
Why diverticula form — risk factors (1 of 2)
🌾
Low dietary fibre — the strongest modifiable factor
Average UK adult consumes ~18g/day — well below the recommended 30g. Low fibre increases colonic pressure during bowel contractions, progressively weakening the bowel wall.
18g
🎂
Age
Rare before 40. Affects ~5–10% of those aged 45+, rising to ~80% of those aged 85+. Cumulative low-fibre diet and progressive bowel wall weakening over decades.
85+
⚖️
Obesity and low physical activity
Both independently increase risk. Physical activity improves gut motility and reduces colonic pressure. Obesity is a particularly strong risk factor in adults under 50.
↑ BMI
Why diverticula form — risk factors (2 of 2)
🚬
Smoking
Increases risk of diverticular disease and complications. NICE NG147 identifies smoking cessation as part of the first-line management of symptomatic diverticular disease.
↑ Risk
💊
NSAIDs and opioids
Regular use of non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs — such as ibuprofen — or opioid painkillers increases the risk of diverticular perforation. NICE NG147 advises avoiding both where possible.
⚠️
Old advice vs evidence — the seeds and nuts myth reversed
❌ Old advice — now reversed
🚫
Avoid seeds, nuts, popcorn, and fruit skins
For decades, people with diverticular disease were routinely told to avoid these foods — on the theory that small particles might lodge inside diverticula and trigger inflammation. This advice was widely given and caused significant unnecessary dietary restriction. There was never any evidence behind it.
Theoretical basis only — no evidence ever supported this
✅ Current NICE guidance — NG147
🥜
No need to avoid seeds, nuts, or fruit skins
NICE NG147 explicitly states there is no need to avoid these foods. Not only is there no evidence they cause diverticulitis — avoiding them reduces fibre intake, which is counterproductive. These foods are a useful source of dietary fibre and belong in a healthy diet for people with diverticular disease.
NICE NG147 · 2019, amended September 2024
Management in UK practice — what your doctor does (1 of 2)
🌾
Lifestyle first — fibre, fluids, exercise, smoking
Gradual increase to 30g fibre/day. ~2L fluid daily. Regular physical activity. Stop smoking. Achieve healthy weight. First-line for all symptomatic disease — NICE NG147.
30g
💊
Paracetamol + antispasmodics for pain
Paracetamol for abdominal pain; antispasmodics — bowel muscle relaxants — for cramping. NSAIDs and opioids explicitly avoided — both increase risk of bowel perforation.
No NSAIDs
🚫
No antibiotics for uncomplicated diverticulitis
Major change from previous practice. Randomised trial evidence shows antibiotics do not reduce complications or speed recovery in uncomplicated cases. NICE NG147 does not recommend them.
No ABx
Management in UK practice — what your doctor does (2 of 2)
🔬
CT scan for suspected acute diverticulitis
CT scanning is the investigation of choice to confirm acute diverticulitis and identify complications — abscess or perforation. Colonoscopy typically follows 6–8 weeks later to exclude colorectal cancer.
CT
🏥
Surgery — for complications or recurrent severe disease
Emergency surgery for peritonitis or perforation. Elective sigmoid colectomy — removal of the sigmoid colon — considered for recurrent disease significantly affecting quality of life.
Rare
What the evidence shows · fibre and diverticular disease
Key Finding
41%
lower risk of diverticular disease / 30g fibre vs low intake ⚑
Aune · Eur J Nutr 2020 · 5 cohorts · 865,829 participants
Aune et al. — dietary fibre and diverticular disease
European Journal of Nutrition · 2020 · DOI: 10.1007/s00394-019-01967-w
Risk fell 23% at 20g/day and 41% at 30g/day. UK recommended intake is 30g — average actual intake ~18g. Consistent dose-response across 5 cohort studies ⚑.
Key Finding
31%
lower risk of diverticular disease / vegetarians vs meat-eaters in UK ⚑
Crowe · BMJ 2011 · EPIC-Oxford · 47,033 UK adults
Crowe et al. — EPIC-Oxford cohort
BMJ · 2011 · DOI: 10.1136/bmj.d4131 · 11.6-year follow-up
47,033 UK participants followed 11.6 years. The association was primarily driven by fibre intake, not meat avoidance. Highest fibre consumers had 41% lower risk than those with lowest intake ⚑.
