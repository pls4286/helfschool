Diverticular Disease
Most people with diverticula never know they have them. This is what the evidence says about diet, lifestyle — and the old advice that turned out to be wrong.

Understanding the spectrum
Three different stages — three different experiences
The distinction matters — most people are in stage 1 and need no treatment
🤫
Diverticulosis — silent pouches
Diverticula present but causing no symptoms. Found incidentally on colonoscopy or scan. No treatment, no dietary restrictions, no follow-up required.
75%
😖
Diverticular disease — symptomatic
Lower left abdominal pain, bloating, altered bowel habit. Pain typically relieved by passing wind or stool. Managed with lifestyle: fibre, fluids, exercise.
25%
🤒
Diverticulitis — inflamed or infected
Persistent lower left pain with fever, nausea, tenderness. Can be uncomplicated (managed at home) or complicated — involving abscess, perforation, or peritonitis.
~5%
Why diverticula form
Risk factors — a Western lifestyle pattern
Rare in rural Africa and Asia — strongly linked to low-fibre Westernised diet
🌾
Low dietary fibre
The strongest modifiable risk factor. Average UK adult consumes ~18g/day — well below the recommended 30g. Low fibre increases colonic pressure during bowel contractions, weakening the bowel wall.
18g
🎂
Age
Rare before 40. Affects ~5–10% of those aged 45+, rising to ~80% of those aged 85+. Cumulative low-fibre diet and progressive bowel wall weakening over decades.
85+
⚖️
Obesity and low physical activity
Both independently increase risk. Physical activity improves gut motility (bowel movement) and reduces colonic pressure. Obesity is a particularly strong risk factor in younger adults under 50.
↑BMI
🚬
Smoking
Increases risk of diverticular disease and complications. NICE NG147 identifies smoking cessation as part of the first-line management of symptomatic diverticular disease.
↑Risk
💊
NSAIDs and opioids
Regular use of non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (such as ibuprofen) or opioid painkillers increases the risk of diverticular perforation. NICE advises avoiding both where possible.
⚠️
Old advice vs current evidence
The seeds and nuts myth — reversed by NICE 2019
Source: NICE NG147 · 2019, amended September 2024
❌ Old advice — now reversed
🚫
Avoid seeds, nuts, popcorn, and fruit skins
For decades, people with diverticular disease were routinely told to avoid seeds, nuts, popcorn, and fruit skins — on the theory that they might lodge inside diverticula and trigger inflammation or infection. This advice was widely given by GPs and dietitians and caused significant dietary restriction in many people.
Theoretical basis only — no evidence ever supported this
✅ Current NICE guidance
🥜
No need to avoid seeds, nuts, or fruit skins
NICE NG147 explicitly states there is no need to avoid these foods. Not only is there no evidence they cause diverticulitis — avoiding them may reduce fibre intake, which is counterproductive. These foods are a useful source of dietary fibre and should form part of a healthy, balanced diet in people with diverticular disease.
NICE NG147 · 2019, amended September 2024
What your doctor does
Management pathway — UK practice
Source: NICE NG147
🌾
Lifestyle first — fibre, fluids, exercise, smoking
Gradual increase to 30g fibre/day; ~2L fluid daily; regular physical activity; stop smoking; achieve healthy weight. First-line for all symptomatic disease.
30g
💊
Paracetamol + antispasmodics for pain
Paracetamol for abdominal pain; antispasmodics (bowel muscle relaxants) for cramping. NSAIDs and opioids explicitly avoided — both increase risk of bowel perforation.
No NSAID
🚫
No antibiotics for uncomplicated disease
Major change from previous practice. Antibiotics are not recommended for uncomplicated diverticular disease or uncomplicated acute diverticulitis — trial evidence shows no benefit.
No ABx
🔬
CT scan for suspected diverticulitis
CT scanning is the investigation of choice to confirm acute diverticulitis and identify complications (abscess, perforation). Colonoscopy typically follows 6–8 weeks later to exclude colorectal cancer.
CT
🏥
Surgery — for complications only
Emergency surgery for peritonitis or perforation. Elective sigmoid colectomy (removal of the sigmoid colon) considered for recurrent disease significantly affecting quality of life.
Rare
The research
Fibre, diet, and diverticular disease — the evidence
Source: Aune et al. Eur J Nutr 2020 · Crowe et al. BMJ 2011
Meta-Analysis · 2020
Dietary fibre and diverticular disease risk — 865,829 participants
Key finding
41%
lower risk of diverticular disease
at 30g dietary fibre per day vs low intake (7.5g/day)
Risk fell 23% at 20g/day, 41% at 30g/day, and 58% at 40g/day. The UK recommended intake is 30g — average actual UK intake is ~18g. Five cohort studies, consistent dose-response.
Aune D et al. · Eur J Nutr · 2020 · DOI: 10.1007/s00394-019-01967-w
UK Cohort · BMJ · 2011
EPIC-Oxford — 47,033 UK adults: vegetarians vs meat eaters
Key finding
31%
lower risk of diverticular disease
in vegetarians vs meat eaters — adjusted for BMI, smoking, alcohol, activity
47,033 UK participants followed 11.6 years. Those with the highest fibre intake had 41% lower risk than those with the lowest — the association was primarily driven by fibre, not meat avoidance.
Crowe FL et al. · BMJ · 2011 · DOI: 10.1136/bmj.d4131
What to take away from this
Four things worth knowing about diverticular disease
🤫
Most diverticula are silent — finding them is not a diagnosis of disease
75% remain silent for life — an incidental finding needs no treatment and no diet changes.
75%
🌾
Dietary fibre is the strongest modifiable factor — 30g per day
A 41% risk reduction at 30g/day versus low intake. Most UK adults consume only 18g. Wholegrains, fruit, vegetables, and pulses all count.
30g
🥜
Seeds, nuts, and fruit skins are fine — old advice was wrong
NICE reversed decades of guidance in 2019. There is no evidence these foods cause diverticulitis — and avoiding them reduces fibre intake unnecessarily.
✅ Fine
🚫
Antibiotics are no longer routine — and NSAIDs carry real risk
No antibiotics for uncomplicated diverticulitis — evidence shows no benefit. NSAIDs increase perforation risk.
No ABx
