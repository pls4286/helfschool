Myth vs Evidence
What social media gets wrong about lung cancer
Two persistent myths — and what the published evidence actually shows
📱 The claim
"Lung cancer only happens to smokers. If you've never smoked, there's nothing to worry about."
✓ The evidence
Around 15% of UK lung cancer diagnoses are in people who have never smoked — making lung cancer in never-smokers the 8th most common cause of cancer death in the UK if counted separately. Radon gas, asbestos, air pollution, and genetics all play a role.
Khan et al. BJC Reports 2023 · PMID 39516402
📱 The claim
"Stage 4 lung cancer is a death sentence. There's nothing medicine can offer — treatment isn't worth pursuing."
✓ The evidence
Immunotherapy and targeted therapy have transformed outcomes for specific patient groups. Osimertinib (Tagrisso) extended median overall survival to 38.6 months in EGFR-mutated NSCLC. Pembrolizumab (Keytruda) nearly doubled progression-free survival versus chemotherapy in high PD-L1 disease.
Ramalingam et al. NEJM 2020 · Reck et al. NEJM 2016
Section 1 · What is it? (1 of 2)
What is lung cancer?
A disease of the airways — with two very different types
🫁
A cancer of the airways and lung tissue
Lung cancer begins when cells lining the airways or lung tissue start dividing in an uncontrolled way — forming a malignant tumour that can invade nearby tissue and spread through the body via the bloodstream or lymphatic system.
🔬
Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC)
Around 80–85% of all lung cancers. NSCLC is a group of related subtypes — adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma. It tends to grow more slowly than SCLC and is often more amenable to surgery, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy.
⚡
Small cell lung cancer (SCLC)
Around 15–20% of cases. Almost always associated with smoking. SCLC grows and spreads faster than NSCLC — it has usually spread beyond the lung by the time it is diagnosed. Treated mainly with chemotherapy; surgery is rarely appropriate.
Section 1 · What is it? (2 of 2)
Risk factors and molecular testing
Why modern treatment starts with testing the tumour's biology
🚭
Smoking — the single biggest risk factor
72% of UK lung cancer cases are directly attributable to smoking, including passive smoking. Together with radon gas, asbestos, occupational exposures, and air pollution, 79% of all UK lung cancer cases are considered preventable.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
🌍
Not just a smoker's disease
Around 15% of UK lung cancer diagnoses are in people who have never smoked — making it the 8th most common cause of cancer death if counted separately. Risk factors for never-smokers include radon gas, asbestos, air pollution, and inherited genetic risk.
Khan et al. BJC Reports 2023 · PMID 39516402
🧬
Molecular testing guides treatment
Every NSCLC tumour is now tested for molecular markers — including EGFR mutations (~10–15% of NSCLC cases) and PD-L1 expression (~23–28% of advanced NSCLC). These results determine whether targeted therapy, immunotherapy, or chemotherapy is the right first-line approach.
NICE NG122 · Reck et al. NEJM 2016
Section 2 · Why does it matter?
The scale — and the opportunity
Why lung cancer demands urgent attention — and why earlier diagnosis changes outcomes
📊
~50,200 new cases per year · nearly 140 every day
Lung cancer is the 3rd most common cancer in the UK, accounting for 12% of all new cases (2019, 2021–2022). Incidence is falling in men as smoking rates decline — but rising in women, where rates have increased by 5% over the last decade.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
⚠️
The UK's biggest cancer killer — 90 deaths every day
Lung cancer accounts for 19% of all UK cancer deaths — more than any other cancer type. Around 32,800 people die from it every year (2022–2024). The 10-year survival rate of 11.1% reflects how often it is diagnosed at an advanced stage.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
🎯
Stage 1: almost 65% survive 5+ years
The overall 10-year survival of 11.1% reflects late-stage diagnoses — but stage 1 five-year survival is almost 65%. This is why NHS targeted CT screening for high-risk individuals — now being rolled out — is such a clinical priority. Earlier detection changes outcomes.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
Section 3 · What your doctor might discuss (1 of 2)
Getting to a diagnosis
From first symptoms to confirmed staging
🔍
Red flag symptoms to know
Key red flags include a persistent cough lasting 3 or more weeks, coughing up blood, new or unexplained breathlessness, chest or shoulder pain, unexplained weight loss, hoarseness, and repeated chest infections that fail to clear. NICE NG122 identifies these as indicators for urgent assessment.
NICE NG122 · Lung cancer: diagnosis and management, 2019
🏥
From chest X-ray to CT, PET and biopsy
Investigation begins with a chest X-ray, then a CT scan of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis. A PET-CT scan identifies spread to lymph nodes or distant organs. A tissue biopsy — via bronchoscopy, CT-guided needle, or EBUS — confirms the diagnosis and enables full molecular testing.
NICE NG122 · Lung cancer: diagnosis and management, 2019
📋
Staging determines the treatment plan
Lung cancer is staged 1–4 using the TNM system: T (tumour size and extent), N (involvement of nearby lymph nodes), M (spread to distant organs such as the brain, liver, or bones). Stage is the single most important determinant of treatment approach and likely outcome.
NICE NG122 · Lung cancer: diagnosis and management, 2019
Section 3 · What your doctor might discuss (2 of 2)
Treatment — more options than ever before
Modern lung cancer treatment is matched to the biology of each tumour
⚕️
Surgery and precision radiotherapy
For early-stage NSCLC, lobectomy — surgical removal of a lobe of the lung — offers the best chance of cure. Where surgery is not appropriate, SABR (stereotactic ablative radiotherapy) delivers high-precision, high-dose radiotherapy to the tumour with excellent outcomes for stage 1 disease.
NICE NG122 · Lung cancer: diagnosis and management, 2019
🛡️
Immunotherapy for high PD-L1 tumours
For NSCLC with high PD-L1 expression (~23–28% of advanced cases), pembrolizumab (Keytruda) is now a standard first-line treatment. Checkpoint inhibitors like this help the immune system recognise and attack cancer cells — with significantly better survival and fewer side effects than chemotherapy alone.
Reck et al. NEJM 2016 · PMID 27718847
🎯
Targeted drugs for EGFR-mutated tumours
EGFR-mutated NSCLC (~10–15% of cases) is treated with EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors — oral targeted drugs that block the faulty growth signal driving the tumour. Osimertinib (Tagrisso) is the preferred first-line agent, with significantly improved survival compared with earlier-generation EGFR drugs.
Ramalingam et al. NEJM 2020 · PMID 31751012
Section 4 · What the research shows (1 of 2)
Screening saves lives — and immunotherapy works
Key Finding
24%
reduction in lung-cancer mortality / CT screening vs no screening
high-risk smokers · 10-year follow-up · NELSON trial

The NELSON trial enrolled 15,789 high-risk current and former smokers aged 50–74 and randomly assigned them to low-dose CT screening at regular intervals or no screening. After 10 years, the CT-screened group had 24% lower lung-cancer mortality. The trial demonstrated that volume-based CT screening can significantly reduce lung cancer deaths in high-risk individuals — with a low rate of false-positive results. It provided the evidence base for NHS targeted lung cancer screening now being implemented in England.

de Koning HJ et al. N Engl J Med 2020;382(6):503–513 · PMID 31995683
Key Finding
10.3 mo
median PFS / pembrolizumab vs 6.0 months on chemotherapy
high PD-L1 NSCLC (≥50% TPS) · first-line · KEYNOTE-024

The KEYNOTE-024 trial compared pembrolizumab (Keytruda) with platinum-based chemotherapy as first-line treatment in advanced NSCLC with high PD-L1 expression. Patients on pembrolizumab had a median progression-free survival of 10.3 months versus 6.0 months on chemotherapy. Overall survival was also significantly better (hazard ratio 0.60 — a 40% lower risk of death). Pembrolizumab was associated with fewer serious side effects. This trial established immunotherapy as the standard first-line option for high PD-L1 NSCLC.

Reck M et al. N Engl J Med 2016;375(19):1823–1833 · PMID 27718847
Section 4 · What the research shows (2 of 2)
Targeted therapy — matching treatment to the tumour
Key Finding
38.6 mo
median overall survival / osimertinib vs 31.8 months on standard EGFR therapy
EGFR-mutated advanced NSCLC · FLAURA trial

The FLAURA trial compared osimertinib (Tagrisso) — a third-generation EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor — with first-generation EGFR TKIs (erlotinib or gefitinib) as initial treatment in patients with advanced NSCLC harbouring EGFR mutations. The overall survival update showed a median survival of 38.6 months with osimertinib compared with 31.8 months on standard EGFR therapy (hazard ratio 0.80). Osimertinib also demonstrated superior penetration of the blood-brain barrier — an important advantage since EGFR-mutated NSCLC has a tendency to spread to the brain. These findings established osimertinib as the preferred first-line treatment for EGFR-mutated advanced NSCLC.

Ramalingam SS et al. N Engl J Med 2020;382(1):41–50 · PMID 31751012
