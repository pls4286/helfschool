Myth vs Evidence
What social media gets wrong about lung cancer
Two persistent myths — and what the published evidence actually shows
📱 The claim
“Lung cancer only happens to smokers. If you’ve never smoked, there’s nothing to worry about.”
✓ The evidence
Around 15% of UK lung cancer diagnoses are in people who have never smoked — making lung cancer in never-smokers the 8th most common cause of cancer death in the UK if counted separately. Radon gas, asbestos, air pollution, and genetics all play a role.
Khan et al. BJC Reports 2023 · PMID 39516402
📱 The claim
“Stage 4 lung cancer is a death sentence. There’s nothing medicine can offer — treatment isn’t worth pursuing.”
✓ The evidence
Immunotherapy and targeted therapy have transformed outcomes for specific patient groups. Osimertinib (Tagrisso) extended median overall survival to 38.6 months in EGFR-mutated NSCLC. Pembrolizumab (Keytruda) nearly doubled progression-free survival versus chemotherapy in high PD-L1 disease.
Ramalingam et al. NEJM 2020 · Reck et al. NEJM 2016
Section 1 · What is it? (1 of 2)
What is lung cancer?
A disease of the airways — with two very different types
🫁
A cancer of the airways and lung tissue
Lung cancer begins when cells lining the airways or lung tissue start dividing in an uncontrolled way — forming a malignant tumour that can invade nearby tissue and spread through the body via the bloodstream or lymphatic system.
🔬
Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC)
Around 80–85% of all lung cancers. NSCLC is a group of related subtypes — adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma. It tends to grow more slowly than SCLC and is often more amenable to surgery, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy.
⚡
Small cell lung cancer (SCLC)
Around 15–20% of cases. Almost always associated with smoking. SCLC grows and spreads faster than NSCLC — it has usually spread beyond the lung by the time it is diagnosed. Treated mainly with chemotherapy; surgery is rarely appropriate.
Section 1 · What is it? (2 of 2)
Risk factors and molecular testing
Why modern treatment starts with testing the tumour’s biology
🚭
Smoking — the single biggest risk factor
72% of UK lung cancer cases are attributed to smoking, including passive smoking. Combined with radon, asbestos, occupational exposures, and air pollution, 79% of all cases are considered preventable.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
🌍
Not just a smoker’s disease
Around 15% of UK lung cancer diagnoses occur in people who have never smoked — the 8th most common cause of cancer death if counted separately. Risk factors include radon, asbestos, air pollution, and inherited genetic risk.
Khan et al. BJC Reports 2023 · PMID 39516402
🧬
Molecular testing guides treatment
Every NSCLC tumour is tested for EGFR mutations (~10–15% of cases) and PD-L1 expression (~23–28% of advanced cases). The results determine whether targeted therapy, immunotherapy, or chemotherapy is the right first-line approach.
NICE NG122 · Reck et al. NEJM 2016
Section 2 · Why does it matter?
The scale — and the opportunity
Why lung cancer demands urgent attention — and why earlier diagnosis changes outcomes
📊
~50,200 new cases per year · nearly 140 every day
The 3rd most common cancer in the UK — 12% of all new cases. Incidence is falling in men as smoking rates decline, but rising in women.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
⚠️
The UK’s biggest cancer killer — 90 deaths every day
19% of all UK cancer deaths — more than any other cancer type. Around 32,800 deaths per year. The overall 10-year survival of 11.1% reflects how often it is diagnosed at an advanced stage.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
🎯
Stage 1: almost 65% survive 5+ years
Stage 1 five-year survival is almost 65%; overall 10-year survival is 11.1%. This gap is exactly why NHS targeted CT screening for high-risk individuals — now being rolled out — is a clinical priority.
Cancer Research UK · cancerresearchuk.org
Section 3 · What your doctor might discuss (1 of 2)
Getting to a diagnosis
From first symptoms to confirmed staging
🔍
Red flag symptoms to know
Persistent cough (3+ weeks), coughing up blood, new breathlessness, chest or shoulder pain, unexplained weight loss, hoarseness, or chest infections that won’t clear — NICE NG122 identifies these as indicators for urgent clinical assessment.
NICE NG122 · Lung cancer: diagnosis and management, 2019
🏥
From chest X-ray to CT, PET and biopsy
A chest X-ray is the starting point. CT of chest, abdomen, and pelvis follows, then PET-CT for spread. A tissue biopsy — via bronchoscopy, CT-guided needle, or EBUS — confirms the diagnosis and enables full molecular testing.
NICE NG122 · Lung cancer: diagnosis and management, 2019
📋
Staging determines the treatment plan
The TNM system stages lung cancer 1–4: T (tumour size), N (lymph node involvement), M (spread to distant organs). Stage is the single most important factor — it determines whether treatment aims to cure or to control.
NICE NG122 · Lung cancer: diagnosis and management, 2019
Section 3 · What your doctor might discuss (2 of 2)
Treatment — more options than ever before
Modern lung cancer treatment is matched to the biology of each tumour
⚕️
Surgery and precision radiotherapy
For early-stage NSCLC, lobectomy (removal of a lung lobe) offers the best chance of cure. Where surgery isn’t suitable, SABR — stereotactic ablative radiotherapy — delivers high-precision treatment with excellent results for stage 1 disease.
NICE NG122 · Lung cancer: diagnosis and management, 2019
🛡️
Immunotherapy for high PD-L1 tumours
For NSCLC with high PD-L1 expression (~23–28% of advanced cases), pembrolizumab (Keytruda) is standard first-line treatment — with better survival and significantly fewer side effects than chemotherapy alone.
Reck et al. NEJM 2016 · PMID 27718847
🎯
Targeted drugs for EGFR-mutated tumours
EGFR-mutated NSCLC (~10–15% of cases) is treated with oral EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors. Osimertinib (Tagrisso) is the preferred first-line agent, with significantly improved survival versus earlier-generation drugs.
Ramalingam et al. NEJM 2020 · PMID 31751012
Section 4 · What the research shows (1 of 2)
Screening saves lives — and immunotherapy works
Key Finding
24%
reduction in lung-cancer mortality / CT screening vs no screening
high-risk smokers · 10-year follow-up · NELSON trial

The NELSON trial randomised 15,789 long-term smokers aged 50–74 to regular low-dose CT screening or no screening. After 10 years, the screened group had 24% lower lung-cancer mortality. This is the evidence behind the NHS targeted lung cancer screening programme now being rolled out in England.

de Koning HJ et al. N Engl J Med 2020;382(6):503–513 · PMID 31995683
Key Finding
10.3
months
median PFS / pembrolizumab vs 6.0 months on chemotherapy
high PD-L1 NSCLC (≥50% TPS) · first-line · KEYNOTE-024

In the KEYNOTE-024 trial, pembrolizumab (Keytruda) was compared with standard chemotherapy in advanced NSCLC with high PD-L1 levels. Progression-free survival was 10.3 months versus 6.0 months. Overall survival was also significantly better, with fewer serious side effects.

Reck M et al. N Engl J Med 2016;375(19):1823–1833 · PMID 27718847
Section 4 · What the research shows (2 of 2)
Targeted therapy — matching treatment to the tumour
Key Finding
38.6
months
median overall survival / osimertinib vs 31.8 months on standard EGFR therapy
EGFR-mutated advanced NSCLC · FLAURA trial

In the FLAURA trial, osimertinib (Tagrisso) — a targeted drug that blocks the faulty EGFR signal driving the tumour — was compared with standard EGFR drugs in advanced NSCLC with EGFR mutations. Median overall survival: 38.6 months versus 31.8 months. Osimertinib is now the preferred first-line treatment for EGFR-mutated NSCLC.

Ramalingam SS et al. N Engl J Med 2020;382(1):41–50 · PMID 31751012
