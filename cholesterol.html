Cholesterol Explained

What is it?

Cholesterol is a fatty substance found naturally in your blood. Despite its bad reputation, your body actually needs it — it plays an essential role in building healthy cells, producing hormones, and helping your body absorb vitamins. The problem arises when there is too much of certain types of cholesterol circulating in the bloodstream.

Cholesterol is carried through the blood by proteins, forming particles called lipoproteins. There are three key types:

Type Common name What it does
LDL (low-density lipoprotein) "Bad" cholesterol Builds up on artery walls, forming plaques that narrow and harden arteries — raising the risk of heart attack and stroke
HDL (high-density lipoprotein) "Good" cholesterol Carries cholesterol away from the arteries back to the liver, where it is broken down and removed from the body
Non-HDL cholesterol Total minus HDL The broader measure of all potentially harmful fats — now the preferred measure in UK clinical guidelines

High cholesterol causes no symptoms. The only way to know your levels is through a blood test.

Key Terms

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) An umbrella term for conditions affecting the heart and blood vessels, including heart attack, stroke, heart failure, and coronary heart disease. CVD is the leading cause of death in the UK.
Ischaemic heart disease A type of cardiovascular disease where the arteries supplying blood to the heart become narrowed or blocked, reducing blood flow. The most common cause of heart attacks. Also called coronary heart disease.
Atherosclerosis The gradual build-up of fatty plaques inside artery walls, causing them to narrow and harden over time. The underlying process behind most heart attacks and strokes.
Triglycerides Another type of fat found in the blood. High triglyceride levels, often seen alongside high cholesterol, further increase cardiovascular risk.
Statin A class of medication that lowers LDL cholesterol by reducing the amount produced in the liver. The most widely prescribed cholesterol-lowering treatment worldwide.
mmol/L Millimoles per litre — the unit used in the UK to measure cholesterol levels in the blood.

Why does it matter?

  • High cholesterol directly contributes to the narrowing and hardening of arteries — the process that leads to heart attacks and strokes.
  • Around 59% of adults in England have a total cholesterol of 5 mmol/L or above — the threshold considered raised under NHS guidelines. Globally, 39% of adults have raised cholesterol. Prevalence is higher in Europe and North America, and lower in Sub-Saharan Africa and parts of Asia — although rates in those regions are now rising. NHS Health Survey for England 2021, NHS Digital; WHO
  • High cholesterol accounts for over 7% of all deaths in England and a third of all ischaemic heart disease worldwide — a figure confirmed by the Global Burden of Disease Study. NHS England; Global Burden of Disease Study
  • If 90% of people with cardiovascular disease in England were identified and treated, an estimated 14,000 heart attacks, strokes and premature deaths could be prevented in just three years. "Improving Lipid Management to Reduce Cardiovascular Disease and Save Lives", NHS England 2023
  • Like high blood pressure, high cholesterol is largely silent — no warning signs, which is why regular testing matters, particularly from age 40 onwards.

What your doctor might do

Checking your cholesterol

Cholesterol is measured through a simple blood test — usually as part of an NHS Health Check (offered to everyone aged 40 to 74 in England every five years), or at your GP surgery if you have risk factors that warrant earlier testing. The test measures your total cholesterol, HDL, and triglycerides — from which your non-HDL and LDL are calculated.

You don't usually need to fast before a cholesterol blood test under current UK guidelines, though your doctor may advise otherwise in certain circumstances.

Understanding your results

Your doctor will look at your numbers in the context of your overall cardiovascular risk — not in isolation. Here are the general targets for most healthy adults:

Measure Target for most healthy adults
Total cholesterolBelow 5.0 mmol/L
Non-HDL cholesterolBelow 4.0 mmol/L
LDL cholesterolBelow 3.0 mmol/L
HDL cholesterol (men)Above 1.0 mmol/L
HDL cholesterol (women)Above 1.2 mmol/L

If you already have cardiovascular disease, your targets will be lower — your doctor will advise you specifically. Your overall risk is assessed using a suitable risk calculator (see the section on risk calculators in our articles on the website).

Treatment

For many people, lifestyle changes can make a real difference — reducing saturated fat in the diet, exercising regularly, maintaining a healthy weight, cutting back on alcohol, and stopping smoking all help to improve cholesterol levels.

If medication is needed, statins are the most prescribed and best evidenced treatment. NICE guidelines recommend offering a statin to anyone with a 10-year cardiovascular risk of 10% or more — most commonly atorvastatin. If statins aren't tolerated, alternatives including ezetimibe are available.

Cholesterol levels are rechecked around three months after starting treatment to see how well they are responding.

What the research shows

Statins and LDL reduction

A meta-analysis of 14 randomised trials involving over 90,000 participants found that each 1 mmol/L reduction in LDL cholesterol cut the risk of major cardiovascular events — including heart attack and stroke — by around one fifth, largely regardless of a person's starting cholesterol level or other risk factors.

Cholesterol Treatment Trialists, The Lancet 2005

Benefits extend to lower-risk groups

A further meta-analysis of 27 trials found that even in people at lower cardiovascular risk, each 1 mmol/L LDL reduction produced approximately 11 fewer major vascular events per 1,000 people over five years.

Cholesterol Treatment Trialists, The Lancet 2012

Supplements versus statins — what the evidence shows

Many people spend money on supplements in the belief they lower cholesterol. The SPORT trial directly compared low-dose rosuvastatin against six commonly used supplements and placebo:

Treatment LDL reduction vs placebo
Rosuvastatin (statin)~38% reduction
Fish oilNo significant reduction
GarlicNo significant reduction
CinnamonNo significant reduction
TurmericNo significant reduction
Plant sterolsNo significant reduction
Red yeast riceNo significant reduction

The 2025 ESC/EAS dyslipidaemia guidelines reviewed all available evidence and reached the same conclusion — no supplement or vitamin was found to be both safe and effective for reducing LDL cholesterol or lowering cardiovascular risk.

SPORT Trial — Laffin et al. JACC 2023 ESC/EAS Focused Update 2025

Current NICE guidance

NICE guideline NG238 (updated December 2023) recommends atorvastatin 20mg for primary prevention in those with a 10-year cardiovascular risk of 10% or more. For people with established cardiovascular disease, the recommended dose is atorvastatin 80mg, with a treatment target of LDL 2.0 mmol/L or below, or non-HDL 2.6 mmol/L or below.

NICE Guideline NG238

The role of lifestyle

Lifestyle changes remain important alongside or instead of medication. Reducing saturated fat, regular aerobic exercise, and weight loss all have solid evidence for improving cholesterol profiles — though many people will still need medication to reach their target levels.

NICE NG238
