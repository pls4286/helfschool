Coeliac Disease —
the silent condition hiding in plain sight
What it actually is, how to recognise it, and the single most common testing trap that delays diagnosis.
Dr Paul · Retired NHS GP · helf.school
What coeliac disease actually is
✓ Healthy gut lining
Tall, finger-like villi absorb nutrients from food — iron, folate, calcium, vitamins.
✗ Coeliac — after gluten
Villi flatten and shrink — the absorbing surface collapses, and nutrients are lost.
The absorbing surface of the small bowel can drop from the size of a tennis court to something closer to a dining table.
Two very different presentations

Classic

  • Chronic diarrhoea
  • Abdominal pain, bloating, wind
  • Weight loss, poor appetite
  • Pale, floating stools (steatorrhoea)
  • Faltering growth in children
  • Nausea, vomiting

Atypical — where most cases hide

  • Iron deficiency anaemia — often the only clue
  • Unexplained fatigue
  • Recurrent mouth ulcers
  • Early osteoporosis
  • Unexplained infertility, recurrent miscarriage
  • Ataxia, neuropathy, brain fog, migraines
  • Itchy blistering rash (dermatitis herpetiformis)
Up to 1 in 4 people later diagnosed with coeliac disease were first told they had IBS.

⚠️
Do not stop gluten
before the test.
The blood test and the biopsy only work while the immune reaction is active. Stop gluten first, and the tests come back negative — even when coeliac disease is there. Keep gluten in the diet, in more than one meal a day, for at least six weeks before testing.

How the diagnosis is made
🩸
Blood test — while still eating gluten
Total IgA plus IgA tTG (tissue transglutaminase antibody — the main coeliac marker). If IgA is low (IgA deficiency), an IgG-based test is used instead.
Step 1
🩺
Positive result → specialist referral
Positive tTG means probability is high. Next step is a gastroenterologist — not starting gluten-free. Stopping gluten now would invalidate further testing.
Step 2
🔬
Endoscopy with duodenal biopsies
The gold standard. Small tissue samples confirm villous atrophy and make the diagnosis definitive.
Step 3
⚡
No-biopsy pathway — for selected adults
BSG 2020: in adults under 55, classic symptoms, no red flags, tTG ≥ 10× upper limit of normal + positive EMA → biopsy can be avoided. ~95% confirmation rate.
BSG 2020

First-degree relative testing
Around 1 in 10 first-degree relatives of a coeliac will also have it — NICE NG20 recommends offering testing to close relatives.

What the evidence actually shows
Global
coeliac now a worldwide disease
Catassi et al. — modern comprehensive review
The Lancet · 2022 · Seminar · 399:2413–2426
Coeliac disease is now recognised as a global disease, not predominantly European, and prevalence continues to rise. Long-term outcomes are determined by strictness of gluten-free diet adherence ⚑.
0.36%
diagnosed UK prevalence · vs ~1% true
Shiha et al. — UK primary care diagnosis gap
Annals of Family Medicine · 2023 · CPRD · 38M records
Diagnosed UK prevalence in 2020 was 0.36% — against a true prevalence of ~1%. Diagnosis has nearly doubled in 20 years, but two-thirds of cases still unidentified ⚑.
~95%
no-biopsy pathway confirmation
Penny et al. — BSG no-biopsy pathway
Gut · 2021 · 70:876–883 · UK cohorts
In selected adults with tTG ≥ 10× ULN plus positive EMA, histological confirmation achieved in ~95% across UK cohorts — enabling diagnosis without biopsy in selected cases ⚑.
