Coeliac Disease:
the silent condition hiding in plain sight
What it actually is, how to spot it, and the testing trap almost everyone falls into

What coeliac disease actually is
Healthy gut lining
Tall, finger-like villi absorb nutrients from food — iron, folate, calcium, vitamins.
Coeliac disease — after gluten
Villi flatten and shrink — the absorbing surface collapses, and nutrients are lost.
The absorbing surface of the small bowel can drop from the size of a tennis court to something closer to a dining table.
Two very different presentations

Classic

  • Chronic diarrhoea
  • Abdominal pain, bloating, wind
  • Weight loss, poor appetite
  • Pale, floating stools (steatorrhoea)
  • Faltering growth in children
  • Nausea, vomiting

Atypical — where most cases hide

  • Iron deficiency anaemia — often the only clue
  • Unexplained fatigue
  • Recurrent mouth ulcers
  • Early osteoporosis
  • Unexplained infertility, recurrent miscarriage
  • Ataxia, neuropathy, brain fog, migraines
  • Itchy blistering rash (dermatitis herpetiformis)
Up to 1 in 4 people later diagnosed with coeliac disease were first told they had IBS.

⚠️
Do not stop gluten
before the test.
The blood test and the biopsy only work while the immune reaction is active. Stop gluten first, and the tests come back negative — even when coeliac disease is there.

Keep gluten in the diet, in more than one meal a day, for at least six weeks before testing.

How the diagnosis is made
1
Blood test — while eating gluten
Total IgA plus IgA tTG — the main coeliac antibody. If IgA is low (IgA deficiency), an IgG-based test is used instead.
2
Positive result → specialist referral
A positive tTG means the probability is high. The next step is a gastroenterologist — not starting gluten-free.
3
Endoscopy with duodenal biopsy
The gold standard. Small tissue samples confirm villous atrophy and make the diagnosis definitive.
4
Or the no-biopsy pathway — for selected adults
BSG 2020: in adults under 55, classic symptoms, no red flags, tTG ≥ 10× upper limit + positive EMA → biopsy can be avoided. 95% confirmation rate.

What the research shows
The Lancet 2022 — the modern comprehensive review
Coeliac disease is now recognised as a global disease — not predominantly European — and prevalence continues to rise. Long-term outcomes are determined by strictness of gluten-free diet adherence.
Catassi C et al., Lancet 2022;399:2413–2426
UK CPRD study — the diagnosis gap
Diagnosed UK prevalence in 2020 was 0.36% — against a true prevalence of ~1%. Diagnosis has nearly doubled in 20 years, but two thirds still unidentified.
Shiha MG et al., Ann Fam Med 2023
BSG no-biopsy pathway — Gut 2021
In selected adults with tTG ≥ 10× ULN plus positive EMA, histological confirmation was achieved in ~95% across UK cohorts — enabling diagnosis without biopsy.
Penny HA et al., Gut 2021;70:876–883
NICE NG20 — UK guideline standard
First-line: total IgA + IgA tTG. Positive → specialist referral for biopsy. HLA testing not recommended in primary care. Test while still eating gluten.
NICE guideline NG20 · last reviewed 2024
Three things to take home
🩺
Ask the question
Persistent gut symptoms, unexplained anaemia, or a close relative with coeliac — NICE guidance says these should trigger testing in UK primary care. Two in three cases are still being missed.
🍞
Keep eating gluten
Do not start gluten-free before the blood test. The test needs the immune reaction to be active — eating gluten for at least six weeks matters.
✅
Strict is the word
Once diagnosed, a strict lifelong gluten-free diet lets the gut heal. A dietitian and Coeliac UK make the practical side far easier.
