Salt and Blood Pressure
What the evidence shows about sodium, hidden salt, and your cardiovascular risk

Where does our salt actually come from?
75%
Already in food before we touch it
Processed food · Bread · Cheese · Ready meals · Meat products
25%
Added during cooking or at the table

The UK target — 6g per day

Most UK adults consume 8–9g of salt per day — well above the NICE and NHS recommended maximum of 6g (roughly one teaspoon, including all salt already in food).

To convert food label sodium to salt: multiply sodium by 2.5

🔴 High: >1.5g salt per 100g
🟡 Medium: 0.3–1.5g per 100g
🟢 Low: <0.3g per 100g
How salt raises blood pressure

systolic BP reduction from sodium reduction in people with hypertension

−42%

The mechanism

Too much sodium → body retains water to dilute it → increased blood volume → higher pressure on artery walls → sustained hypertension → accelerated atherosclerosis and cardiovascular damage.

Practical steps — where to focus
Reduce processed & packaged food — the single biggest lever. The main source of hidden salt.
Check labels — wide variation between brands of same product. Use high/medium/low guide.
Use herbs, spices, lemon, pepper — effective flavour alternatives that don't raise blood pressure.
Taste before adding — most people add salt by habit, not need.
Potassium — the other side

Potassium directly counteracts sodium's effect on blood pressure. 24% lower stroke risk with higher potassium intake.

Rich sources: bananas · sweet potatoes · spinach · beans · lentils · dairy
NICE NG136
Salt reduction is first-line lifestyle advice for everyone with hypertension — alongside medication, not instead of it.
What the research shows
INTERSALT Study — BMJ 1988
10,000+ people across 52 populations worldwide. Populations with lowest sodium intakes had significantly lower blood pressure — and far less age-related rise in BP. Sodium intake tracked blood pressure closely from near-zero to 242 mmol/24h.
INTERSALT Cooperative Research Group · BMJ 1988;297:319–28
DASH-Sodium Trial — NEJM 2001
412 adults. Low-sodium DASH diet reduced systolic BP by 8–14 mmHg systolic BP reduction — comparable to a single antihypertensive drug. Even normotensive individuals benefited meaningfully.
Sacks FM et al. · NEJM 2001;344:3–10
Cochrane Review — He, Li, MacGregor 2013
Modest sustained salt reduction over 4+ weeks produced consistent, significant BP reductions in both hypertensive and normotensive individuals. The lower the intake achieved, the lower the blood pressure — within 3–12g/day range.
He FJ, Li J, MacGregor GA · Cochrane Database 2013
UK Salt Reduction Programme — BMJ Open 2014
Average salt intake fell ~15% (1.4g/day) 2003–2011. Stroke mortality fell 42%. Ischaemic heart disease mortality fell 40%. Population-level change works.
He FJ, Pombo-Rodrigues S, MacGregor GA · BMJ Open 2014;4:e004549
🏷️
Check labels
High salt = >1.5g per 100g. Start with the foods you eat every day — bread, cereal, cheese.
🥗
Think sources not shakers
75% of salt is already in food. More home cooking, less processed food = the biggest reduction.
🍌
Add potassium too
Fruit, vegetables, beans, dairy. Potassium counteracts sodium — 24% lower stroke risk with higher intake.
