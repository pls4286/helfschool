Where excess salt drives cardiovascular harm — 1 of 2
❤️
Blood pressure rise
Sustained sodium excess is a primary driver of hypertension — the main pathway by which salt damages the cardiovascular system.
8–14 mmHg
🧠
Stroke risk
Higher salt intake drives higher BP — the strongest modifiable risk factor for ischaemic and haemorrhagic stroke.
↑ Stroke
💓
Heart attack & heart failure
High BP accelerates coronary atherosclerosis, raises MI risk, and over time remodels the heart, contributing to failure.
↑ CHD, HF
Where excess salt drives cardiovascular harm — 2 of 2
🫘
Kidney strain
Chronic sodium load stresses kidney filtration, accelerates chronic kidney disease, and worsens BP feedback loops.
↑ CKD
🫀
Left-ventricular hypertrophy
Sustained afterload forces the left ventricle to thicken — an independent predictor of arrhythmia, heart failure and death.
LVH
Practical swaps · what actually lowers intake — 1 of 2

Check the label
Above 1.5 g salt / 100g is high — below 0.3 g is low. Two brands of the same product often differ 2–3×.

Effective flavour alternatives that don't raise blood pressure. Flavour does not require sodium.

Research · salt and blood pressure
Key Finding
Dose-response
sodium intake and blood pressure
confirmed across 52 countries and 10,000+ participants
INTERSALT — international BP survey
INTERSALT Cooperative Group · BMJ · 1988 · n>10,000
Populations with the lowest sodium intakes had lower blood pressures and far less age-related BP rise. Sodium intake tracked closely with blood pressure across very different cultures worldwide.
Key Finding
−8 to −14
mmHg systolic BP
from low-sodium DASH diet — comparable to a single drug
DASH-Sodium — controlled feeding trial
Sacks et al · NEJM · 2001 · n=412
The DASH diet with low sodium reduced systolic BP by 8–14 mmHg in people with hypertension — matching a single blood pressure drug. Benefits also seen in those without hypertension.
Research · reducing salt at scale
Key Finding
Lower = lower
salt intake, lower blood pressure
sustained over 4+ weeks — dose-response confirmed
Cochrane — long-term salt reduction
He, Li, MacGregor · Cochrane Database · 2013
Sustained modest salt reduction produced consistent BP reductions in hypertensive and normotensive adults alike — the lower the intake achieved, the lower the blood pressure.
Key Finding
−42%
stroke mortality
during UK salt reduction programme 2003–2011
UK Salt Reduction Programme
He et al · BMJ Open · 2014
UK average salt intake fell ~15% between 2003 and 2011. Stroke mortality fell ~42% and ischaemic heart disease mortality ~40% over the same period.
Research · potassium — the other side
Key Finding
−24%
lower stroke risk
associated with higher potassium intake — WHO meta-analysis
Potassium intake & CV outcomes
Aburto et al · BMJ · 2013 · WHO meta-analysis
Higher potassium intake is associated with ~24% lower stroke risk and lower blood pressure. Fruit, vegetables, beans and dairy are the main potassium sources.
Key Finding
First-line
lifestyle recommendation
NICE NG136 — for all adults with hypertension
NICE guidance — hypertension in adults
NICE NG136 · ongoing review
NICE places salt reduction as first-line lifestyle advice for everyone with hypertension — alongside medication where indicated, not instead of it.
How excess salt raises blood pressure — 1 of 2
💧
Volume expansion
Extra sodium pulls water into the bloodstream to maintain dilution — circulating volume rises, pressure increases.
→ ↑ Volume
💪
Vessel stiffening
Sustained higher pressure stiffens artery walls over time, further raising systolic blood pressure.
→ ↑ Stiffness
🌊
Endothelial dysfunction
High sodium impairs nitric-oxide signalling — arteries lose their ability to dilate properly.
→ ↓ NO
How excess salt raises blood pressure — 2 of 2
⚡
Sympathetic activation
Salt can increase sympathetic-nervous-system tone, raising heart rate and peripheral resistance.
→ ↑ SNS
🫘
Renal sodium handling
Alters renin-angiotensin-aldosterone (RAAS) signalling — kidneys hold onto more sodium, raising BP further.
→ RAAS
🫀
Cardiac remodelling
Chronic afterload thickens the left ventricle — a strong predictor of arrhythmia, failure and death.
→ LVH
