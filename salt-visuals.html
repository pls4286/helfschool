Where excess salt drives cardiovascular harm
❤️
Blood pressure rise
Sustained sodium excess is a primary driver of hypertension — the main pathway by which salt damages the cardiovascular system.
8–14 mmHg
🧠
Stroke risk
Higher salt intake drives higher BP — the strongest modifiable risk factor for ischaemic and haemorrhagic stroke.
↑ Stroke
💓
Heart attack & heart failure
High BP accelerates coronary atherosclerosis, raises MI risk, and — over time — remodels the heart, contributing to failure.
↑ CHD, HF
🫘
Kidney strain
Chronic sodium load stresses kidney filtration, accelerates chronic kidney disease, and worsens BP feedback loops.
↑ CKD
🫀
Left-ventricular hypertrophy
Sustained afterload forces the left ventricle to thicken — an independent predictor of arrhythmia, heart failure and death.
LVH
Practical swaps · what actually lowers intake
🏷️
Check the label
Above 1.5 g salt / 100g is high — below 0.3 g is low. Two brands of the same product often differ 2–3×.
→ Read
🍞
Know the top UK sources
Bread, cheese, cured / processed meats, ready meals, sauces, crisps, breakfast cereals — daily foods, daily exposure.
→ Target
🍳
Home cook more often
Shifts intake from "already-in-food" 75% toward "added-by-you" — where you control the amount.
→ Cook
🌿
Herbs, spices, lemon, pepper
Effective flavour alternatives that don't raise blood pressure. Flavour does not require sodium.
→ Season
🍌
Add potassium-rich foods
Bananas, sweet potatoes, spinach, beans, lentils, dairy. Higher potassium is associated with lower stroke risk.
−24% stroke
👅
Taste before adding
Much added salt is habit, not need. Taste preference adjusts downward over ~2–4 weeks of lower intake.
→ Pause
Research · salt and blood pressure
Tracks
sodium intake · population BP · 52 countries
INTERSALT — international BP survey
INTERSALT Cooperative Group · BMJ · 1988 · n>10,000
Populations with the lowest sodium intakes had lower blood pressures and much less age-related BP rise. Sodium intake and blood pressure tracked closely across very different cultures and diets.
−8 to −14
mmHg systolic · low-Na DASH diet
DASH-Sodium — controlled feeding trial
Sacks et al · NEJM · 2001 · n=412
A low-sodium DASH-style diet reduced systolic BP by 8–14 mmHg in people with hypertension — a drop comparable to a single antihypertensive drug. Benefit also seen in normotensive participants.
Research · reducing salt at scale
Lower = lower
salt & BP · sustained 4+ weeks
Cochrane — long-term salt reduction
He, Li, MacGregor · Cochrane Database · 2013
Sustained, modest salt reduction produced consistent, significant BP reductions in both hypertensive and normotensive adults — the lower the intake achieved, the lower the blood pressure, across the 3–12 g/day range.
−15% Na
UK average intake · 2003–2011 programme
UK Salt Reduction Programme
He et al · BMJ Open · 2014
UK average salt intake fell ~15% (≈1.4 g/day) between 2003 and 2011 through reformulation and labelling. Stroke mortality fell ~42% and ischaemic heart disease mortality ~40% over the same period — consistent with population-level benefit.
Research · potassium — the other side
−24%
stroke risk · higher potassium intake
Potassium intake & CV outcomes
Aburto et al · BMJ · 2013 · WHO meta-analysis
Higher potassium intakes were associated with about 24% lower stroke risk and lower blood pressure. Potassium directly counteracts sodium's pressure-raising effect — fruit, vegetables, beans, and dairy are the main sources.
First-line
lifestyle advice · NICE NG136
NICE guidance — hypertension in adults
NICE NG136 · ongoing review
NICE places salt reduction as first-line lifestyle advice for everyone with hypertension — alongside medication where indicated, not instead of it.
How excess salt raises blood pressure
💧
Volume expansion
Extra sodium pulls water into the bloodstream to maintain dilution — circulating volume rises.
→ ↑ Volume
💪
Vessel stiffening
Sustained higher pressure stiffens artery walls over time, further raising systolic BP.
→ ↑ Stiffness
🌊
Endothelial dysfunction
High sodium impairs nitric-oxide signalling — arteries lose their ability to dilate properly.
→ ↓ NO
⚡
Sympathetic activation
Salt can increase sympathetic-nervous-system tone, raising heart rate and peripheral resistance.
→ ↑ SNS
🫘
Renal sodium handling
Alters renin-angiotensin-aldosterone signalling — kidneys hold onto more sodium, raising BP further.
→ RAAS
🫀
Cardiac remodelling
Chronic afterload thickens the left ventricle — a strong predictor of arrhythmia, failure and death.
→ LVH
