Hypertension

What is it?

High blood pressure — known medically as hypertension — means the force of blood pushing against the walls of your arteries is consistently too high. Think of it like water pressure in a hosepipe: if the pressure is always too strong, over time it starts to damage the pipe itself.

Blood pressure is recorded as two numbers. The top number (systolic) measures the pressure when your heart beats. The bottom number (diastolic) measures the pressure between beats when your heart is resting. A reading is written as, for example, 120/80 mmHg — said as "120 over 80."

A reading of 140/90 mmHg or above, confirmed on more than one occasion, is how hypertension is diagnosed. Many people have no symptoms at all, which is why it is often called a silent condition. You can have it for years without knowing.

Key Terms

Systolic pressure The top number in a blood pressure reading — the pressure in your arteries when your heart beats.
Diastolic pressure The bottom number — the pressure in your arteries between heartbeats, when the heart is resting.
mmHg Millimetres of mercury — the unit used to measure blood pressure. Written as, for example, 120/80 mmHg.
ABPM (Ambulatory Blood Pressure Monitoring) A portable monitor worn for 24 hours that records blood pressure automatically throughout the day and night — giving a much more accurate picture than a single clinic reading.
White coat hypertension When blood pressure readings are higher in a medical setting due to anxiety or stress, but normal at other times.
Cardiovascular disease (CVD) An umbrella term for conditions affecting the heart and blood vessels, including heart attack, stroke, heart failure, and coronary heart disease. CVD is the leading cause of death in the UK.
ECG (Electrocardiogram) A simple, painless test that records the electrical activity of the heart. Used to check for signs of heart strain or damage.
ACE inhibitors / ARBs Two types of blood pressure medication that work by relaxing blood vessels. ACE inhibitors (e.g. ramipril) and ARBs (e.g. losartan) are often the first choice for younger adults.
Calcium channel blockers Blood pressure medicines (e.g. amlodipine) that relax and widen blood vessels by reducing the amount of calcium entering the vessel walls.
Thiazide-like diuretics A type of "water tablet" (e.g. indapamide) that lowers blood pressure by helping the kidneys remove excess fluid and salt from the body.

Why does it matter?

  • Raised blood pressure puts constant extra strain on your heart, arteries, brain, kidneys, and eyes — over time this damage adds up, even when you feel perfectly well.
  • It is one of the leading causes of stroke, heart attack, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease.
  • Around 1 in 3 adults in the UK has high blood pressure, rising to approximately 60% of people aged 65 and over. Among those aged 75 and over, the figure reaches 66% of men and 71% of women. NHS Health Survey for England 2022 (NHS Digital); ONS Risk Factors for Undiagnosed High Blood Pressure in England, 2015–2019
  • Around 1.4 billion adults worldwide — roughly one in three — are estimated to have hypertension, and approximately 600 million of them are unaware of it. WHO Global Hypertension Report 2024
  • It is responsible for around 10 million deaths per year globally, more than any other single condition. WHO Global Hypertension Report 2023

What your doctor might do

Confirming the diagnosis

Your doctor will usually offer you a 24-hour blood pressure monitor to wear at home (called ambulatory blood pressure monitoring, or ABPM). This gives a much more accurate picture of your blood pressure during everyday life than a single clinic reading, and helps avoid "white coat hypertension" — where the stress of being in a medical setting temporarily raises your numbers.

If ABPM isn't suitable, home readings taken twice a day over several days are used instead.

Checking for damage and risk

Your GP will also arrange some basic tests — usually blood tests, a urine test, and sometimes a heart trace (ECG) — to check whether the high blood pressure has already affected your kidneys or heart. An eye examination may also be arranged, as the small blood vessels at the back of the eye can show early signs of damage from high blood pressure.

Together these tests give an overall picture of your cardiovascular risk, assessed using a suitable risk calculator (see the section on risk calculators in our articles on the website).

Treatment

Treatment depends on how high your blood pressure is and what other health factors are present. For many people, lifestyle changes alone can make a meaningful difference — reducing salt intake, losing excess weight, taking regular exercise, cutting back on alcohol, and stopping smoking are all recommended.

If medication is needed, there are several well-established options. In the UK, NICE guidelines suggest starting with one of three main drug types depending on your age and other health factors: ACE inhibitors or ARBs, calcium channel blockers, or thiazide-like diuretics. Many people end up on a combination of two or more.

Your blood pressure will be checked regularly once you are on treatment to make sure it is well controlled. For most people under 80, the target is below 140/90 mmHg in clinic, or below 135/85 mmHg on home monitoring. For people aged 80 and over, the targets are slightly higher — below 150/90 mmHg in clinic, or below 145/85 mmHg on home monitoring. These targets are set in accordance with current NICE guidelines (NG136).

What the research shows

A large analysis drawing on data from hundreds of thousands of participants across randomised trials found that reducing systolic blood pressure by just 5 mmHg cut the risk of major cardiovascular events — including stroke and heart attack — by around 10%, regardless of whether someone had existing heart disease.

The Lancet, 2021 — Blood Pressure Lowering Treatment Trialists' Collaboration

A review of 16 trials involving nearly 27,000 adults aged 60 and over found that blood pressure-lowering medication reduced the chance of death overall, and substantially reduced the risk of stroke and heart attack in this age group — evidence rated as moderate to high certainty.

Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews

For people with mild hypertension (blood pressure 140–159/90–99 mmHg) and no existing cardiovascular disease, a separate review (updated 2025) found that the evidence for medication reducing overall death or heart attack risk in this lower-risk group is currently limited, though there may be some reduction in stroke risk. This is an area of ongoing research and discussion.

Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews, 2025

Current guidance recommends that for people under 80 with confirmed hypertension and a 10-year cardiovascular risk of 10% or more, medication should be offered alongside lifestyle advice. For those over 80, treatment is recommended when blood pressure is above 150/90 mmHg.

NICE Guideline NG136 (last updated 2025)

Lifestyle changes are not just add-ons — they have real, measurable effects. Salt reduction, weight loss, regular aerobic exercise, and the DASH diet (a dietary pattern rich in fruit, vegetables, and low-fat dairy) each have good evidence behind them for meaningfully reducing blood pressure.

BMJ · Cochrane · NICE NG136
