Why IBD matters — impact on daily life (1 of 2)
🚽
Urgency & diarrhoea
Bloody diarrhoea in UC, chronic loose stools in Crohn's. Urgency can dictate daily routines — where people go, what they eat, when they sleep.
↑ Urgency
⚖️
Weight loss & malnutrition
Ongoing inflammation impairs nutrient absorption. Children with Crohn's can fall off growth charts — a key reason early specialist referral matters.
↓ Absorption
😔
Fatigue & mental health
Persistent fatigue is one of the most commonly reported symptoms. Anxiety and depression are much more prevalent in IBD — UK guidelines emphasise psychological care alongside medication.
↑ Fatigue
Why IBD matters — serious long-term risks (2 of 2)
🧬
Colorectal cancer risk — UC
Ulcerative colitis raises colorectal cancer risk around 2.4× vs the general population, rising to ~4× in extensive colitis. The evidence basis for the NHS surveillance colonoscopy programme ⚑.
2.4×
🏥
Flares, complications & surgery
Most people with IBD will need a hospital admission at some point. Around half of people with Crohn's have surgery within ten years — for strictures, abscesses, or perianal disease ⚑.
~50% surgery
What your doctor might do — diagnosis & care (1 of 2)
🧪
Faecal calprotectin — the key first test
Stool test measuring gut inflammation. Reliably distinguishes IBD from IBS and determines who needs colonoscopy. A normal result strongly argues against active IBD.
1st-line
🔬
Colonoscopy with biopsies
Direct visualisation plus tissue samples confirm diagnosis, distinguish Crohn's from UC, and assess disease extent. Also used for ongoing cancer surveillance in long-standing UC.
Confirm
🩺
IBD multidisciplinary team (MDT)
Gastroenterologist · IBD specialist nurse · dietitian · colorectal surgeon · clinical psychologist. NICE NG129 and NG130 recommend this team model for all IBD patients.
NHS MDT
What your doctor might do — ongoing support (2 of 2)
📞
IBD specialist nurse — direct access
Most UK IBD services offer a direct helpline. Direct access at the first sign of a flare means treatment can start earlier rather than waiting for a routine appointment.
Flare line
🧠
Psychological care alongside medication
Anxiety and depression are common and worsen outcomes if untreated. UK guidelines recommend active treatment — cognitive therapy, medication, or both — as part of standard IBD management.
→ Support
Treatment — a stepped approach (1 of 2)
1
Aminosalicylates (5-ASA) — for UC
First-line anti-inflammatory agents for mild to moderate ulcerative colitis. Reduce mucosal inflammation and maintain remission. Less effective in Crohn's.
Mild UC
2
Corticosteroids — to induce remission
Effective for active flares — not for long-term maintenance. The aim is always to taper and stop. Prolonged steroid use causes significant side effects.
Flare only
Treatment — a stepped approach (2 of 2)
3
Immunomodulators — maintenance
Dampen the immune response to maintain remission. Take months to reach full effect — regular blood monitoring required throughout treatment.
Maintenance
4
Biologics & small molecules — moderate–severe
Anti-TNF agents, gut-selective antibodies, interleukin inhibitors, and JAK inhibitors. Have transformed IBD management for moderate to severe disease over 20 years.
Biologics

CCUK — UK IBD prevalence study
Crohn's & Colitis UK / University of Nottingham · 2022
Over 500,000 people in the UK — 1 in 123 — are living with IBD, nearly double the previous estimate of 300,000. Adolescent incidence is rising year on year ⚑.
Key Finding
43
countries showing rising IBD rates / incidence accelerating globally ⚑

Ng et al. — global IBD epidemiology
The Lancet · 2017 · 43-country systematic review
IBD is becoming a global disease — the UK has among the highest rates worldwide. Incidence accelerating in newly industrialised countries, pointing to environmental and dietary drivers ⚑.
