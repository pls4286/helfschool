Inflammatory Bowel Disease —
Crohn's & Ulcerative Colitis
Two related but distinct conditions — what IBD actually is, how they differ, and what the evidence shows works.
Dr Paul · Retired NHS GP · helf.school

LocationAnywhere — mouth to anus

Ulcerative Colitis

LocationColon and rectum only
PatternContinuous from rectum

Why IBD matters — what it does to daily life
🚽
Urgency & diarrhoea
Bloody diarrhoea in UC, chronic loose stools in Crohn's. Urgency can dictate daily routines — where people go, what they eat, when they sleep.
↑ Urgency
⚖️
Weight loss & malnutrition
Ongoing inflammation impairs absorption of nutrients. Children with Crohn's can fall off growth charts — a key reason for early specialist referral.
↓ Absorption
😔
Fatigue & mental health
Persistent fatigue is one of the most commonly reported symptoms. Anxiety and depression are much more prevalent in IBD — part of why UK guidelines emphasise psychological care alongside medication.
↑ Fatigue
🧬
Colorectal cancer risk — UC
Ulcerative colitis raises colorectal cancer risk around 2.4× vs the general population, rising to ~4× in extensive colitis. Basis for the NHS surveillance colonoscopy programme.
2.4×
🏥
Flares & surgery
Most people with IBD will need a hospital admission at some point. Around half of people with Crohn's have surgery within ten years — for strictures, abscesses or perianal disease.
~50% surgery
What your doctor might do — diagnosis & ongoing care
🧪
Faecal calprotectin — the key first test
Stool test measuring gut inflammation. Reliably distinguishes IBD from IBS and determines who needs colonoscopy. A normal result strongly argues against active IBD.
1st-line
🔬
Colonoscopy with biopsies
Direct visualisation + tissue samples confirm diagnosis, distinguish Crohn's from UC, and assess disease extent. Also used for ongoing cancer surveillance in long-standing UC.
Confirm
🩺
IBD multidisciplinary team (MDT)
Gastroenterologist · IBD specialist nurse · dietitian · colorectal surgeon · clinical psychologist. The team exists to help — NICE NG129/NG130 recommend this model of care.
NHS MDT
📞
IBD specialist nurse — direct access
Most UK IBD services offer a direct helpline or email. Direct access at the first sign of a flare means treatment can start earlier rather than waiting for a routine appointment.
Flare line
🧠
Psychological care alongside medication
Anxiety and depression are common and worsen outcomes if untreated. UK guidelines recommend active treatment — CBT, medication, or both — as part of standard IBD management.
→ Support
Treatment — a stepped approach
1
Aminosalicylates (5-ASA) — for UC
Mesalazine (Asacol, Pentasa, Mezavant). First-line for mild to moderate ulcerative colitis — reduces mucosal inflammation and maintains remission. Less effective in Crohn's.
Mild UC
2
Corticosteroids — to induce remission
Prednisolone or budesonide (Entocort). Effective for active flares but not for long-term maintenance. The aim is always to taper and stop — prolonged steroid use causes significant side effects.
Flare only
3
Immunomodulators — maintenance
Azathioprine (Imuran), mercaptopurine, methotrexate. Dampen the immune response to maintain remission. Take months to reach full effect — regular blood monitoring throughout.
Maintenance
4
Biologics & small molecules — moderate–severe
Anti-TNF: infliximab (Remicade), adalimumab (Humira) · vedolizumab (Entyvio) · ustekinumab (Stelara) · JAK inhibitors: tofacitinib (Xeljanz), upadacitinib (Rinvoq). Transformed IBD management over 20 years.
Biologics

Crohn's & Colitis UK — UK prevalence
CCUK / University of Nottingham · 2022 · 38.3M GP records
Over 500,000 people in the UK (1 in 123) are living with IBD — nearly double the previous estimate of 300,000. Adolescent incidence rising around 3% per year ⚑.
Key Finding
Global
rise in newly industrialised countries
incidence accelerating across Asia, South America and Africa
Ng et al. — global epidemiology of IBD
The Lancet · 2017 · systematic review · 43 countries
IBD has become a global disease. The UK has among the highest rates worldwide. Incidence accelerating in newly industrialised countries — pointing to environmental and dietary drivers ⚑.

Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology · 2012 · meta-analysis
UC raises colorectal cancer risk 2.4× vs general population; extensive colitis raises it to around 4×. This is the evidence base for the NHS surveillance colonoscopy programme ⚑.

NICE NG129 & NG130 · BSG IBD guidelines
NICE 2019 · Lamb et al. Gut 2019 · 88,247 publications reviewed
UK standard for Crohn's and UC management. Both emphasise the IBD MDT and active treatment of psychological comorbidity — anxiety and depression are highly prevalent and worsen outcomes if untreated ⚑.
