▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — Acute vs Chronic
The first thing worth getting clear is the difference between acute stress and chronic stress — because they are not the same thing.
— pause —
Acute stress — a surge of adrenaline and cortisol for a specific challenge. An interview. An argument. A near-miss in traffic. Then it's over. The body returns to baseline. That is the system working exactly as designed. That's healthy.
— pause —
Chronic stress is the same response running for weeks, months, years. Cortisol stays elevated. Inflammation builds. Sleep fragments. Mood drops. The body never gets the all-clear. Doctors sometimes call this allostatic load — the cumulative wear and tear on every system in your body.
— pause —
Chronic stress is not a character flaw. It is physiology that forgot how to switch off.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Five systems
So where does chronic stress go in the body? Five systems.
— pause —
The heart and arteries. Blood pressure rises. Arteries inflame. Heart-attack risk climbs — in the same league as smoking, in the research. The mind and sleep. Sleep breaks down first — often a three-in-the-morning awakening. Memory slips. Concentration wavers. Anxiety and depression become far more likely.
— pause —
The gut. Brain and gut are directly wired via the vagus nerve. Reflux, bloating, and IBS all worsen with chronic stress. The immune system — dampened immunity, rising background inflammation. More colds, slower healing, flares of eczema and IBD. And behaviour. Stress pulls toward quick fixes — extra alcohol, missed walks, poor food. Each one feeds more stress. The spiral tightens.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — What a GP can do
A GP can't remove the source of stress — but there are things a consultation can do.
— pause —
The starting point is listening to the whole picture. Work, home, sleep, alcohol, exercise, money, relationships — each amplifies or buffers stress. NHS appointments are short, so noting main concerns in advance means nothing gets missed in the time available.
— pause —
A GP can screen for anxiety and depression using two short questionnaires — the PHQ-9 and GAD-7. Two minutes each. They identify whether stress has crossed into a clinically treatable condition where specific therapies have the strongest evidence.
— pause —
And they can point toward NHS Talking Therapies — CBT, mindfulness, counselling. Free in England. Self-referral is available without a GP letter. Medication has a role where symptoms meet clinical threshold — but for most people, talking therapy is the more durable option.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 5 — Research A (what stress does)
Now the research. Three studies — and they show that chronic stress is not soft. The data is hard.
— pause —
INTERHEART, The Lancet 2004 — 52 countries, over 11,000 cases. Psychosocial stress ranked alongside smoking, high blood pressure, and cholesterol as one of the nine biggest drivers of a first heart attack. About a third of heart-attack risk was attributable to psychosocial factors. Effect seen across every country and ethnic group.
— pause —
IPD-Work, The Lancet 2012 — 197,000 adults across 13 studies. Job strain — high demands, low control — was linked to a 23% higher coronary heart disease risk. Not just a hard job. A hard job where you have no autonomy.
— pause —
And perhaps the most striking — Tawakol et al., The Lancet 2017. PET-CT scans traced the full biological pathway: amygdala activity in the brain drives bone marrow, which produces inflammatory cells, which inflame the artery wall, leading to heart attacks and strokes. Not vague. Not in your head. Imaged, step by step.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 6 — Research B (what helps)
And now the other side — what the evidence says actually works.
— pause —
Rebar et al., 2015 — a meta-meta-analysis in Health Psychology Review. Regular movement reduced anxiety symptoms by approximately 38%. Any format works — walking, cycling, resistance. Regularity matters more than intensity. Thirty minutes, three to five times a week, is evidence-based stress medicine.
— pause —
NHS Talking Therapies — England-wide programme outcomes, 2024. Over 1.7 million referrals. Roughly half of people who complete a course are classified as recovered — anxiety and depression scores fall below clinical threshold. Real-world data, not selected trial samples.
— pause —
Carpenter et al., 2018, in Depression and Anxiety — 41 placebo-controlled trials. CBT produced response rates roughly three times higher than placebo for generalised anxiety disorder. Large effect size. Effects durable at follow-up.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 8 — CTA
So what does the research point toward?
— pause —
Three patterns with solid evidence. The first is breathing — slow, extended out-breaths, four seconds in and six seconds out, measurably activate the vagus nerve within one to two minutes. This is the fastest-acting of all the stress-reduction techniques in the research.
— pause —
The second is NHS Talking Therapies — free in England, self-referral available, approximately 50% recovery rate in real-world data. Search NHS Talking Therapies and a postcode — the process takes a few minutes online and no GP appointment is needed first.
— pause —
The third is regular movement. Three sessions of thirty minutes weekly is the pattern associated with approximately 38% reduction in anxiety symptoms. Any format works. Regularity matters more than anything else.
— pause —
The evidence is clear that any one of these approaches can make a measurable difference — small, consistent steps are how the research reaches daily life.
