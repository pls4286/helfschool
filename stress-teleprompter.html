Segment 1 — The one distinction that changes everything
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Slide 2: Acute vs chronic · two-column cards · ⚡ acute (green) / 🌀 chronic (red) · "allostatic load"

"The first thing worth getting clear is the difference between acute stress and chronic stress. Because they are not the same thing at all." PAUSE

"Acute stress — a surge of adrenaline and cortisol, the main stress hormone, for a specific challenge. An interview. An argument. A near-miss in traffic. Then it's over. The body returns to baseline. That is the system working exactly as designed. That's healthy."

"Chronic stress is the same response running for weeks, months, years. Cortisol stays elevated. Inflammation builds. Sleep fragments. Mood drops. The body never gets the all-clear. Doctors sometimes call this allostatic load — the cumulative wear and tear on every system in your body."

"Chronic stress is not a character flaw. It is physiology that forgot how to switch off." PAUSE

Segment 2 — Five systems chronic stress reaches
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Slide 3: Five systems · five cards animating in · ❤️ heart · 🧠 mind · 🫁 gut · 🛡️ immune · 🍷 behaviour

"So where does chronic stress go in the body? Five systems." PAUSE

"Your heart and arteries. Blood pressure rises. Arteries inflame. Heart-attack risk climbs — in the same league as smoking, in the research."

"Your mind and sleep. Sleep breaks down first — often a three-in-the-morning awakening with a mind that won't stop. Memory slips. Concentration wavers. Anxiety and depression become far more likely."

"Your gut. The brain and gut are directly wired via the vagus nerve. Reflux, bloating, and IBS — cramping and urgency — all worsen with chronic stress."

"Your immune system — dampened immunity and rising background inflammation. More colds, slower healing, flares of eczema and IBD."

"And behaviour. Stress pulls toward quick fixes — extra alcohol, missed walks, poor food. Each one feeds more stress. The spiral tightens." PAUSE

Segment 3 — What a GP can actually do about stress
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Slide 4: What your doctor can do · 5 doc cards animating in · hero: "Listen — whole picture" · PHQ-9/GAD-7 · NHS Talking Therapies · medication · preparation tip

"A GP can't remove the source of your stress — but there are things a consultation can do." PAUSE

"The starting point is listening to the whole picture. Work, home, sleep, alcohol, exercise, money, relationships — each one amplifies or buffers stress. If you're going in — write down your three main concerns first. NHS appointments are short."

"A GP can screen for anxiety and depression using two short questionnaires — the PHQ-9 and GAD-7. Two minutes each. They identify whether stress has crossed into a clinically treatable condition."

"And they can point you toward NHS Talking Therapies — CBT, mindfulness, counselling. Free in England. You can self-refer — no GP letter needed. Search NHS Talking Therapies plus your postcode. Medication has a short-term role too, but for most people, talking therapy is the more durable option." PAUSE

Segment 4 — The research — cardiovascular impact
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Slide 5: Research A · three evidence cards · INTERHEART / IPD-Work +23% / Tawakol amygdala pathway

"Now the research. Three landmark studies — and they show that chronic stress is not soft. The data is hard." PAUSE

"INTERHEART — published in The Lancet in 2004 — 52 countries, over 11,000 cases. Psychosocial stress ranked alongside smoking, high blood pressure, and cholesterol as one of the nine biggest drivers of a first heart attack. Effect seen across every country and every ethnic group."

"IPD-Work — The Lancet, 2012 — 197,000 adults across 13 studies. Job strain — high demands, low control — was linked to a 23% higher coronary heart disease risk. Not just a hard job. A hard job where you have no autonomy."

"And perhaps the most striking — Tawakol et al. in The Lancet, 2017, from Massachusetts General Hospital and Harvard. PET-CT scans traced the full biological pathway: amygdala activity in the brain drives bone marrow, which produces inflammatory cells, which inflame the artery wall, leading to heart attacks and strokes. Not vague. Not in your head. Imaged." PAUSE

Segment 5 — The research — what reliably helps
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Slide 6: Research B · three evidence cards · Rebar ~38% / NHS Talking Therapies ~50% / Carpenter CBT 3×

"And now the other side — what the evidence says actually works." PAUSE

"Rebar et al., 2015 — a meta-meta-analysis in Health Psychology Review. Regular movement reduced anxiety symptoms by approximately 38%. Any format works — walking, cycling, resistance. Regularity matters more than intensity. Thirty minutes, three to five times a week, is evidence-based stress medicine."

"NHS Talking Therapies — England-wide programme outcomes, NHS England Digital, 2024. Over 1.7 million referrals. Roughly half of people who complete a course are classified as recovered — anxiety and depression scores fall below clinical threshold. Real-world data, not selected trial samples."

"Carpenter et al., 2018, Depression and Anxiety — 41 placebo-controlled RCTs. CBT produced response rates roughly three times higher than placebo for generalised anxiety disorder. Large effect size. Effects durable at follow-up." PAUSE
