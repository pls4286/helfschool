Segment 1 — UK Guidelines

Advance through Screen 2 (Four types) → arrive at Screen 3: UK CMO Guidelines · 150 minutes / 2 strength sessions

"The UK Chief Medical Officers' guidelines are clear: 150 minutes of moderate activity a week — that's 30 minutes, five days a week, a brisk walk — plus two sessions of strength training working the major muscle groups."

"The strength part is the one most people skip — and shouldn't. Just 30 to 60 minutes a week of strength work — press-ups, squats, weights, heavy gardening — independently lowers your risk of dying by ten to twenty per cent. That's on top of any aerobic benefit." PAUSE

"The key principle: something is always better than nothing. The biggest health dividend comes from moving from no activity to any activity — not from hitting the full target. The first fifteen minutes a day delivers a disproportionately large share of the benefit."

Segment 2 — Both count

Screen 4: Both valuable / Venn diagram · everyday movement = structured exercise

"Before we go further — one message that matters above all others. People often say 'I don't really exercise' — and dismiss all the movement they're already doing. Don't."

"A gym session counts. So does gardening. Walking to the shops, taking the stairs, pushing a pram — all of it counts. For nearly every health outcome, what matters is the total amount of movement across the day — not where it happened." PAUSE

"A study of 650,000 adults found that people doing half the recommended amount still lived nearly two years longer than those doing nothing. The evidence is emphatically on the side of any movement."

Segment 3 — Lifespan

"Here's the headline number. Regular exercise adds years of life — and not a small number."

"A large Taiwanese study in the Lancet followed over 400,000 people. Those doing just fifteen minutes a day of moderate activity — less than half the guideline — lived, on average, three years longer than those who did nothing. Bigger doses added up to four and a half years." PAUSE

"The important point? The biggest dividend comes from moving off the sofa — not from hitting the full target. The curve is steep at the bottom and flattens at the top."

Segment 4 — 10,000 steps myth
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Advance through Screens 6 (benefits) & 7 (doctor) → Screen 8: 10,000 steps origin + evidence cards

"You've probably heard you need ten thousand steps a day. Here's where that number actually comes from."

"In 1965, a Japanese company launched the world's first consumer pedometer. They called it the Manpo-kei — which translates as 'ten thousand steps meter'. The number was chosen because it was catchy. It was marketing — not medicine." PAUSE

"A huge 2025 review in the Lancet finally settled it. Seven thousand steps a day captures almost all the benefit. At that level, you get a 47% lower risk of dying early compared to someone doing 2,000 steps. And even going from 2,000 to 4,000 drops that risk by over a third. Ten thousand was always a marketing target — the evidence says seven thousand is enough."
