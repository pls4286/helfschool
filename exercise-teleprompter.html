▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — UK CMO Guidelines
The UK Chief Medical Officers' guidelines set out two elements: 150 minutes of moderate activity a week — that's 30 minutes, five days a week, a brisk walk — plus two sessions of strength training working the major muscle groups.
— pause —
The strength component is the one most commonly overlooked — and it has independent evidence behind it. Just 30 to 60 minutes a week of strength work — press-ups, squats, weights, heavy gardening — independently lowers mortality risk by ten to twenty percent. That's on top of any aerobic benefit.
— pause —
The key principle in the evidence: the biggest health dividend comes from moving from no activity to any activity — not from hitting the full target. The first fifteen minutes a day delivers a disproportionately large share of the benefit.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — Everyday movement
One message that matters above all others in the evidence. People often dismiss all the movement they're already doing — everyday activities, gardening, walking to the shops, taking the stairs, pushing a pram.
— pause —
For nearly every health outcome, what matters is the total amount of movement across the day — not where it happened or whether it was formal exercise. A gym session counts. So does gardening. The evidence doesn't distinguish between them.
— pause —
A study of 650,000 adults found that people doing half the recommended amount still lived nearly two years longer than those doing nothing. The evidence is emphatically on the side of any movement at all.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 5 — Lifespan dose-response
Here's the headline number. Regular exercise adds years of life — and not a small number.
— pause —
A large Taiwanese study in the Lancet followed over 400,000 people. Those doing just fifteen minutes a day of moderate activity — less than half the guideline — lived on average three years longer than those who did nothing. Bigger doses added up to four and a half years.
— pause —
The curve in the data is steep at the bottom and flattens at the top. The largest benefit comes from moving off the sofa — not from hitting the full target or exceeding it.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 8 — 10,000 steps origin
A word on the ten thousand steps target — because it comes up constantly.
— pause —
In 1965, a Japanese company launched the world's first consumer pedometer. They called it the Manpo-kei — which translates as "ten thousand steps meter." The number was chosen because it was catchy. It was marketing — not medicine.
— pause —
A large 2025 review in the Lancet finally addressed this directly. Seven thousand steps a day captures almost all the benefit. At that level, there is a 47% lower risk of dying early compared to someone doing 2,000 steps. The benefit curve plateaus well before ten thousand. The evidence says seven thousand is enough.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 11 — CTA
So — what does the evidence point to? More movement across the day, less prolonged sitting, and two short strength sessions a week. These are the patterns with the strongest evidence for long-term health.
— pause —
For anyone with a health condition — raised blood pressure, diabetes, or a cardiac history — NHS Exercise Referral schemes are available in many areas, offering twelve weeks of supervised activity at low or no cost. Cardiac Rehabilitation is the recognised clinical pathway after a cardiac event, and participation consistently improves outcomes.
— pause —
The evidence is clear that the starting point matters far more than the endpoint. Any increase from a current baseline produces measurable benefit. What that looks like in practice is a conversation for you to have with your GP or healthcare professional.
