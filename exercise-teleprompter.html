Segment 1 Introduction · ~15 sec
▶ ADVANCE NOW — Slide 1 · Intro title card Speak to camera. Warm, direct.

I'm Dr Paul — I spent over twenty years as an NHS GP before I retired in 2019. Today I want to talk to you about exercise.

Now — what I'm giving you here is health education, not medical advice. If anything feels relevant to you, please take it to your own doctor. Right — let's get into it.

Segment 2 Physical activity AND exercise — both count
▶ ADVANCE NOW — Slide 4 · Both valuable / Venn Two overlapping circles — physical activity + exercise. Gesture to both.

Before we talk about how much to do, one message matters above all others.

People often say "I don't really exercise" — and dismiss all the movement they're already doing. Don't. A gym session counts. So does gardening. So does walking to the shops, taking the stairs, pushing a pram.

Here's the evidence: a 2012 study of 650,000 adults found that people doing half the recommended amount of activity still lived nearly two years longer than those doing nothing. What counts is total movement — not where it happened.

Segment 3 How much should you do?
▶ ADVANCE NOW — Slide 3 · CMO Guidelines Point to 150 minutes. Then point to 2+ strength sessions.

The UK Chief Medical Officers' guidelines are clear: 150 minutes of moderate activity a week — that's 30 minutes, five days a week, a brisk walk — plus two strength sessions working the major muscle groups.

The strength part is the one most people skip — and shouldn't. Just 30 to 60 minutes a week of resistance work — press-ups, squats, weights, heavy gardening — independently lowers your risk of dying by 10 to 20 per cent. That's on top of any aerobic benefit.

Segment 4 Lifespan — the headline
▶ ADVANCE NOW — Slide 5 · Lifespan J-curve Dose-response graph showing +3y at 15 min/day, +4.5y at higher doses.

Here's the big one. Regular exercise adds years of life — and not a small number.

A huge Taiwanese study in the Lancet followed over 400,000 people. Those who did just 15 minutes a day of moderate activity — less than half the guideline — lived, on average, three years longer than those who did nothing. Bigger doses added up to four and a half years.

The important bit? The biggest dividend comes from moving off the sofa — not from hitting the full target.

Segment 5 The 10,000 steps myth
▶ ADVANCE NOW — Slide 8 · 10,000 steps origin + evidence Show the Japanese character 万歩計 first. Then the three stat cards: 36% / 47% / plateau.

You've probably heard you need 10,000 steps a day. Here's where that number actually comes from.

In 1965, a Japanese company launched the world's first consumer pedometer. They called it the Manpo-kei — which literally translates as "10,000 steps meter". The number was chosen partly because the Japanese character for 10,000 looks like a person walking, and partly because it was catchy. It was marketing — not medicine.

A huge 2025 review in the Lancet finally settled it: 7,000 steps a day captures almost all the benefit. At that level, you get a 47% lower risk of dying early compared to someone doing 2,000 steps. And even going from 2,000 to 4,000 a day drops that risk by over a third.

Segment 6 Begin where you are
▶ ADVANCE NOW — Slides 10–12 · CTAs + closing Walk CTA → Strength CTA → Closing quote. Hold eye contact on final line.

So what does this all mean in practice?

If you do nothing else — walk a bit more. Aim for 7,000 steps a day. Take the stairs. Get off the bus a stop early.

Add two strength sessions a week. Press-ups and squats at home will do.

And for anyone with an existing health condition — raised blood pressure, diabetes, a heart problem — the NHS runs two evidence-based programmes worth knowing about: cardiac rehab after a cardiac event, and the NHS Exercise Referral scheme. Most areas run one or both. They are supervised, structured, and designed exactly for the situation of wanting to move more with a condition in the background.

You don't need a gym. You don't need a plan. You just need to begin.

Begin where you are. A few minutes of movement today is worth more than a perfect plan you never start.
