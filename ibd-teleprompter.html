▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 2 — Crohn's vs UC comparison
IBD is not the same as IBS. That distinction matters — they are completely different conditions, and confusing them leads to the wrong management.
— pause —
Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis are both chronic inflammatory conditions of the gut — immune-mediated, not functional. The inflammation is real, measurable, and causes structural damage over time. Over 500,000 people in the UK are living with IBD — around 1 in 123.
— pause —
Crohn's can affect any part of the gut from mouth to anus — in patches, through the full thickness of the bowel wall. Ulcerative colitis affects the colon and rectum only, continuously from the rectum upwards, and the inner lining only. Different distribution, different depth, different complications — and importantly, different surgical options. Colectomy is curative in UC. In Crohn's, surgery removes the worst-affected bowel but does not cure the condition.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 3–4 — Why it matters
IBD has a substantial impact on daily life. Urgent diarrhoea — sometimes bloody — can dictate where people go, what they eat, when they sleep. Chronic inflammation leads to malnutrition and weight loss. Children with Crohn's can fall off growth charts.
— pause —
Fatigue is one of the most commonly reported symptoms — often more debilitating than the gut symptoms themselves. Anxiety and depression are much more prevalent in IBD than in the general population — part of why UK guidelines place psychological care alongside medication as standard, not optional.
— pause —
And there are serious long-term risks. Around half of people with Crohn's will need surgery within ten years. Ulcerative colitis raises colorectal cancer risk approximately 2.4 times the general population — rising to around 4 times in extensive disease. That is the evidence base for the NHS surveillance colonoscopy programme.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 5–8 — Diagnosis, care, and treatment
The starting point in primary care is usually faecal calprotectin — a stool test that measures gut inflammation. It reliably distinguishes IBD from IBS and determines who needs colonoscopy. A normal result strongly argues against active IBD.
— pause —
Diagnosis is confirmed by colonoscopy with biopsies — direct visualisation plus tissue samples. For Crohn's, MRI enterography assesses the small bowel and looks for complications like strictures and fistulae. Treatment follows a stepped approach. For UC — aminosalicylates first. For flares — corticosteroids, but not long-term. For maintenance — immunomodulators. For moderate to severe disease — biologics.
— pause —
Biologics — anti-TNF agents, gut-selective antibodies, interleukin inhibitors, and JAK inhibitors — have transformed IBD management over the last twenty years. Most people with IBD can achieve sustained remission with the right treatment and the right team around them.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 9–10 — Research evidence
The research tells a clear story. A 2022 UK study using 38 million GP patient records found over 500,000 people living with IBD — 1 in every 123. Nearly double the previous estimate of 300,000.
— pause —
A 2017 Lancet systematic review across 43 countries confirmed that IBD has become a global disease. Incidence is accelerating in newly industrialised countries — pointing to environmental and dietary factors as key drivers. The NICE and BSG guidelines — published in 2019 and reviewing nearly 90,000 publications — set the UK standard for Crohn's and UC management. Both emphasise the IBD multidisciplinary team and the active treatment of psychological comorbidity. Anxiety and depression must be treated — they worsen outcomes if left unaddressed.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 11 — CTA
A few things to take from this. IBD is a genuine, serious, immune-mediated disease — not functional, not stress-related. An accurate diagnosis, a proper management plan, and access to a specialist team are the standard of care.
— pause —
Persistent rectal bleeding or urgent diarrhoea is clinically recognised as warranting prompt investigation. Faecal calprotectin is the evidence-based first investigation — it distinguishes IBD from IBS and determines who needs further testing. Most UK IBD services have a direct nurse helpline — earlier contact at the first sign of a flare means faster treatment rather than waiting for a routine appointment. And mental health is part of the clinical picture — the research is consistent that treating anxiety and depression improves IBD outcomes.
