Segment 1 — What is IBD?
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 2: Crohn's vs UC gut diagram · ibd-gut-screen.html · location and depth of inflammation

"Inflammatory bowel disease — IBD — covers two conditions: Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis. Both involve chronic inflammation of the gut. Both follow a pattern of flares — periods of active disease — followed by remission, where symptoms settle. And both are real, serious conditions that can significantly affect a person's life."

"The first thing I want to be clear about — IBD is not the same as IBS. Irritable bowel syndrome is a disorder of gut-brain interaction — symptoms without structural damage. IBD involves genuine inflammation and, over time, structural damage to the gut wall. They can look similar on the surface. But the causes, the investigations, and the treatments are entirely different." PAUSE

"Crohn's disease can affect any part of the gut — from mouth to anus. Most commonly the end of the small bowel and the colon. The inflammation goes through the full thickness of the gut wall. That's why Crohn's can lead to fistulae, strictures, and abscesses."

"Ulcerative colitis is different — it affects only the colon and rectum. The inflammation stays in the inner lining. It always starts at the bottom and works its way up." PAUSE

"What causes IBD? It's not fully understood. What we know is that it involves an abnormal immune response — the body attacks the contents of its own gut — against a background of genetic susceptibility, an altered gut microbiome, and environmental triggers. The rise in IBD in newly industrialised countries strongly points to environmental factors — diet, early antibiotic exposure, the hygiene hypothesis."

"One thing worth flagging — smoking has opposite effects on the two conditions. It worsens Crohn's disease — more flares, more surgery, medications work less well. It appears to lower risk in ulcerative colitis. PAUSE That is not a reason to smoke. In Crohn's specifically, the evidence shows stopping smoking is among the most impactful modifiable factors — it roughly halves relapse rate, improves medication response, and reduces the likelihood of surgery."

Segment 2 — Why does it matter?
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 3: Stat cards · 500,000+ UK · 1 in 123 · 2.4× CRC risk in UC

"IBD matters for several reasons. First — it's a lifelong condition. There's no cure for Crohn's disease. Ulcerative colitis can be cured by removing the colon — but most people manage it medically for years or decades. The goal is remission — getting symptoms under control and keeping them there."

"Second — it significantly affects quality of life. Active IBD means pain, urgent diarrhoea — sometimes bloody — fatigue, weight loss. It affects work, relationships, travel, diet. Studies consistently show IBD has a quality of life impact comparable to rheumatoid arthritis. This is not a minor inconvenience." PAUSE

"Third — complications can be serious. Crohn's can cause bowel obstruction, fistulae, abscesses. Both conditions carry an increased risk of colorectal cancer — around 2.4 times the general population risk for ulcerative colitis, rising to nearly four times in extensive disease. That's why regular surveillance colonoscopy is recommended."

"And fourth — IBD is not rare, and it's increasing. Over 500,000 people in the UK are living with IBD — that's 1 in every 123. Nearly double the previous estimate. Rates are rising globally. This is a significant and growing burden on patients and on the NHS." PAUSE

Segment 3 — What your doctor will do
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 4: Treatment ladder · ibd-treatment-screen.html · four steps animating in

"IBD is diagnosed through a combination of blood tests, stool tests, endoscopy, and imaging. Your GP plays a critical role in recognising symptoms and making the right referral — but diagnosis needs specialist gastroenterology involvement."

"One test that's particularly useful in primary care is faecal calprotectin — a stool test released by inflamed gut. A normal result makes significant inflammation very unlikely. An elevated result is the trigger for urgent gastroenterology referral." PAUSE

"The gold standard investigation is ileocolonoscopy with biopsies — a camera that visualises the colon and end of the small bowel, taking tissue samples. For Crohn's disease, MRI enterography assesses the small bowel and looks for complications like strictures and fistulae."

"Treatment follows a stepped approach. Use the least intensive effective treatment — escalate if needed. For ulcerative colitis: aminosalicylates first. For flares: corticosteroids — effective, but not for long-term use. For maintenance: immunomodulators like azathioprine. For moderate to severe disease: biologics." PAUSE

"Biologics have transformed IBD management over the last twenty years. Anti-TNF drugs, vedolizumab, ustekinumab, JAK inhibitors. Most people with IBD can achieve sustained remission with the right treatment."

"Surgery is required in a significant proportion of patients. For Crohn's — surgery removes the worst-affected bowel, but it doesn't cure the condition. For ulcerative colitis, removing the colon is curative. Most patients can have a pouch procedure to avoid a permanent stoma."

Segment 4 — What the research shows
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read
Screen 5: Research cards · Ng 2017 · Crohn's & Colitis UK 2022 · NICE NG129/NG130 · Jess CRC 2012

"Let me take you through the key studies. PAUSE In 2017, Ng and colleagues published a landmark systematic review in The Lancet — 43 countries, the conclusion stark: IBD has become a global disease. The UK has one of the highest rates in the world. In newly industrialised countries, incidence is accelerating."

"In the UK, a 2022 study using records from 38 million GP patients found over 500,000 people living with IBD — 1 in every 123. Nearly double the previous estimate. Adolescent incidence rising 3% a year. The NHS needs to plan for a significantly larger IBD population." PAUSE

"The NICE guidelines — NG129 for Crohn's, NG130 for UC — set the UK standard. The BSG guidelines, also 2019, reviewed nearly 90,000 publications. Both emphasise the IBD multidisciplinary team. Both flag that anxiety and depression are highly prevalent in IBD and must be actively treated."

"On cancer risk — Jess and colleagues in 2012 found that ulcerative colitis increases colorectal cancer risk 2.4-fold. In extensive colitis, nearly four times. That's the evidence behind surveillance colonoscopy — and a real reason to stay engaged with specialist follow-up." PAUSE
