Segment 2: What is MASLD?

One in five people in the UK are estimated to have this condition. That's approximately 9.9 million people. And here's the thing — most have no symptoms and don't know they have it.

Visual: Show slide 2 — Normal vs Fatty Liver diagram

MASLD stands for Metabolic dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease. It's the new name for what most people know as fatty liver disease. The name changed in 2023 to better reflect what actually causes it.

At its simplest, MASLD means that excess fat has accumulated in the liver — in people who drink little or no alcohol. The liver isn't supposed to store significant amounts of fat. When it does, you get a condition that ranges from completely silent and reversible at one end, to serious liver disease at the other.

Segment 3: How MASLD Progresses
Visual: Show slide 3 — Disease Progression steps

MASLD isn't a single condition. It's a spectrum that progresses through several stages — though many people never progress beyond the earliest.

Stage one is simple steatosis — fat accumulation with no significant inflammation. This is often stable or reversible with lifestyle change.

Stage two is MASH — that's fat plus inflammation and cell damage. This can progress if untreated.

Stage three is fibrosis — scar tissue forming. Early fibrosis can still improve with intervention.

And stage four is cirrhosis — advanced scarring that's largely irreversible, where the risk of liver failure and cancer rises significantly.

Segment 4: What Causes MASLD
Visual: Show slide 4 — Key Risk Factors

MASLD develops when the liver accumulates more fat than it can process. This happens against a background of metabolic dysfunction.

The strongest single risk factor is obesity — particularly abdominal obesity. And here's something important: waist circumference matters more than weight alone.

For men, risk increases above 94 centimetres — that's 37 inches. For women, it's 80 centimetres — 31 and a half inches.

Other major drivers include type 2 diabetes and insulin resistance. About 65% of people with type 2 diabetes have MASLD.

And diet plays a huge role — ultra-processed foods, added sugars, and refined carbohydrates drive liver fat accumulation.

Segment 5: Why This Matters

Most people with MASLD have no symptoms — and no idea they have it. The liver has no pain receptors. Fatty liver disease is almost always silent until it's advanced.

This means millions of people in the UK are walking around with a condition that, if identified early, is often reversible — but if left undetected, can progress quietly to cirrhosis or liver cancer over years or decades.

But here's the good news: early MASLD is reversible. Lifestyle change can reduce liver fat, improve inflammation, and even reverse early scarring. The evidence for this is robust and consistent.

Segment 6: Evidence-Based Treatment
Visual: Show slide 5 — Weight Loss Thresholds

For the vast majority of people with MASLD, lifestyle intervention is both the first-line and the most effective treatment available.

There are specific thresholds that matter. Five percent weight loss reduces liver fat. Seven percent improves inflammation. Ten percent can stabilise or reverse scarring — even at the F2 to F3 stage.

These aren't arbitrary numbers. They come from multiple randomised trials and are endorsed by the latest European guidelines.

The Mediterranean dietary pattern has the strongest evidence base — reducing liver fat and improving insulin sensitivity across multiple trials.

Segment 7: Emerging Treatments
Visual: Show slide 6 — ESSENCE Trial Results

There are exciting developments in drug treatment. The ESSENCE trial — published in the New England Journal of Medicine this year — studied semaglutide in patients with MASH.

The results were impressive: 62.9% of patients treated with semaglutide achieved resolution of liver inflammation with no worsening of scarring, compared to 34.3% on placebo.

That's a 28.7% absolute difference — statistically significant and clinically meaningful.

Semaglutide — that's Ozempic and Wegovy — isn't currently licensed specifically for MASLD in the UK, but it may be prescribed for diabetes or obesity in patients who also have fatty liver disease.

Segment 8: The Diagnosis Problem
Visual: Show slide 7 — The Diagnosis Gap

Here's the concerning bit: roughly half of the people with MASLD in the UK remain undiagnosed. That's about five million people.

MASLD is often picked up incidentally — on an ultrasound done for another reason, or when routine blood tests show mildly elevated liver enzymes.

The guidelines now recommend a structured approach. First, calculate something called a FIB-4 score — that uses age and simple blood test results to estimate scarring risk. If that's indeterminate, the next step is usually a FibroScan — a non-invasive scan that measures liver stiffness.

Segment 9: What You Can Do

If you have known risk factors — obesity, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, or routine blood tests showing elevated liver enzymes — it's worth raising fatty liver disease with your GP.

Early identification genuinely changes outcomes.

And if you're diagnosed with MASLD, remember: lifestyle change works. A 5 to 10% reduction in body weight, a shift toward a Mediterranean dietary pattern, regular physical activity — these can reduce liver fat, dampen inflammation, and reverse early scarring.

These aren't small effects. They're among the most powerful interventions in liver medicine.

Segment 10: Closing

MASLD is one of the most common chronic conditions in the UK — and one of the most under-recognised. Most people with it feel nothing while their liver is accumulating fat and potentially progressing through inflammation to scarring.

But the encouraging truth is that early MASLD is highly responsive to lifestyle change. Patient knowledge and action genuinely matter here.
