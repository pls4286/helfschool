▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — MASLD spectrum
MASLD stands for Metabolic dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease — the new name for what most people call a fatty liver, previously known as NAFLD. The name changed in 2023. If you've been told you have a fatty liver or NAFLD, this is the same condition.
— pause —
It means excess fat has accumulated in the liver in people who drink little or no alcohol. The liver isn't supposed to store significant amounts of fat. When it does, it creates a spectrum of disease — from completely silent and reversible at one end, to serious liver damage at the other. The spectrum: simple steatosis — fat in the liver, no inflammation, often reversible. Then MASH — inflammation and cell damage. Then fibrosis — scarring. Then cirrhosis — advanced scarring largely irreversible. And in some cases, liver cancer. The key point: the liver has no pain receptors. Most stages are completely silent.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Risk factors
MASLD affects around 1 in 5 people in the UK — approximately 10 million people. The vast majority have no symptoms. No pain. No warning signs.
— pause —
This is a condition that, if identified early, is often completely reversible. But if left undetected, it can progress quietly over years to cirrhosis or liver cancer. It is now the leading cause of chronic liver disease in the UK. The main drivers are central obesity — particularly abdominal fat — type 2 diabetes, insulin resistance, high triglycerides, and a diet high in ultra-processed food and added sugar. And importantly: a normal BMI doesn't rule it out. Waist circumference is a better measure of visceral fat — risk rises above 94cm in men and 80cm in women. MASLD also significantly increases cardiovascular risk — which remains the leading cause of death in people with the condition. Managing fatty liver and managing cardiovascular risk are largely the same conversation.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — What doctors do
MASLD is often picked up incidentally — on an ultrasound done for another reason, or when a blood test shows raised liver enzymes. Many GPs now screen people with known risk factors: obesity, type 2 diabetes, or metabolic syndrome.
— pause —
The key question isn't just whether there's a fatty liver — it's whether there's significant fibrosis. The guidelines recommend a two-step approach. Step one: a FIB-4 score — a simple calculation from routine blood tests. Age, two liver enzyme measurements, and platelet count. Low score means low risk, manage in primary care. High score means specialist referral. Step two where needed: FibroScan — a non-invasive scan that measures liver stiffness as a proxy for fibrosis. Treatment: lifestyle intervention is the cornerstone. Weight loss targets are specific — 5% reduces liver fat. 7% improves inflammation. 10% can reverse fibrosis. GLP-1 receptor agonists — semaglutide, tirzepatide — are showing significant benefit and may be prescribed for their licensed indications with liver benefit as an additional effect.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 6–7 — Research evidence cards
The evidence base is clear. The EASL/EASD/EASO joint guidelines — published in the Journal of Hepatology in 2024 — set the international standard. FIB-4 as first-line fibrosis assessment in primary care. Lifestyle as the primary treatment at every stage.
— pause —
On weight loss: multiple randomised trials confirm that ≥5% reduces steatosis, ≥7% improves inflammation, ≥10% can reverse fibrosis — even at F2–F3 stage. The challenge is sustaining the loss. On diet: a 12-week randomised trial found the Mediterranean diet reduced liver fat by approximately 39% and improved insulin sensitivity compared with a standard low-fat diet. Mediterranean pattern consistently outperforms other diets in MASLD research.
— pause —
And on coffee — genuinely evidence-based. Regular consumption of 2 to 3 cups per day is associated with slower liver disease progression. The EASL 2024 guidelines explicitly include it — anti-inflammatory and antifibrotic effects from bioactive compounds. This is one of the more welcome findings in liver medicine.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 8 — CTA
A few things to take from this. MASLD is common, silent, and in the early stages, highly reversible. Most people who have it don't know. And most of what drives it is modifiable.
— pause —
The risk picture worth knowing — obesity, type 2 diabetes, a raised waist measurement, or elevated liver enzymes on routine blood tests. In that picture, the evidence-based next step is a FIB-4 score — a simple calculation from results already available in primary care. Earlier identification changes outcomes. The evidence-based response where MASLD is present: Mediterranean diet, weight loss, exercise, less ultra-processed food and added sugar. And apparently — coffee helps too.
