▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — Blood pressure diagram
High blood pressure — known medically as hypertension — means the force of blood pushing against the walls of the arteries is consistently too high. Think of it like water pressure in a hosepipe — if the pressure is always too strong, over time it starts to damage the pipe itself.
— pause —
Blood pressure is recorded as two numbers. The top number — systolic — measures the pressure when the heart beats. The bottom number — diastolic — measures the pressure between beats when the heart is resting. A reading of 140 over 90 or above, confirmed on more than one occasion, is how hypertension is clinically defined.
— pause —
The important thing to understand is that most people have no symptoms at all. It can be present for years without any awareness of it. That's what makes routine blood pressure checking clinically significant.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Five body systems
Why does high blood pressure matter? Because sustained elevated pressure causes progressive damage across five body systems — the heart, brain, kidneys, eyes, and arteries. This damage accumulates silently, even when there are no symptoms.
— pause —
In the UK, around 1 in 3 adults has high blood pressure — rising to more than half of those aged 65 and over. And approximately 600 million people globally have hypertension without knowing it. Globally, hypertension accounts for around 10 million deaths per year — more than any other single condition.
— pause —
The good news is that hypertension is highly treatable, and the cardiovascular benefit of even modest reductions in blood pressure is well established by large-scale evidence.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — Doctor's toolkit
In UK practice, confirming a diagnosis of hypertension typically involves a 24-hour blood pressure monitor worn at home — ambulatory blood pressure monitoring, or ABPM. This gives a much more accurate picture than a single clinic reading, and avoids the effect of white coat hypertension — where the clinical setting itself temporarily elevates readings.
— pause —
Management depends on how high blood pressure is and what other health factors are present. Lifestyle measures — salt reduction, weight management, regular aerobic exercise, reduced alcohol intake, and smoking cessation — are all clinically recognised as producing meaningful blood pressure reductions.
— pause —
Where medication is indicated, UK practice follows NICE guideline NG136 — a stepped approach starting with one of three main drug classes: an ACE inhibitor or ARB, a calcium channel blocker, or a thiazide-like diuretic. Management on a combination of two or more is common. Treatment targets are set at below 140/90 mmHg in clinic for most people under 80.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 5–7 — Research evidence
The evidence base for treating hypertension is strong. A large analysis published in The Lancet in 2021 — pooling data from hundreds of thousands of participants — found that a reduction of just 5 mmHg in systolic blood pressure cut major cardiovascular events by 10%, regardless of whether existing heart disease was present.
— pause —
The SPRINT trial — published in the New England Journal of Medicine — showed that an intensive blood pressure target below 120 mmHg reduced cardiovascular events by 25% and all-cause mortality by 27% compared to a standard target. The trial was stopped early on efficacy grounds.
— pause —
On the lifestyle side, the DASH diet — a dietary pattern rich in fruit, vegetables, and whole grains — reduced systolic blood pressure by up to 14 mmHg in clinical trials. That's comparable to single-drug therapy. Weight reduction contributes around 1 mmHg per kilogram lost, with salt reduction and aerobic activity adding further independent effects.

So — what are the key points?
— pause —
Hypertension is one of the most common and most consequential conditions in the UK — affecting around 1 in 3 adults and driving enormous numbers of strokes, heart attacks, and episodes of kidney failure each year. Most people have no symptoms. The condition is typically found through routine monitoring.
— pause —
The evidence for treatment is clear. Even modest reductions in blood pressure produce meaningful reductions in cardiovascular events. Lifestyle measures have real, quantifiable effects and are a recognised part of management at every stage.
