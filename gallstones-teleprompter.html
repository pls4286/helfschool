▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 2 — Scale and numbers
The gallbladder is a small organ that sits under the liver and stores bile — the digestive fluid that helps break down fats. Gallstones form when the balance of substances in bile goes wrong and cholesterol crystallises into hard deposits. They can be as small as a grain of sand or as large as a golf ball.
— pause —
And here's the crucial point — around 80% of people with gallstones never get any symptoms at all. They're found incidentally on a scan done for something else. For most of those people, the answer is simple: do nothing. Watch and wait.
— pause —
But gallstones are genuinely common. In the UK, somewhere between 15 and 20% of adults have them — and around 66,000 cholecystectomies — gallbladder removals — are performed here every year. It's one of the most common surgical procedures in the country.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 3–4 — The 5 Fs
Who gets gallstones? Medical students learn the "5 Fs" — Female, Forty, Fertile, Fat, and Fair. It's a rough mnemonic, but it captures something real.
— pause —
Women are about twice as likely to develop gallstones as men — largely because oestrogen increases cholesterol in bile and slows gallbladder emptying. Pregnancy amplifies this significantly. Excess body weight increases cholesterol output in bile. And family history matters — if a close relative has had gallstones, your own risk is higher.
— pause —
So if someone comes to see me — a woman in her forties who's had two or three pregnancies — I'd have a much higher index of suspicion for gallstones than for a young male with upper abdominal pain.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 5–6 — Complications
When gallstones do cause problems, it's because one has moved and got stuck in a bile duct. The classic symptom is biliary colic — a severe, cramping pain in the upper right abdomen, often after a fatty meal, that builds over minutes and can last several hours before it settles.
— pause —
If the stone stays stuck, the gallbladder becomes inflamed — that's acute cholecystitis — persistent pain with fever and tenderness. Acute cholecystitis is clinically recognised as requiring hospital assessment.
— pause —
Stones can also pass into the common bile duct and cause jaundice — yellowing of the skin and eyes. Or they can trigger pancreatitis — inflammation of the pancreas — which ranges from mild to very serious.
— pause —
⚠️ RED FLAG — Charcot's triad: upper right pain + fever + jaundice. That combination suggests cholangitis — infection in the bile ducts — clinically recognised as time-sensitive. 111 and urgent GP services exist for this.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 7–8 — Diagnosis and treatment
In practice, when gallstones are suspected, the first investigations are blood tests — liver function tests, CRP, and amylase — and an abdominal ultrasound. Ultrasound is the go-to first imaging: no radiation, quick, and it detects gallbladder stones well.
— pause —
If the blood tests suggest the common bile duct is involved — raised bilirubin or alkaline phosphatase — the next step is usually an MRCP, which is an MRI scan of the bile ducts. If a stone is confirmed there, ERCP — a procedure using a camera through the mouth — is used to remove it.
— pause —
For asymptomatic gallstones, NICE guidelines say: reassure and watch. No surgery needed. For symptomatic gallstones, the standard treatment is laparoscopic cholecystectomy — keyhole surgery to remove the gallbladder. Usually a day case. Most people are back to normal within a couple of weeks.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 9 — C-GALL trial
Now — what does the evidence actually say about surgery versus watchful waiting for symptomatic gallstones? There's a UK randomised trial — the C-GALL trial — published in 2024 — that looked at exactly this.
— pause —
Adults with symptomatic uncomplicated gallstones were randomised to either immediate surgery or watchful waiting, with two years of follow-up. The headline finding was that overall quality of life — measured by a pain score — was similar between the two groups at two years.
— pause —
But when they looked at condition-specific quality of life — how much the gallstones were affecting daily life — surgery came out significantly better. And crucially — around half of those in the watchful-waiting group ended up having surgery anyway within two years due to ongoing symptoms or complications.
— pause —
The message from C-GALL: watchful waiting is a reasonable short-term option, but for most people with symptomatic gallstones, surgery is where they end up — and it produces better disease-specific outcomes.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 10 — NICE guidelines
NICE guideline CG188 — confirmed current — makes three straightforward recommendations. Silent gallstones: no treatment, just reassurance. Symptomatic gallstones: offer keyhole surgery. Acute cholecystitis: surgery within one week of diagnosis to prevent complications while waiting.
— pause —
One more thing worth knowing: after successful surgery, there's no need to restrict diet. The gallbladder is a storage organ — the liver keeps making bile and it drains directly into the small intestine. People eat normally.
