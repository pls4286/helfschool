▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 2 — Villi comparison
Coeliac disease is a lifelong autoimmune condition. When someone with coeliac eats gluten — the protein in wheat, rye, and barley — their own immune system attacks the lining of their small intestine.
— pause —
It's not a food allergy. It's not the same as a gluten intolerance. It's a specific autoimmune reaction — and over time, that reaction damages the tiny finger-like projections in the gut called villi. Villi are what absorb nutrients from food. When they're damaged, absorption fails. The absorbing surface of a healthy small bowel is roughly the size of a tennis court. In untreated coeliac disease, that can collapse to something closer to a dining table. That's why iron levels fall, that's why people get tired, that's why bones get thinner.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 3 — Classic vs atypical presentations
And it runs in families. If a parent, sibling, or child has coeliac disease, the risk in close relatives is around 1 in 10 — significantly higher than the general population.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 4 — Diagnosis gap
In the UK, roughly 1 in 100 people have coeliac disease — about 700,000 people. But only around 1 in 3 is actually diagnosed. Two out of every three people with coeliac disease don't yet know they have it.
— pause —
Why does it get missed? Because it doesn't always present the way people expect. Yes — some people have the classic picture: diarrhoea, bloating, weight loss, stomach pain. But a large proportion present completely differently. Unexplained iron deficiency. Persistent fatigue. Recurrent mouth ulcers. An itchy blistering rash. Early osteoporosis. Unexplained infertility. All of those can be coeliac disease. And as many as 1 in 4 people eventually diagnosed with coeliac were first told they had IBS. The average delay from first symptoms to diagnosis in the UK is around eleven years. Eleven years of damage accumulating quietly. Because untreated coeliac carries a small but real increased risk of serious complications — including a rare bowel lymphoma — earlier diagnosis matters.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 5 — How the test works
This next point is the single most important piece of information in this whole video.
— pause —
The blood test looks for the immune reaction that happens when someone with coeliac eats gluten. No gluten — no immune reaction. No reaction — nothing for the test to detect. So if gluten is removed before the blood test is done, the antibodies quietly fall away. The test comes back negative — even when coeliac disease is genuinely there. The diagnosis gets missed. This is the commonest mistake clinicians see. Someone feels terrible on bread, cuts gluten out, feels better, goes to the GP, gets the blood test — and it's negative. Not because the person doesn't have coeliac disease, but because the gluten came out too early.
— pause —
The NICE guidance is clear — for an accurate test, gluten needs to be in the diet, in more than one meal a day, for at least six weeks before the blood sample is taken. The sequence that works: gluten in, test done, then decide.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slides 6–10 — Diagnosis, recovery, and research
Testing starts with a blood test from a GP — the main one is called tTG, tissue transglutaminase. It looks for the antibody the body makes when someone with coeliac eats gluten. If strongly positive, the next step is usually referral for an endoscopy — where small tissue samples from the top of the small bowel confirm the diagnosis. That is the gold standard.
— pause —
There's now a newer pathway for selected adults where the blood result is very strongly positive — where a biopsy isn't always needed. But for most people, biopsy remains the standard. Treatment: there is no medication that cures coeliac disease. The treatment is a strict, lifelong gluten-free diet. And the word strict matters — a gluten-free diet is not a low-gluten diet. Even tiny amounts can keep the immune reaction going. Gluten hides in places people don't expect: some sauces, malt vinegar, some medications. For anyone newly diagnosed, a registered dietitian makes an enormous difference — and Coeliac UK is an excellent practical resource.
— pause —
Once gluten is out of the diet, the gut heals. In children that healing is pretty complete within two years — around 95%. In adults it's slower — about a third recover fully at two years, about two thirds at five years. But the direction of travel is the same.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · Slide 11 — CTA
A few things to take from this. Coeliac disease is common, under-diagnosed, and fully treatable. A Lancet review confirms it's now a global disease with outcomes determined above all by how strictly the gluten-free diet is followed. A 2023 study using 38 million UK GP records confirms diagnosis has nearly doubled in twenty years — but we still only identify about a third of cases.
— pause —
The patterns worth knowing: ongoing gut symptoms, unexplained tiredness, anaemia that hasn't been fully explained, or a close relative with coeliac disease. The tTG blood test is available through GPs on the NHS. And the single point that most often stands between getting the right answer and missing the diagnosis — gluten stays in the diet until the test is done.
