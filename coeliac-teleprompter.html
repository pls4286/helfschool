Segment 1 — What coeliac disease actually is

"Coeliac disease is a lifelong autoimmune condition. When someone with coeliac eats gluten — the protein in wheat, rye, and barley — their own immune system attacks the lining of their small intestine."

"It's not a food allergy. It's not the same as a gluten intolerance. It's a specific autoimmune reaction — and over time, that reaction damages the tiny finger-like projections in the gut called villi. The villi are what absorb nutrients from your food. When they're damaged, you stop absorbing properly." PAUSE

"To give you an idea of the scale — the absorbing surface of a healthy small bowel is roughly the size of a tennis court. In untreated coeliac disease, that can collapse down to something closer to a dining table. That's why iron levels fall, that's why people get tired, that's why bones get thinner — the gut simply isn't doing its job." PAUSE

"One more thing worth saying upfront. There is a genetic element — it runs in families. If you have a parent, sibling, or child with coeliac disease, your own risk is about 1 in 10. That's significantly higher than the general population."

Segment 2 — Why it matters, and why it gets missed

"Here's the thing that really matters about coeliac disease. In the UK, roughly 1 in 100 people have it — that's about 700,000 people. But we think only about 1 in 3 of those is actually diagnosed. Two out of every three people with coeliac disease don't yet know they have it." PAUSE

"Why does it get missed so often? Because it doesn't always show up the way people expect. Yes — some people do have the classic picture. Diarrhoea, bloating, weight loss, stomach pain. That's the textbook case."

"But a huge number of people present completely differently. Unexplained iron deficiency — that's often the only clue. Persistent fatigue with no obvious cause. Recurrent mouth ulcers. An itchy blistering rash on the elbows or knees. Early osteoporosis. Unexplained infertility or recurrent miscarriage. Brain fog, migraines, balance problems. All of those can be coeliac disease." PAUSE

"And here's a really important one — as many as 1 in 4 people who are eventually diagnosed with coeliac disease were first told they had irritable bowel syndrome. That's why the guidance is clear: if someone has new IBS-type symptoms, they should be tested for coeliac disease. Because the treatments are completely different."

"The average delay from first symptoms to a proper diagnosis, in UK studies, is around eleven years. Eleven years of feeling unwell, of being bounced between appointments, of damage accumulating quietly in the gut. And because coeliac disease left untreated carries a small but real increased risk of serious complications — including a rare bowel lymphoma — getting the diagnosis right, and getting it earlier, genuinely matters." PAUSE

Segment 3 — The testing trap

"Now — this next bit is the single most important piece of information in this whole video, and it's the one the medical profession wishes more people knew before they walked into a testing appointment." PAUSE

"Here's how the testing works. The blood test looks for the immune reaction that happens when someone with coeliac eats gluten. No gluten, no immune reaction. No reaction, nothing for the test to detect."

"So if someone cuts gluten out before the blood test is done, the antibodies quietly fall away. The test comes back negative — even when coeliac disease is genuinely there. The diagnosis gets missed." PAUSE

"This is the commonest mistake clinicians see. Someone feels terrible on bread, cuts gluten out, feels better, goes to the GP, gets the blood test — and it's negative. Not because the person doesn't have coeliac disease, but because the gluten came out too early." PAUSE

"The NICE guidance is clear — for an accurate test, gluten needs to be in the diet, in more than one meal a day, for at least six weeks, before the blood sample is taken. Same principle for the biopsy if it gets that far. The evidence-based sequence is: gluten in, test done, then decide."

Segment 4 — What your doctor will do

"So what does testing actually look like? It starts with a simple blood test from your GP. The main one is called tTG — tissue transglutaminase. It looks for the antibody the body makes when someone with coeliac eats gluten."

"If that blood test comes back strongly positive, the next step is almost always referral to a gastroenterologist for an endoscopy — a camera test where small tissue samples are taken from the top of the small bowel. That's the gold standard for confirming the diagnosis." PAUSE

"There's now a newer pathway for some adults — where the blood result is very strongly positive and there are no red-flag symptoms — where a biopsy isn't always needed. But for most people, a biopsy is still the standard, and that's absolutely fine."

"What about treatment? Here's where coeliac disease is unusual — there is no medication that cures it. The treatment is a strict, lifelong gluten-free diet. And I want to stress the word strict. A gluten-free diet is not a low-gluten diet. Even tiny amounts — a shared toaster, a bit of gravy thickener, the wrong soy sauce — can keep the immune reaction going." PAUSE

"Gluten hides in places people don't expect. Some sausages and processed meats. Some sauces and stock cubes. Malt vinegar. Some medications that use wheat starch. Even some lipsticks. This is why — if you're newly diagnosed — getting a session with a registered dietitian is worth its weight in gold. And Coeliac UK is an excellent practical resource for anyone living with it."

"The good news is that once gluten is out of the diet, the gut heals. In children, that healing is pretty complete within two years. In adults it's slower — about a third recover fully at two years, about two thirds at five years. But the direction of travel is the same. Bones strengthen. Iron levels recover. Energy comes back. And the long-term risks drop substantially." PAUSE

"Once diagnosed, coeliac disease needs annual review — usually a repeat antibody level, a check on iron, folate, B12, vitamin D, calcium, and a bone density scan at some point. A pneumonia vaccination is recommended too, because coeliac disease can affect how the spleen works. All of this becomes routine — but it matters."

Segment 5 — What the research shows

"Let me take you through the key research very briefly. PAUSE The most comprehensive modern review is a paper in The Lancet in 2022 — Catassi and colleagues. It confirms coeliac disease is now a global disease, not a European one, and that long-term outcomes are driven, above all, by how strictly the gluten-free diet is followed."

"On UK numbers — a 2023 study from Annals of Family Medicine using data on 38 million people registered with UK GPs confirms that diagnosis has nearly doubled in twenty years — but we still only have about a third of cases identified. The gap is enormous." PAUSE

"The UK diagnostic standard is NICE guideline NG20. First-line blood test is total IgA plus IgA tTG. If that's positive, referral for biopsy. And the 2021 paper from Penny and colleagues in Gut has underpinned the newer no-biopsy pathway for selected adults — showing 95% confirmation when blood antibody levels are at least ten times the upper limit."

"So the evidence is consistent. It's common. It's under-diagnosed. It's treatable. And strict gluten avoidance genuinely protects against complications." PAUSE

Segment 6 — Closing

"So — the picture worth knowing. Ongoing gut symptoms, unexplained tiredness, anaemia that hasn't been fully explained, or a close relative with coeliac disease — these are the patterns NICE guidance says should trigger coeliac testing in UK primary care. The tTG blood test is available on the NHS through GPs."

"And the one piece clinical experience keeps circling back to — gluten stays in the diet until the test is done. That single point is the difference between getting the right answer and missing the diagnosis for another five or ten years." PAUSE
