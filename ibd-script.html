What is IBD?
~70 sec
ON CAMERA — SLIDE 1

"Inflammatory bowel disease — IBD — covers two conditions: Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis. Both involve chronic inflammation of the gut. Both follow a pattern of flares — periods of active disease — followed by remission, where symptoms settle. PAUSE And both are real, serious conditions that can significantly affect a person's life."

Emphasise 'real, serious conditions' — patients are sometimes dismissed before diagnosis.

ON CAMERA — SLIDE 2

"The first thing I want to be clear about — IBD is not the same as IBS. Irritable bowel syndrome is a disorder of gut-brain interaction — symptoms without structural damage. IBD involves genuine inflammation and, over time, structural damage to the gut wall. They can look similar on the surface — but the causes, the investigations, and the treatments are entirely different."

This distinction matters enormously — stress it clearly.
VISUAL CUES
Text Overlay
IBD ≠ IBS
Graphic
Two-column comparison card: IBD (inflammation, structural damage) vs IBS (gut-brain, no damage)
ON CAMERA — SLIDE 3

"Crohn's disease can affect any part of the gut — from mouth to anus. Most commonly it hits the end of the small bowel and the colon. The inflammation goes through the full thickness of the gut wall — we call that transmural. That's why Crohn's can lead to complications like fistulae, strictures, and abscesses. PAUSE Ulcerative colitis is different — it affects only the colon and rectum, the inflammation stays in the inner lining, and it always starts at the bottom and works its way up."

ON CAMERA — SLIDE 4

"What causes IBD? The honest answer is — it's not fully understood. What we do know is that it involves an abnormal immune response — the body attacks the contents of its own gut. That happens against a background of genetic susceptibility, an altered gut microbiome, and environmental triggers. PAUSE The rise in IBD in newly industrialised countries — particularly in Asia and South America — strongly points to environmental factors. Diet, antibiotic exposure early in life, the hygiene hypothesis. This is not just a Western disease anymore."

Keep the tone curious and honest — we don't have all the answers yet.

ON CAMERA — SLIDE 5

"One thing worth flagging — smoking has opposite effects on the two conditions. It worsens Crohn's disease — more flares, more surgery, medications work less well. It appears to be protective against ulcerative colitis. PAUSE That is not a reason to smoke. Smoking causes far more harm than any benefit. But it does matter clinically — and if you have Crohn's, stopping smoking is one of the most important things you can do."

Why does it matter?
~60 sec
ON CAMERA — SLIDE 6

"IBD matters for several reasons. First — it's a lifelong condition. There's no cure for Crohn's disease. Ulcerative colitis can be cured by removing the colon — but most people manage it medically for years or decades. The goal is remission — getting symptoms under control and keeping them there."

VISUAL CUES
Graphic
Relapsing-remitting timeline — flare / remission / flare / remission pattern
ON CAMERA — SLIDE 7

"Second — it significantly affects quality of life. Active IBD means pain, urgent diarrhoea, sometimes bloody, fatigue, weight loss. It affects work, relationships, travel, diet. PAUSE Studies consistently show IBD has a quality of life impact comparable to rheumatoid arthritis. This is not a minor inconvenience."

ON CAMERA — SLIDE 8

"Third — complications can be serious. Crohn's can cause bowel obstruction, fistulae, abscesses. Both conditions carry an increased risk of colorectal cancer — around 2.4 times the general population risk for ulcerative colitis, rising to nearly 4 times in extensive disease. PAUSE That's why regular surveillance colonoscopy is recommended."

Rates are rising globally, particularly in countries adopting Western lifestyles. PAUSE This is a significant and growing burden on patients and on the NHS."

3
What your doctor will do
~75 sec
ON CAMERA — SLIDE 10

"IBD is diagnosed through a combination of blood tests, stool tests, endoscopy, and imaging. Your GP can't diagnose IBD from a blood test alone — but they play a critical role in recognising the symptoms and making the right referral. PAUSE One test that's particularly useful in primary care is faecal calprotectin — a stool test. It's a protein released by inflamed gut. A normal result makes significant inflammation very unlikely. An elevated result is the trigger for urgent gastroenterology referral."

ON CAMERA — SLIDE 11

"The gold standard investigation is ileocolonoscopy with biopsies — a camera examination that visualises the colon and the end of the small bowel, and takes tissue samples. For Crohn's disease, MRI enterography is also used to assess the small bowel and look for complications like strictures and fistulae."

VISUAL CUES
Text Overlay
Gold standard: ileocolonoscopy with biopsies
ON CAMERA — SLIDE 12

"Treatment follows a stepped approach. The principle is — use the least intensive effective treatment, and escalate if you need to. PAUSE For ulcerative colitis, we start with aminosalicylates — drugs like mesalazine. For flares in either condition, corticosteroids bring things under control — but they're not for long-term use. Then there are immunomodulators like azathioprine, which maintain remission. And for moderate to severe disease, biologics — which have transformed IBD management over the last twenty years."

Don't rush this slide — the treatment ladder is important for patients to understand.

ON CAMERA — SLIDE 13

"Surgery is required in a significant proportion of patients at some point. For Crohn's — surgery removes the worst-affected section of bowel, but it doesn't cure the condition. Disease can come back at the surgical site. For ulcerative colitis, removing the colon is curative — the disease is gone. Most patients can have a pouch procedure to avoid a permanent stoma. PAUSE And I want to say again — if you have Crohn's disease, stopping smoking is one of the most impactful things you can do. It doubles the relapse rate. It reduces how well medications work. It increases the chance of needing surgery."

What the research shows
~70 sec
ON CAMERA — SLIDE 14

"Let me take you through a few of the key studies. PAUSE In 2017, Ng and colleagues published a landmark systematic review in The Lancet — analysing population-based studies from 43 countries. The conclusion was stark: IBD has become a global disease. The UK has one of the highest rates in the world. And in newly industrialised countries across Asia, South America, and Africa, incidence is accelerating — almost certainly driven by the adoption of Western lifestyles."

"In the UK specifically, a 2022 study commissioned by Crohn's and Colitis UK — using records from 38 million GP patients — found over 500,000 people living with IBD. That's 1 in every 123. Nearly double the previous estimate. Adolescent incidence is rising by around 3% a year. The NHS needs to plan for a significantly larger IBD population over the coming decades."

ON CAMERA — SLIDE 16

"The NICE guidelines — NG129 for Crohn's and NG130 for ulcerative colitis — published in 2019, set the UK standard for management. They cover everything from diagnosis in primary care to biologics to surveillance colonoscopy. The BSG consensus guidelines, also 2019, involved systematic review of nearly 90,000 publications and produced 168 recommendations. PAUSE One thing both guidelines emphasise — the IBD multidisciplinary team. Gastroenterologist, IBD nurse, dietitian, surgeon, psychologist. And active assessment and treatment of anxiety and depression — which are highly prevalent in IBD."

The psychological component is underemphasised in practice — worth dwelling on briefly.

PAUSE That's the evidence base behind the surveillance colonoscopy programme — and it's a real reason to stay engaged with specialist follow-up if you have IBD."
