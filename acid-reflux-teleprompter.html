▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 2 — LOS anatomy diagram
Around one in four UK adults have regular heartburn. Most manage it with antacids from the chemist and never mention it to their GP. And yet persistent reflux is one of the most undertreated conditions I encountered in general practice — not because treatment doesn't work, but because people assume it's just something to put up with.
— pause —
Between the oesophagus — the gullet — and the stomach there's a muscular valve called the lower oesophageal sphincter. In normal digestion it opens for food, then closes tightly. In acid reflux it doesn't close properly — and stomach acid travels back up. The oesophageal lining isn't designed to handle acid. That inflammation is what you feel as heartburn — the burning behind the breastbone. When this happens frequently enough to cause symptoms or damage, it becomes GORD — Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease. Occasional heartburn is normal. GORD is when it becomes your normal.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 3 — Risk factor cards
Several things weaken that valve or increase pressure on the stomach. Excess weight is the biggest modifiable factor — abdominal pressure pushes contents upwards. A hiatus hernia physically disrupts the sphincter mechanism. Smoking weakens the valve and reduces saliva, which normally neutralises stray acid.
— pause —
Pregnancy — both hormonal relaxation of the sphincter and a growing uterus increasing abdominal pressure. Certain medications — NSAIDs like ibuprofen, calcium channel blockers, some antidepressants — can all worsen reflux. And meal timing matters: large meals, eating late, lying flat soon after eating all increase the opportunity for acid escape.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 4 — Barrett's pathway
For most people GORD is a nuisance. But I want to be direct about why it matters more than that.
— pause —
Repeated acid exposure inflames the oesophagus. Over time this can cause erosions, ulceration, and in some cases narrowing — making swallowing progressively difficult. More importantly: in around 10 to 15% of people with longstanding GORD, the normal oesophageal lining is replaced by a different cell type. This is called Barrett's oesophagus — a precancerous change. Each year approximately 0.33% of people with Barrett's develop oesophageal cancer — around 1 in 300 per year.
— pause —
That risk is low for any individual in any given year. But it compounds. And it's the reason that persistent, longstanding reflux — particularly in middle-aged and older men — warrants proper assessment. The NHS has surveillance programmes for Barrett's, and early detection works.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDE 5 — Stepped approach
GORD is usually diagnosed from the history — no test needed for a typical presentation. The approach is stepped. Lifestyle first. Weight loss is the most evidence-based lifestyle step. Late meals, large portions, and lying flat are all associated with increased reflux. A raised bed head reduces night-time symptoms.
— pause —
For medication: start with antacids and alginates — Gaviscon, Rennies — for mild symptoms. If those aren't enough, H2 receptor antagonists like famotidine. If those aren't sufficient: a proton pump inhibitor — a PPI. Omeprazole or lansoprazole. One important point about PPIs: 30 to 60 minutes before a meal — not at bedtime. The proton pump activates with eating. Wrong timing means reduced effect.
— pause —
Alarm features — difficulty swallowing, unexplained weight loss, vomiting blood — are clinically recognised as time-sensitive. 111 or urgent GP access exists for this.
▶ ADVANCE NOW — then read · SLIDES 6–7 — Research evidence cards
The evidence is worth pulling out. PPIs versus H2 receptor antagonists: a large Cochrane analysis — 134 trials, nearly 36,000 patients — confirmed PPIs produce significantly higher rates of healing and symptom control. They are the correct first-line pharmacological treatment for GORD.
— pause —
The LOTUS trial — over 500 patients followed for five years — found laparoscopic surgery and long-term PPI produced equivalent remission rates of around 85 to 90%. Surgery works as well as medication in appropriately selected patients. On weight loss: the Nurses' Health Study found even modest weight loss — around 3.5 kilograms — produced significant symptom reduction. This is evidence-based first-line treatment, not soft advice.
